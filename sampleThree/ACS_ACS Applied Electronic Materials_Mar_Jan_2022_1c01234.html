    Abstract

    Material composition plays a crucial role in the device performance; thus, nonvolatile memory devices from a small molecule named 5-mercapto-1-methyl tetrazole (MMT) in an insulating polymer matrix of poly(4-vinyl pyridine) (PVP) were fabricated. The composition of the active material in the device was varied to observe its influence on the device’s electronic properties. The device with a more or less weight ratio of MMT has a much smoother surface morphology, whereas when the contributions of MMT and PVP were equal, the average surface roughness increased. However, the maximum on–off current ratio for all the devices is 105, suggesting that the MMT molecule does not show any change in its characteristic properties when surrounded by an insulating material. When the device was fabricated without the polymer matrix, the surface morphology of the device completely changed as it was filled with large holes. These holes provide short-circuited pathways for the current by forming the direct metal contact between the top and bottom electrodes. The carrier transport through these devices follows various conduction mechanisms. Some of the dominating conduction mechanisms are direct tunneling and trap-free and trap-assisted space–charge-limited conduction.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The rapid increase in the amount of information has increased the need for information storage devices, which demands research and development on memory storage devices. Among all the types of memory devices, the resistive switching memory device has acquired great interest to become the next-generation data storage device. The simple architecture, flexibility, short response time (few nanoseconds), and ease of fabrication are the main reasons it is called the next-generation memory. (1) Resistive memory can be used in various advanced applications such as memristor devices, (2) neuromorphic computing, (3−5) optoelectronic logic operation, (6) optogenetic-inspired synaptic function, (7) and so forth. The memristors are the fourth fundamental circuit elements that can be reasoned by Strukov’s model. (8) The last 2 decades have seen much research on oxide material-based memory (oxM) devices. (9−11) Aluminum-, tantalum-, and hafnium-based oxides have been studied widely for resistive memory devices. (12−14) Though it possesses many advantages, oxM has many drawbacks, such as poor repeatability and nonuniformity of the resistance level. (15)
    Organic electronics is an emerging field that offers molecular structure-based electronics, optical properties, flexibility, and low-cost devices. (16−18) Different types of devices such as solar cells, sensors, and light-emitting diodes based on organic materials have been developed worldwide. (19−21) Organic resistive random access memory (O-RRAM) is one such device that has been showing excellent data storage capability, where the organic molecule is used as an active material between two electrodes. The organic material with a memory switching property switches from a high-resistance state (HRS) to a low-resistance state (LRS) at a particular applied voltage. The HRS can be denoted as the 0 state and the LRS as the 1 state. So, HRS-to-LRS switching is considered as 0 to 1 in binary conversion. Different types of organic materials have been used as the active material for RRAM devices, for example, natural polymers, organic conjugated polymers, metal–organic complexes, small molecules, (22−26) and so forth. Polymers with an azobenzene group are promising candidates for resistive switching. (27) Conformational changes and cis–trans isomerization with an applied electric field lead to a change in the conduction state. Also, the conjugation of azobenzene and charge trapping on azobenzene chromophore moieties lead to switching. (28,29) Polymers with a metal complex exhibit excellent resistive switching due to good redox reversibility and the electron-accepting metal complex. (30,31) The electron-accepting moieties in organometallic complexes also help the rapid change in electrical conductivity. (32) Small-molecule-based electronic devices can be useful because of the facile synthesis and purification of the molecule, high device stability, flexibility, and high electronic property reproducibility. (33,34) A small molecule with a conjugation may give rise to resistive switching. (29,30) Many investigations have been carried out to identify the actual reason for the resistive switching mechanism. People have also worked to improve the stability, reliability, and other performances by modifying the molecular structure, device fabrication method, area and thickness of the active layer, and metal contacts, but there is still scope for improvement in resistive memory devices.
    This work demonstrates the memristive application of a small organic molecule 5-mercapto-1-methyl tetrazole (MMT) with high stability and repeatability. Small organic molecules have a well-defined molecular structure, and these molecules can be synthesized, purified, and fabricated easily. (35) A small pentagonal MMT molecule has been used as an active material. Poly(4-vinyl pyridine) (PVP) has been used as a matrix polymer to bind the molecule and to achieve a smooth surface morphology. MMT is an excellent molecule having different tautomeric forms and rich chemical reactivity. (36) The same tautomeric effect may be seen if single-molecule devices are studied. Devices have been fabricated with varying percentages of the weight of the molecule, which gives a maximum on–off ratio of 105. The high stability, retention, and random-access behavior of the small molecule-based devices can be utilized to fabricate high-density, next-generation organic memristors as they obey the properties of a memristor.

    Experimental Section

    Device Fabrication

    Memristive devices have been fabricated on an indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass substrate, purchased from Techinstro, India. Standard processes have been followed to etch the glass substrates. (37) The substrate cleaning procedure is the same as that in our previously reported work. (38) The etched samples were dipped into the soap solution and cleaned with ultrasonication for 15 min, which were subsequently dipped into methanol, deionized water, and acetone one after another and ultrasonicated three times with each step for 15 min. After cleaning, substrates were kept in an oven for 24 h at 60 °C. MMT and PVP purchased from Sigma-Aldrich were used as active materials without further purification. The schematic of our prepared crossbar-structured device is shown in Figure 1. Four device compositions were chosen, which are MMT + PVP with weight ratios of 1:3 (D1), 1:1 (D2), and 2:1 (D3) and a pristine MMT-based device (D4). A 20 mg/mL MMT + PVP solution was prepared in ethyl alcohol and spin-coated at 1000 rpm with an acceleration of 500 rpm for 30 s to deposit a thin film. To get the top contact, an 85 nm thick aluminum layer was deposited using a thermal evaporation system.

    Characterizations

    For optical characterizations, the absorption spectrum of each composition was obtained using a Shimadzu UV1800 ultraviolet–visible (UV–vis) spectrophotometer. The surface morphology of the devices was studied using a Park Systems XE-70 atomic force microscope in the noncontact mode. An NCST-Au tip was used to carry out the scan at a speed of one line per second. The devices’ active layer thickness has been measured using a field emission scanning electron microscope (NNS-450). The current–voltage characteristics of all the devices were studied using a vacuum probe station and a Keithley 6430 femto ampere source meter.

    Computational Methodology

    The optical property of MMT and the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) have been studied using density functional theory (DFT) calculations with the ORCA simulation package. Time-dependent DFT (TD-DFT) was performed to understand the absorption peak of MMT. Geometry optimization and single-point energy calculations were performed using the B3LYP correlational functional and def2-SVP basis set, as implemented in ORCA software. (39)

    Optical Study

    The molecular structure of MMT and PVP and the UV–vis absorption spectra of pure MMT and its mixture with PVP in ethanol are shown in Figure 2a–c. The theoretically calculated HOMO, LUMO, and UV–vis spectra are shown in Figure 2d–f. The pristine molecule gives a sharp peak at 288 nm, whereas the absorption intensity is slightly less compared to that of the pristine one for the other compositions. PVP solution gives a broad shoulder around 285 nm. It has been observed that when the percentage of MMT is increased, the peak shifts from 285 nm toward 288 nm. There is no sign of charge transfer complex formation as no new peak has appeared in the mixture solutions.
    The theoretically calculated values of HOMO and LUMO energies were found to be −6.4 and −1.87 eV, respectively, which gives the HOMO–LUMO gap of 4.53 eV for the molecule. The frontier orbitals for the HOMO are contributed by the N═C–N and N═N bonds of the aromatic ring and S–H bonding.
    In contrast, for the LUMO, it is contributed by each atom separately in the aromatic ring. The conductor-like polarizable continuum model was used to account for the solvent effect for the absorption study. Ethanol was used as a solvent for the solutions. The peak is observed at 290 nm, which is nearly the same as the experimental value. TD-DFT suggests that the peak arises due to the electronic transition from the HOMO to LUMO.

    Surface Morphology Study

    The atomic force microscopy (AFM) image of a 5 × 5 μm2 area is shown in Figure 3. The same environment as that of the device was maintained to produce the thin film for the AFM scan. Figure 3a shows the topography of the device made with pure MMT molecules. The topography shows a rough surface with the highest roughness of 23 nm and an average roughness of 4 nm. It shows many big holes, marked by a green circle with an average diameter of 500 nm. Device D1 has a smoother surface with the highest roughness of 0.9 nm and an average roughness of 0.23 nm, as shown in Figure 3b. Many tiny holes are found on the surface, as shown by the red arrow in the green circle. The other devices (D2 and D3) also offer a smoother texture, and the average roughness values were 0.32 and 0.21 nm, respectively. It is found that the average roughness of surfaces oscillates with the weight ratio. These tiny holes can be responsible for migrating metallic ions forming metal filaments, which are also accountable for resistive switching.
    Figure 4a–c clearly shows three distinct layers on the top of the glass substrate. The thickness of ITO was found to be 180 nm, while the thicknesses of active layers are different for each device, which are 125, 136, and 117 nm for D1, D2, and D3, respectively. The deposited Al has almost the same thickness for all three devices, which is nearly 85 nm.

    Electrical Study

    For the pristine MMT-based device, a high current (short-circuit current) has been observed with the applied bias, which can be attributed to the formation of a direct aluminum channel through the huge holes on the surface. Thus, as expected, we did not get any resistive switching. The other three devices showed impressive results. The devices were studied by applying a range of voltages between ±2 V, and the corresponding changes in the currents were recorded. Figure 5a shows the I–V curve of device D1, where the blue arrows indicate the direction of the voltage trace. The current change with the applied voltage can be easily noticed from the figure. Below 1.23 V, the device was in a low conducting state (LCS) or an HRS, and above this, it changed its state to a high conducting state (HCS) or an LRS. This voltage where the resistance changes is called the SET voltage. In the HRS, the current is in the nanoampere range and changes linearly with the applied bias. The device stays in the LRS or HCS until 2 V and remains in this state when it is retraced back toward 0 V. In this state, the order of current is in the microampere range, which varies linearly and can be attributed to Ohmic conduction. At a particular negative bias called the RESET voltage, the device goes back to the HRS.
    Figure 5b shows the I–V curves for device D2. Device D2 does not show a similar behavior within ±2 V but rather at an applied voltage range of ±2.6 V; that is, voltage iteration cycles were initially increased from 0 to 2.6 V, then back-traced to −2.6 V, and finally changed to 0 V. With the increase in positive voltage, the device switches from the LCS to HCS at 1.56 V, and the current reaches a maximum of 19 μA at 2.6 V. While tracing back, the HCS is maintained until a sufficient negative bias is applied. Device D3 shows resistive switching during the iterative cycle of ±2 V, as shown in Figure 5c. Current increases linearly with applied voltage till 0.77 V, which further increases quadratically up to 1.37 V. The device then switches to the HCS, and the RESET state occurs at −1.56 V. The inset image in each figure shows the logarithmic current with applied bias. The different colored lines indicate the number of iterations. The 1st, 10th, 20th, 40th, and 50th cycles have been shown in these figures. In all the cycles, we can see the sharp switching from one resistance state to another.
    The ratio of HCS current and LCS current can be calculated from one complete cycle, giving the on–off ratio. As Figure 5d suggests, for D1, the maximum on–off ratio is 1.3 × 105 at −1.2 V. Similarly, for device D2, the maximum value is 1.3 × 105 at −1.8 V. Figure 5f represents the on–off ratio of device D3, and the maximum on–off ratio is 1.1 × 105 at −0.18 V. The on–off ratio of the devices is nearly 103 in the most stable region of the I–V curve.
    The maximum power consumption by the three devices is calculated by multiplying the highest switching current and voltage from the I–V curve. For device D1, the switching voltage is 1.24 V, and the highest current is 23.11 μA. Thus, the power requirement for this device is 28.66 μW, while the power consumption values of D2 and D3 are 29.81 and 25.067 μW, respectively.
    To check the devices’ stability, reliability, and data retention capability, we have studied the current of the LCS and HCS with time, as shown in Figure 6a–c, for devices D1, D2, and D3, respectively. The retention of the devices was checked at a particular bias of 0.3 V, which is called the read voltage. A write voltage pulse of 2 V for D1 and D3 and 2.6 V for D2 was applied for 6 s, which switched the device to the HCS, and a constant read voltage pulse of 0.3 V was applied to measure the read current. Similarly, for the LCS retention study, an erase voltage of −2 V for D1 and D3 and −2.6 V for D2 was applied and read at the same 0.3 V bias. For read, write, and erase voltage pulses, the pulse duration is 1 s. It has been noticed that these devices were showing two different states for a longer duration. Retention data were taken for more than 4 h, and very stable on and off states can be observed. All the devices showed an on–off current ratio of more than 103. This study indicates the ability of the devices to be used for read-only memory applications. The endurance of the devices was tested by running the voltage iteration for more than 250 cycles to check the stability of the device, as shown in Figure 6d–f. The devices show two stable resistance states.
    The random access memory (RAM) behavior has been studied by continuously applying erase–read–write–read bias pulses. At first, an erase voltage was applied for 6 s, and then, a read voltage bias for 6 s was used to check the conducting state of the device. After reading, a write voltage for 6 s was applied and again read by a small voltage to probe the conducting state. Figure 7a–c shows the RAM behavior of the devices. For devices D1 and D3, we applied 2, −2, and 0.3 V as write, erase, and read voltages, respectively. For D2, however, write, erase, and read voltages were 2.6, −2.6, and 0.3 V, respectively. Hundreds of such cycles were observed, and the devices showed very stable behavior.
    To understand the conduction mechanism, we have plotted the positive part of the I–V cycle in a logarithmic scale and fitted it, as shown in Figure 8a–c. For device D1, the current increases linearly (I ∝ V) with the applied bias up to 0.8 V and then increases quadratically (I ∝ V2) until applied voltage reaches 1.34 V. The device switches to the HCS at 1.35 V, and after that, the current varies linearly with bias. Similarly, for D2, the current varies linearly till 1 V, and beyond that, the current is proportional to the cube of the applied voltage till 2.35 V (I ∝ V3), switching to the HCS at 2.35 V. In the HCS, the current varies linearly with the applied voltage. D3 also has three different slopes, similar to device D1. First, its current changes linearly up to 0.76 V and then quadratically up to 1.4 V, and finally, the device switched to the HCS exponentially where the current varies linearly with voltage.     To understand the switching process, we have considered the energy diagram of PVP and MMT, as shown in Figure 9a. The HOMO and LUMO of PVP are −5.66 and −2.42 eV, respectively. At the same time, the HOMO and LUMO values for MMT are −6.4 and −1.87 eV, respectively. We have considered that the PVP matrix surrounds the molecule from all sides. At low voltage, thermally excited electrons can tunnel through the potential barrier and go to the Al contact from ITO. At moderate bias, more electrons can move through the potential barrier. At sufficiently high voltage, electrons transfer through a continuous path, which leads to the metal-like behavior of conduction called Ohmic conduction. In reverse bias, the reverse process occurs. At moderate bias, the continuous path starts breaking, and at higher negative bias, the path completely breaks, and the conduction occurs through the tunneling process. Along with this, metal ion penetration might also have contributed to the switching mechanism. From the AFM topography images, we can see the presence of holes and pinholes, through which ions can penetrate and make a continuous ionic filament at sufficiently high voltage. (37,42) At sufficiently high negative voltage, the filaments break, and the device switches to the LCS. The probability of filament formation is the maximum for the pristine device as there are big holes on the film surface. This is indeed evident as the pristine device has shown a short device behavior.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    MMT- and PVP-based organic memory devices were fabricated. Three different compositions for the two materials were considered for device fabrication. All three devices showed good switching behavior with the maximum on–off ratio of 105 from each device and an on–off ratio of 103 at the most stable region of the I–V curves. This suggests that the variation in the composition does not impact the on–off ratio. The conduction mechanism is mostly the same for all the devices. However, the conduction mechanism in one of the devices varied slightly for the LCS. The contribution due to the tautomeric changes of the molecule was not observed, rather the conduction in the devices was observed to be due to the direct tunneling, trap-free and with trap state SCLC, and Ohmic conduction. Holes and pinholes on the surface might also have contributed to the conduction mechanism as they provide pathways for forming metal filaments. This is indeed the case; the two devices having similar roughness show the same conduction mechanism, whereas the one with higher average roughness follows a different conduction mechanism.
