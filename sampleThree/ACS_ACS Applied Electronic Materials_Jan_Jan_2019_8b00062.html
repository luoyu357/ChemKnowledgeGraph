      Abstract

      We report a new Ba2Ga2GeO7:Bi3+ phosphor with broad emission almost covers the entire visible area generated from different bismuth emission centers in crystalline structure. The two peaks’ positions are invariably located at 497 and 611 nm which were independently excited by 365 and 387 nm excitation sources. As the emission tightly relies on the excitation source in an extremely wide range (260–470 nm), the emission color can be easily manipulated from cyan to red. The WLEDs devices with Ra = 90.40 and 91.98 were fabricated by employing Ba2Ga2GeO7:0.012Bi3+ as a red component phosphor. Through analyzing, we found P2 (Ga/Ge) sites tend to form vacancies in the sintering process. When bismuth concentration is set fixed, the luminescent intensity is quite dependent on the quantity of Ga or Ge vacancies which are beneficial to form Bi3+ emission centers and provide a fresh idea for structural micromodulation. Our results also provide new insights for understanding the roles of the vacancy played in phosphors.

      As phosphor-converted white-light-emitting diodes (pc-WLEDs) are considered to be a dominant light source and become the most favored way to generate white light in the current lighting market, (1,2) they have received extensive attention because of numerous advantages including luminous efficiency, environment-friendly characteristics, and low-energy consumption in comparison with conventional incandescent and fluorescent lamps. (3−6) These devices combined with a monochromatic LED chip generate white light from following two methods. (7) Therein, the most widely used approach is combining a blue-emitting (InGa)N LED with yellow-emitting phosphor Y3Al5O12:Ce3+, which is practical for the generation of a cheap, bright, and stable white-light source. (8) But the low color rendering index (CRI) as well as the high color correlated temperature (CCT) is accompanied by the generated light. Nevertheless, the spectral properties could be improved by introducing a long-wavelength emitting phosphor like Sr2Si5N8:Eu2+. (9) Beyond that, white light can also be realized by combining a UV or near-UV (NUV) LED with trichromatic phosphors. (8) A very high color tunability of the WLED can be received with this method. Thereby, to realize more sophisticated applications of pc-WLEDs, developing new phosphors with superior performance is crucial link. Numerous investigations have been implemented so far, and a great number of phosphors with excellent luminescent properties have been developed. Today, commercially available blue and green phosphors tend to be mature, but the current red phosphors are far from meeting performance requirements. (10) For Eu2+-activated phosphors like CaS:Eu2+ and CaAlSiN3:Eu2+ their excitation in the visible range inevitably leads to a reabsorption in the WLED device that may further decrease the device luminous efficiency. (11) For the phosphors doped with trivalent rare earths such as Eu3+, Sm3+, and Pr3+, their absorption region is remarkably narrow because of the forbidden transitions between shielded 4f electrons, which results in relative lower utilization efficiency of the LED chip emission. (11,12) Phosphors with red emission components and broad absorption band in the UV region are very desirable. (13) Finding new phosphor candidates is one solution to this problem although it is a big challenge.
      Recently, much attention is focused on main-group element bismuth cations; the exploitation of innovative Bi-doped phosphors for pc-wLEDs application is progressing. Bi3+, with [Xe]4f145d106s2 electronic configuration, whose 6s electrons are susceptive to the surrounding environment renders tunable emission. (14−16) The fluorescence properties of Bi3+ may be versatile in different hosts, and the photoluminescence can cover the full visible spectral range. For instance, the emission of LaOCl:Bi3+ located in the ultraviolet region, Ca3Al2O6:Bi3+ in green, K2MgGeO4:Bi3+ in orange, and ScVO4:Bi3+ in red. (13,17−19) For ScVO4:Bi3+, which was investigated by Peng et al., they suggested that the red emission stems from the perturbed Bi3+ luminescence center caused by oxygen vacancies. (15) In this case, Bi3+ luminescence centers are likely to depend on available oxygen vacancies contents, which further influence the photoluminescence of Bi3+, although this hypothesis was not yet provided. (15) Besides, for Y2SiO5: Bi3+, three discernible peaks exist in the emission spectra, of which two peaks are attributed to the occupation of Bi3+ ions for the two types of Y sites and one peak corresponds to the Bi3+ emission center bound to an oxygen vacancy. Its emission can be tuned from blue to green because of the energy transfer among the emission centers. (10) For ZnWO4:Bi3+, different charge states of multiple defects may be generated in the phosphor (like BiZn, BiW, interstitial Bi or 2BiZnVW, etc.). (11) Bi3+ is focused on by an increasing number of researchers because of its particularity of electronic configuration, and diverse luminescence properties could be realized by artificial design at the present. That is also the reason we chose Bi3+ as the activator.
      As is well-known, phosphor host plays a significant role in the luminescence process. Germanate, which possesses various crystal structures, has been proved to be good candidate for a phosphor host, some of which exhibits excellent luminescent properties when doped with luminescent ions. (16,20−22) In this work, aiming to better investigate Bi3+ emission, we selected the recently reported green germanate phosphor (23) and reported its unusual luminescent properties unexplored.

      Materials and Synthesis

      Ba2–xGa2GeO7:xBi3+ (BGGO:xBi3+, x = 0–0.05) were synthesized using high-temperature solid-state reaction. Stoichiometric amounts of the starting materials BaCO3 (99.99%), Ga2O3 (99.99%), GeO2 (99.99%), and Bi2O3 (99.99%) were accurately weighed and ground thoroughly in an agate mortar. After 30 min grinding, the mixture was transferred into an alumina crucible for sintering at 1150 °C for 10 h under air atmosphere conditions. The samples were ground into powders for further measurement after cooling. Therein, individual batches of starting materials were weighed stoichiometrically to BayGa2GeO7:1.2 mol % Bi (y = 1.90, 1.96, 2.04, 2.1), Ba1.988GazGeO7:1.2 mol % Bi (z = 1.90, 1.96, 2.04, 2.1), and Ba1.988Ga2GevO7:1.2 mol % Bi (v = 0.95, 0.98, 1.02, 1.05) ground, mixed, and sintered at 1150 °C for 10 h under air atmosphere. For example, the samples of y = 1.90, z = 1.96, and v = 0.95 are labeled as Ba1.90, Ga1.96, and Ge0.95.

      Materials Characterization

      The phase purity and crystal structure of samples were identified by an X-ray diffractometer (XRD) using a Bruker-D8 Focus diffractometer at 40 kV and 40 mA and graphite-monochromated Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.15405 nm). The General Structure Analysis System (GSAS) package was adopted in Rietveld structure refinements to determine the change in crystal structure. The fluorescence decay curves were recorded with a homemade instrument mainly consisting of a Lecroy Wave Runner 6100 digital oscilloscope (1 GHz) and a 928 PMT using a tunable laser (pulse width = 4 ns, gate = 50 ns) as the excitation source. The diffuse reflectance spectra were performed using a 3600 UV–vis–NIR spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan) with reference of barium sulfate. The elementary composition of the sample was recorded by an inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometer (ICP-OES, Thermo Scientific X Series 2). The photoluminescence excitation (PLE) spectra and the photoluminescence (PL) spectra within the temperature range 273–423 K were recorded by a high-resolution FLS 920 spectrofluorometer (Edinburgh Instruments) coupled with a temperature controller. The morphology and element mappings of the sample were observed with a scanning electron microscope (SEM, S-4800, Hitachi). The valence state of the bismuth ion was analyzed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo) using a thermo ESCALAB 250 system with a focused monochromatic Al Kα X-ray beam. The PL spectra and the parameters of the fabricated WLEDs devices were collected using a Rainbow-Light portable light meter MR-16 v3 (Rainbow Light Technology CO. Ltd.) with an integrating sphere.

      BGGO, with melilite structure type, formed by polyhedral layers along the c tetragonal axis alternately, possesses a crystalline structure similar as Ba2Fe2GeO7 and was first reported by Ismatov in 1978. (24) It comprises three types of coordination polyhedral, in which the dodecahedral site was occupied by Ba2+ and the tetrahedral site is taken by Ga3+ and Ge4+. According to the report of Petrakovskii, (25) the Ga ions in BGGO act the Fe part in Ba2Fe2GeO7. The unit cell parameters of BGGO viewed in direction of the c-axis is illustrated in Figure 2a. P1 sites are predominantly occupied by Ga and further formed regular tetrahedra with surrounding oxygen atoms (with symmetry of 4̅). P2 sites are occupied by Ge and Ga approximately in a 1:1 ratio and formed distorted tetrahedra with surrounding oxygen atoms (with mm2 symmetry). Remarkably, the P1 tetrahedra is more regular than the P2 tetrahedra. (25) The coordination environments of the Ba2+, P1, and P2 sites in BGGO and BGGO:0.012Bi3+ are exhibited Figures 2b and 2c. The average bond distance of Ba–O decreases as the Bi3+ content (<1.2 mol %) increases because the larger cationic radius of Ba2+ (r = 1.42 Å, CN = 8) was substituted by the smaller Bi3+ (r = 1.17 Å, CN = 8), (26) which leads to the shrinkage of BaO8 polyhedral volume. Moreover, considering the relatively small differences of the radii between Ba2+ and Bi3+, we propose that Bi3+ cations prefer to substitute for Ba2+.       Spectroscopic Study of Bi3+-Doped BGGO

      The diffuse reflection spectra of BGGO:xBi3+ (x = 0, 0.012, 0.05) are presented in Figure 3a. There is an obvious absorption band in the region of 200–300 nm, and it extends with the Bi3+ content increasing. The absorption enhancement after 300 nm originates from the Bi3+ transition of 1S0 to 3P1. The inset of Figure 3a shows the absorption spectrum of BGGO host calculated by the Kubelka–Munk equation. The band gap Eg could be calculated as the intersection value of a tangent line with a photon energy axis. Therefore, Eg value of BGGO is calculated to be 4.89 eV.
      Figure 3b illustrates the spectra with the excitation wavelength changing from 290 to 405 nm. The emission spectra exhibit strong excitation-dependent behavior and an independence of the emission positions at 497 and 611 nm as the two emission positions barely move with changing the excitation wavelength. Considering the luminescent properties and the bismuth valence identified by the XPS analysis (see Figure S4), we attribute the dual emission peaks to Bi3+ emission centers. The intensity ratio of the two peaks (IBi1/IBi2) approximates to 1 when the excitation wavelength is 290 nm. As the excitation wavelength enlarges from 290 to 330 nm, both the intensities of the two emission peaks augment and the ratio increases to 4.47; when the excitation wavelength further increases, the 497 nm peak intensity continues to increase and reaches a maximum at 365 nm, while the ratio decreases inversely (see the inset of Figure 3b). When the excitation wavelength exceeds 365 nm, the intensity corresponding to Bi1 (497 nm) emission peak begins to decrease, and the ratio also decreases sharply. With 387 nm excitation, the emission with two subordinate emissions at 497 and 611 nm can be observed. The extraordinary broad band almost covers the entire visible area. As the excitation wavelength moves from 387 to 405 nm, the Bi2 becomes the dominant emission center. When the excitation wavelength is 410 nm, the ratio reaches to 0.14, the minimum in this study. The blue shift of the emission peak can be observed, which starts from the Bi1 emission becoming more effective compared to Bi2 when the excitation wavelength changes from 290 to 330 nm (see Figure S5a). The position as well as the shape of the emission spectra is barely changed, and the emission is almost completely dominated by the Bi1 center as the excitation wavelength continues to increase from 320 to 350 nm. When the excitation wavelength changes from 360 to 425 nm, the Bi2 emission peak dominates the advantages and Bi1 peak weakens gradually (see Figure S5b).
      The Commission International de l’Eclairage (CIE) chromaticity coordinates corresponding to different wavelengths were obtained using the emission spectra data and are listed in Table S3. The x and y values are virtually both declined first and then increased along with λex. Thus, the emission color can be altered from cyan to red based on excitation light modulation. The marked CIE chromaticity coordinates of the phosphors together with photographs recorded upon 395 and 365 nm irradiation are also illustrated in Figure 3c. Such broadly color-tunable luminescence in single doped phosphor based on excitation light modulation is extremely rare in phosphor families.
      The excitation spectra (λem = 497 and 611 nm) of BGGO:0.012Bi3+ together with the diffuse reflection curve of BGGO host are plotted in Figure 3d. The excitation spectra mainly originate from the spin-allowed 1S0 → 3P1 transition of Bi3+. The spectra exhibit two partly overlapping bands in the range 260–470 nm, which is very unusual for Bi3+-doped phosphors since there are very few visible light excited Bi3+-doped phosphors that have been reported as far as we know. The shapes of the two spectra are indeed very different due to the existence of two Bi3+ emission centers. For the emission of 497 nm, the excitation spectrum assigned to the Bi1 center is an asymmetrical peak, and the maximum locates at 365 nm. For the 611 nm emission which is attributed to the Bi2 center, the strongest excitation peak is detected at about 387 nm along with a shoulder peak at 300 nm. Although UV–vis diffuse reflectance spectra show a broad absorption band in the range 200–320 nm, there is no luminescence in the blank BGGO sample under the exciting light of 200–410 nm. Between the two emission centers, the Bi1 center is the dominant one in the range 260–387 nm, as shown in Figure 3d, which is in well agreement with the emission spectra as the ratios of IBi1/IBi2 are greater than 1 in this range (see in the inset of Figure 3b). By contrast, the spectral region of Bi2 excitation is much broader than Bi1, and only the Bi2 center can be excited in the range 420–470 nm. The intersection of the two excitation curves is about 387 nm, which is exactly the maximum of the Bi2 center excitation.
      For Bi3+, 1S0 with the 6s2 electronic configuration is the ground state, and the 6s6p configurations involving splitting 3P0, 3P1, 3P2, and 1P1 are the excited states. Transitions from 1S0 to 3P1 or 1P1 are parity-allowed and have relatively higher absorption strength because of spin–orbit coupling. The transition of 1S0 to 1P1 is usually excluded by the researchers because its absorption lies in the far-UV region. Additionally, transitions from ground state 1S0 to excited state 3P0 or 3P2 are generally spin-forbidden. (13,17) Accordingly, we suggest that as shown in the schematic diagram in the inset of Figure 3d, under the excitation light, the electrons in the 1S0 level can be excited to the 3P1 level by absorbing the energy from the excitation light directly. Afterward, these electrons will flee back to ground state 1S0 accompanied by emission or relax to the bottom level 1S0 by a nonradiative way.
      In the analysis above, we concluded that Ba2+ is the only cation that Bi3+ can occupy in the crystal structure. And there is only one type of Ba2+ site; however, the emission spectra indicate that there are two types of Bi3+ emission centers in the structure. Because Bi3+ is sensitive to the surrounding environment, we proposed that Bi2 emission peak originates from Ba2+ site occupied by Bi3+ ion which is close to P2 vacancies as presented in Figure 4a. The reasons are as follows. To confirm the existence of gallium and germanium vacancies, we employed ICP-OES measurements. (30−32) The results are listed in Table S4 and indicate the gallium and germanium vacancies are abundant in the phosphors.

      Structural Micromodulation on Bi3+-Doped BGGO

      To explore how vacancies affect luminescence, we designed the contrast experiments. Generally, under the same synthesis conditions, on the basis of the stoichiometric ratio of BGGO:0.012Bi3+, the less Ga or Ge will help to trigger more P2 vacancies. We synthesized individual batches of samples as exhibited in Figure 4b, and the XRD patterns of the representative samples are displayed in Figure S6. The peaks of most samples agreed well with the of BGGO standard data which shows that the samples are single phase. But for Ge0.95, a subtle impure peak at about 28.1° was detected, which stems from BaGa2O4 (PDF card no. 46-0415). The XRD Rietveld refinement analysis was also performed on some individual samples. The refined structural parameters, selected bond distances, and refinement patterns are presented in Table S2, Table S1, and Figure S3, respectively. By analyzing the emission spectra of the samples, we found that the samples of Ge0.95 (a, i), Ge0.98 (b, j), Ga1.90 (e, m), and Ga1.96 (f, n) possess high performance compared to the others. Therein, the luminescent performance can be enhanced by decreasing of the content of Ga and Ge. What needs to be pointed out is that the charge imbalance makes it difficult for Bi3+ to replace Ba2+, but the excess positive charge of this replacement can be compensated by the negative charge of Ga and Ge vacancies. In this case, more Bi3+ tends to enter into the lattice and substitute for Ba2+, and both Bi1 and Bi2 emissions are improved accordingly. Compared to Bi1, the Bi2 emission center, which is closer to P2 vacancy, is more affected by the vacancy as shown in the normalized emission spectra. Upon 387 nm excitation, the normalized emission intensity of Bi2 increases along with the reduction of Ga or Ge, which is evidence that the generation of Bi2 is related to the vacancy, as displayed in Figure 5. The actual PL spectra are also shown in Figure S7. In addition, the crystal structure analysis of the phosphor also indicates that the vacancy of P2 affects the emission center rather than P1.
      The crystal model of BGGO:0.012Bi3+ in view of the direction of c-axis is represented in Figure 6. Therein, the crystal network structure formed by P2 (Ga/Ge) around Ba is illustrated in Figure 6a. Except for oxygen sites, the closest sites to Ba are P2 sites. There are six P2 sites which form the trigonal prism structure in the surrounding of Ba sites as displayed in the inset of Figure 6a. The Ba site locates in the center and O sites in the edge of the trigonal prism. The shortest bond distance of Ba to P2 is 3.4536 Å, and the average bond distance is 3.7328 Å (see Table S5). However, as illustrated in the crystal network structure formed by P1 of Figure 6b, the distances between P1 and Ba is 4.2154 or 4.1475 Å, which are much longer than the distances between P2 and Ba site. What is more, the tetrahedron of P1 is more regular than P2, indicating that P2 tends to form a vacancy more easily than P1. The results of the contrast experiments also demonstrate that the reduction of Ga3+ and Ge4+ plays the same part in luminescence enhancement as both Ga3+ and Ge4+ can occupy this P2 position.

      Decay Lifetime and Thermal Stability Analysis

      Considering the great influence of the Bi3+ content on emission intensity in previous luminescent multicenters of Bi3+ studies, (10,27,33) the influence of Bi3+ content in the luminescence properties of BGGO is investigated by varying the Bi3+ content between 0.0005 and 0.05. Figure 7 exhibits the emission spectra of the BGGO samples with different Bi3+ concentrations (x = 0.0005, 0.001, 0.003, 0.005, 0.008, 0.01, 0.012, 0.015, 0.02, 0.025, 0.03, and 0.05) under 365 nm UV lamp irradiation. With the increasing of Bi3+ concentration, a dominating emission band centered at 497 nm with a broad bandwidth always exists. The emission intensities enhances along with increasing Bi3+ content until x = 0.012. Afterward, the emission intensity declines dramatically, which is because of the concentration quenching of Bi3+.       In summary, we synthesized a novel and promising Bi3+-based phosphor and investigated the photoluminescence properties systematically. There are two distinct types of Bi3+ emission centers in the phosphor. The emission color can be altered from cyan to red based on excitation light modulation. The two broad bands originate from the 3P1 → 1S0 transition of two types of bismuth (Bi1 and Bi2) in the phosphor. The Bi1 and Bi2 emission centers both stem from the Ba2+ sites substituted by Bi3+, and the luminescent intensity is quite dependent on the quantity of Ga and Ge vacancies which are beneficial to form emission centers. The thermal stability was also explored. The findings indicate that the BGGO:Bi3+ is a promising phosphor for applications in WLEDs.
