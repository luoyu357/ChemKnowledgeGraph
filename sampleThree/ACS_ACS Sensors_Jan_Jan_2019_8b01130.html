      Abstract

      A distance-based analysis of potassium ion (K+) is introduced that is performed on a microfluidic paper-based analytical device (μPAD) coupled to an ion-selective capillary sensor. The concept is based on two sequential steps, the selective replacement of analyte ion with an ionic dye, and the detection of this dye in a distance-based readout on paper. To achieve the first step, the capillary sensor holds a poly(vinyl chloride) (PVC) membrane film layer plasticized by dioctyl sebacate (DOS) that contains the potassium ionophore valinomycin, a lipophilic cation-exchanger and the ionic indicator Thioflavin T (ThT) on its inner wall. Upon introduction of the sample, K+ in the aqueous sample solution is quantitatively extracted into the film membrane and replaced with ThT. To convert the ion exchange signal into a distance-based analysis, this solution was dropped onto the inlet area of a μPAD to flow the ThT along a channel defined by wax printing, resulting in the electrostatic binding of ThT to the cellulose carboxylic groups. The initial amount of K+ determines the amount of ThT in the aqueous solution after ion-exchange, and consequently the distance of ThT-colored area reflects the sample K+ concentration. The ion exchange reaction was operated in a so-called “exhaustive sensing mode” and gave a distinct response in a narrow range of K+ concentration (1–6 mM) that cannot be achieved by the classical optode sensing mode. The absence of hydrogen ions from the equilibrium competition of the capillary sensor contributed to a complete pH-independence, unlike conventional optodes that contain a pH sensitive indicator. A very high selectivity for K+ over Na+ and Ca2+ has been confirmed in separate solutions and mixed solutions tests. K+ measurements in pooled serum samples at concentrations between 2 and 6 mM are successfully demonstrated on a temperature controlled support.

      Metal ions play an important role in biological function and the body maintains a constant, optimal metal ion level to achieve normal homeostasis. Inappropriate concentrations indicate a health risk, and for this reason the measurement of metal ion levels in physiological samples is one of the most important criteria in clinical diagnostics. For potassium, the normal range for K+ in serum is approximately 2–6 mM, with a normal homeostasis range of 3.5–5 mM, while hypokalemia and hyperkalemia are indicated for 2.0–3.5 mM and 5–6 mM, respectively. (1) Clinical potassium concentration is today routinely measured with ion-selective electrodes on the basis of ionophore-based membranes. (2−5) While this technique is reliable and well established, it is not an instrument-free technique and therefore not ideally suited for resource-poor settings.
      Microfluidic paper-based analytical devices (μPADs) have been introduced in 2007 as simpler variants of lab-on-a-chip (LOC) devices. They are attractive because they can handle limited small sample volumes, are cost-effective, and may be disposed of by incineration. (6,7) μPADs may aim at point-of-care (POC) testing for the general population or medical institutions in resource-scarce countries. For this application, colorimetric detection is one of the most promising readout principles for μPADs. However, conventional colorimetric μPADs have suffered from a poor ability to meet World Health Organization (WHO) requirements and the associated feasibility for commercialization. (8,9) To better address this, equipment-free μPADs that do not require external equipment to analyze the data for quantitation have been recently put forward. They allow for a readout signal on the basis of counting, (10−12) text, (13−16) time, (17−19) and distance. (20,21) Since those devices are able to complete the entire analytical process by themselves, they may currently be among the most promising clinical devices for use in resource-poor settings.
      μPADs have already been applied to the measurement of a range of metal ions by colorimetric readout. (22−24) However, the quantification of clinically relevant ions including K+, Na+, Ca2+, and Cl– on μPADs has been challenging due to the scarcity of chromogenic reagents that are sufficiently selective and reactive for these ions. While metal chelating fluorophores have been used for the simultaneous Na+, K+, Ca2+, and H+ quantification on μPADs, (25) fluorometry itself is considered inferior to colorimetry on μPADs because of the need of a controlled excitation light source. More recently, neutral carrier-based ion-selective optodes (ISOs), (26,27) which can detect ion concentration based on a chemically selective two-phase ion exchange reaction, have been applied to μPADs for the detection of Na+, (28,29) K+, (30) and the anionic species Cl–, F–, and polyanions. (31) Thus, the application of optode principles to μPADs aims to achieve point-of-care testing of biologically important ionic species, especially alkali metal ions. Until now, the need for external equipment is still indispensable for colorimetric quantification and reduces the practicability of those devices.
      Henry and co-workers have recently reported on a distance-based analysis of potassium ions on μPADs created with conventional ion-selective optode particles composed of a lipophilic pH indicator (chromoionophore I), a lipophilic cation-exchanger salt (NaTFPB), and the established potassium ionophore valinomycin that were deposited on a defined channel of the paper substrate. (32) The ISO response to the primary ion is based on an ion-exchange reaction where potassium ions are extracted into the sensing phase and in turn expel hydrogen ions into the sample, thereby deprotonating the indicator and resulting in the desired color change. The authors obtained a heterogeneous colorimetric pattern within the channel that allowed for a distance-based detection by using an optimal amount of sensing components to deplete most potassium ions from the sample as it flows down the channel. Despite this success, some problems remain. The ion-exchange process remains inherently sensitive to sample pH, which may introduce errors, and the potassium uptake kinetics must be significantly faster than the flow rate within the channel while offering high uptake capacity for best performance, which is very difficult to achieve. The device put forward is not yet sufficiently exhaustive for routine use.
      Solvatochromic dye-based ion-selective optodes may be one of the best candidates to overcome the problems mentioned above. Monodispersed particulate optodes containing Pluronic F-127 surfactant as matrix and solvatochromic dye as indicator have shown high pH-independence by removing the hydrogen ion from the equilibrium ion exchange competition. Instead, the solvatochromic dye indicators themselves are displaced by the target primary ions and result in an optical readout because of the drastic change in solvent environment. (33,34) This type of ionic indicator is required to exhibit appropriate lipophilicity to be liberated from the organic optode phase at the desired ion concentration. The partitioning of solvatochromic dye from reactive microemulsions has been documented in heterogeneous titrations (33) and is a very promising approach to the design of optical ion assays that are independent of solution pH.
      This paper introduces a novel distance-based, instrument-free ion detection principle where the above-mentioned challenges may be overcome. This is achieved by separating the recognition and detection steps as follows. The recognition step involves the use of a K+-selective capillary film sensor, the principle of which is analogous to a classical capillary optode, (35) that contains the ionic dye Thioflavin T (ThT) of moderate and optimal lipophilicity as indicator (33,36) instead of an established lipophilic H+-chromoionophore. (32) A selective and exhaustive ion exchange reaction is performed by the capillary sensor with a defined volume of aspirated K+ sample solution and gives a solution where the initial concentration of potassium is quantitatively replaced by the same concentration of ThT dye. This modified sample solution is subsequently applied on a μPAD where the ionic dye passes through the channel and is allowed to bind electrostatically to the anionic sites of cellulose. (37) The distance of the colored channel area now depends on the amount of dye and hence reflects the initial amount of K+ in the sample. No H+ is involved in the ion exchange equilibrium, unlike classical pH indicator-based optodes, (26,28−31) and the readout signal can be expected to be independent of solution pH. (33,36) As demonstrated earlier by Wolfbeis (35) a coated capillary is suitable for fabricating thin ion-selective films, which is used to realize a rapid, reproducible, and exhaustive response to the target ion. The exhaustive sensing mode can give a linear response to ion concentration and is especially suited to detect narrow concentration ranges as required in clinical diagnostics. (38,39)

      Materials and Instruments

High molecular weight poly(vinyl chloride) (PVC), poly(acrylic acid) (PAA; average molecular weight 1800 g mol−1), tetramethylammonium hydroxide pentahydrate (TMAOH), thioflavin T, Kimble microcapillary pipettes 10 μL (length: 5 in., material: soda lime glass, manufacturer part No: 71900–10) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, USA). N-(2-Hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid (HEPES) and sodium tetrakis-[3,5-bis(trifluoromethyl)phenyl]borate (NaTFPB) were sourced from Dojindo Laboratories (Kumamoto, Japan). For classical sensing mode only, valinomycin, THF, DOS, and NaTFPB were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, USA) and thioflavin T was from Acros Organics, Thermo Fisher Scientific (Massachusetts, USA). 20 μL micropipette and corresponding micropipette tips were from GILSON. 9 mL screw vials were purchased from AS ONE Corporation. Ultrapure water (>18 MΩ cm) was obtained from a PURELAB flex water purification system (ELGA, Veolia Water, Marlow, U.K.). Advantec No. 5C filter paper was from Toyo Roshi (Tokyo, Japan). Hot lamination films (75 and 100 μm thickness, film material: polyethylene terephthalate and poly(vinyl alcohol) as a thermoplastic adhesive) were obtained from Jointex (Tokyo, Japan). An HM-30R pH meter (DKK-TOA Co., Ltd.) was used to measure the pH in preparing HEPES-TMAOH buffer stock solutions. A ColorQube 8570 printer (Xerox, Norwalk, CT) was used to pattern wax on the filter paper. Film lamination was performed on a QHE325 hot laminator (Meikoshokai, Tokyo, Japan). A Canoscan 9000F Mark II (Canon, Tokyo Japan) was used to obtain image data of μPADs. In the serum sample measurements, temperature control of channels was performed by a MiniT-1 model of Biological Indicator Incubator (Serial No.: AS-MInIT-1122E, Hang Zhou Allsheng Instruments Co., Ltd.). Absorbance spectral data were obtained by a UV/vis Spectrophotometer SPECTRO 250 PLUS (Analyticjena, Jena, Germany).

      Fabrication of Capillary Sensor

      16 mg of PVC, 32 mg of DOS were dissolved in 3 mL of THF. 0.40 mL of this mixture was used to dissolve 2.66 mg of valinomycin (4.96 mmol/kg), 1.68 mg of NaTFPB (3.92 mmol/kg), and 0.54 mg of thioflavin T (3.5 mmol/kg) to obtain a near-exhaustive membrane response in 1–6 mM K+ sample. A 10 μL pipet tip connected to a 10 μL micropipettor was cut to size in order to squeeze a 10 μL capillary into it. This was used to aspirate 10 μL of the membrane into the capillary by the micro pipettor. After 5 min of filling the capillary with the membrane cocktail it was heated with a hair dryer to evaporate THF and to form a thin ion-selective membrane film layer inside the capillary.

      Fabrication of μPADs

      An A4 size of Advantec No. 5C filter paper was fed into a Xerox wax printer to pattern channels on the paper surface designed by Adobe illustrator CC software. On this occasion, channels designed with scales and numbers were printed on the upper side of the paper and pristine channels on the other side. The wax-patterned paper was covered with a baking sheet and heated up twice with a laminating machine in the operation mode “250 μm blue” to permeate the hydrophobic wax through the paper thickness and define hydrophobic channels. Subsequently, the paper of which the upper side was covered by the baking sheet was inserted between laminate films and fed into a laminating machine in the operation mode “100 μm red”. The upper side of the laminate film was removed from the bottom side containing the paper-based channel. In the case of serum sample detection, the laminated paper channel was soaked into an 890 mg/mL PAA solution for 10 min. After removing the paper from the PAA solution tray container, excess aqueous solution was wiped off and dried with a hair dryer for approximately 1 min. A laminate film of 75 μm thickness was cut to cover only the channels of the laminated paper, and laminated in the operation mode “100 μm red”.

      Standard and Serum Sample Preparation

      KCl, NaCl, or CaCl2 were dissolved into deionized water to prepare K+, Na+, and Ca2+ standard solutions.
      For the pH independence test, 500 mL of 40 mM HEPES was prepared to which 1 M of TMAOH was added. Once the solution pH reached the desired pH (7, 7.4, 8, or 9), the solution was diluted with water to 1 L (giving a final concentration of 20 mM HEPES-TMAOH). Equal volumes of this solution and 5 mM KCl aqueous solution were mixed.
      In the serum sample preparation, 2.4 mg of PVC, 4.8 mg of DOS, 2.664 mg of valinomycin, and 2.00 mg of NaTFPB were dissolved into 1 mL of THF. Half of the membrane cocktail was placed into an 8 mL vial and was dried by a hair drier while the vial was tilted and rotated. 1 mL of serum sample was cast in the vial where ion-selective membrane was fixed and gently shaken by hand. Upon contact of the serum sample with the ion-selective membrane, K+ extracted into the membrane as it exchanged with Na+, the initial counterion of TFPB–. In this manner a reduction of K+ concentration was possible. The ion concentration was potentiometrically tracked by a HORIBA K+ meter. Each desired concentration of serum was prepared by the same procedure.

      Fabrication of Ion-Selective Membrane and Measurement for Classical Sensing Mode Evaluation

      1.25 mg of PVC, 2.50 mg of DOS, 4.96 mmol/kg of valinomycin, 3.92 mmol/kg of NaTFPB, and 3.50 mmol/kg of ThT were dissolved in THF. An 80 μL aliquot of the cocktail was cast into a 5 mL vial, and THF was evaporated using a hair drier while tilting and rotating the vial to affix the resulting membrane on the inner lateral side wall of the vial. Consequently, 1 mL of K+ standard samples was added into the vial containing the ion-selective membrane film, and in turn, the vials were set in a vortex machine and shaken at a strength of “4”. After 2 h of vortexing, 0.3 mL of sample solution was transferred into a plastic cuvette and diluted with 0.6 mL of deionized water for absorbance measurement.

      Detection Method

      10 μL of sample solution was aspirated into the capillary and incubated for 5 min. For absorbance measurements, the incubated solution was pipetted into a plastic cuvette of an absorbance spectrophotometer and mixed with 0.9 mL of deionized water to measure the absorbance spectra of thioflavin T. In the case of distance-based analysis on the paper substrate, the incubated sample solution was dropped onto the inlet area of the μPADs. About 15 min later, when channels were dried, an image data of those μPADs was taken and analyzed by ImageJ software. In the case of serum sample detection, the incubated serum sample in the capillary sensor was dispensed onto the μPADs containing a laminated channel and placed onto a temperature control device (Minit-1 Biological Indicator Incubator), and left for about 30 min until serum flow stopped. The image data was taken and analyzed by the same procedure.

      Basic Concept and Theory of the Assay

      Plasticized PVC-based ion-selective membrane films were solvent cast onto the inner wall of a glass capillary and contained the cationic thioflavin T indicator, a lipophilic cation exchanger, and the potassium ionophore valinomycin. The capillary accepts just 10 μL of aspirated sample and ion exchange can be carried out inside the capillary (Scheme 1). The ThT expelled from the membrane matrix into the aqueous solution is then, in a second step, moved by capillary force through a paper channel to achieve a distance-based detection. While making contact with the paper substrate during flow, the dye will absorb onto the surface of the cellulose fibers by electrostatic interaction with negatively charged functional groups, mainly carboxylates, of cellulose. The presence of carboxylic groups on cellulose fibers is well established and known to interact with positively charged solutes. (37,40) The travel distance of those dyes on the paper substrate depends on the density of anionic sites on the paper, reaction rate of those with ThT, flow rate of the solution, and the amount of ThT. The acquired distance will reflect chiefly the amount of ThT, which is in turn equivalent to the initial amount of K+ in the sample.
      For an ionic dye to achieve this sensing mechanism, it must have optimal balance of hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity. An excessive hydrophilicity may cause nonselective ion exchange and severe leakage with background electrolyte, even in the absence of the target primary ion. Conversely, a dye with too much hydrophobicity would more favorably remain in the ion-selective membrane film, resulting in a response range at impractically high concentration of target ion. The ionic dye must therefore be chosen with a correct balance to be retained in the film in the absence of K+ and exchanged only with primary ions in the desired sensing range.
      The distance-based mechanism on the paper substrate is analogous to that of paper chromatography, where paper substrate and sample solution can be treated as stationary and mobile phase, respectively. To obtain a perceivable and distinct distance-based signal, the ionic dye must exhibit an optimal balance of hydrophilicity and hydrophobicity for the readout step as well for it to be adsorbed on the cellulose fiber. As shown in Figure S1, the ionic dye ThT gave the desired distance in flow tests on paper substrate compared to other compounds.
      Thioflavin T (ThT) was chosen as one of the best prospects owing to its high hydrophilicity and sufficient lipophilicity to be retained in the film in the absence of K+ so that it can be exchanged only with potassium ions in the sensing range of interest (1–6 mM), and compatibility with a direct paper readout.
      The first step of the detection scheme involves the quantitative replacement of potassium in the sample with the ionic dye. The ion-selective membrane was calculated to exhibit a distinctive response for a 1–6 mM K+ sample concentration range, which is more narrow and sensitive than the well-known classical sensing mode. According to the calculation, the molar amounts of valinomycin, NaTFPB, and ThT are capable to quantitatively extract/exchange K+ from a sample of up to 4.2 mM concentration while the experimental responses showed a sufficiently distinctive result for samples containing 1–6 mM K+ (Figure 1). Concentrations above 4.2 mM can still be detected, albeit no longer exhaustively. This particular ion-selective membrane composition was mainly chosen because of the limited travel distance of ThT on the paper substrate, which will be discussed later.

      Distance-Based Analysis on Paper Substrate

      The capillary sensor was confirmed to show a more sensitive and linear response than the classical mode that was well described by theory. Consequently, the ThT-modified sample solutions were applied to μPADs for detection. Figure 2 shows the travel distance of ThT on the paper substrate as analyzed by imageJ. Please refer to Figure S3 for the corresponding images of the μPADs.
      Compared to previously reported work on distance-based μPADs created with ion-selective optodes, (32) the response to K+ from 1 to 6 mM on this μPADs system is more sensitive in terms of distance traveled and distance ratio. This was made possible by replacing the analyte ion by an easily adsorbing dye in the capillary sensor compartment.

      pH Independence

      As mentioned above, conventional ion optodes suffer from significant pH cross-response. Previous work on distance-based K+ analysis suffered to some extent (32) although aqueous samples in the real world exhibit a range of pH values. Serum pH can also vary slightly. (41) The resistance against such pH variation is therefore important for widespread acceptance of the methodology. The membrane-modified capillary sensor system utilizes ThT as indicator (Scheme 1) instead of a lipophilic pH indicator such as the well-established chromoionophore series and is not expected to be influenced by sample pH. Indeed, we have confirmed the absence of pH dependence, as shown in Figure 3.
      Despite such large H+ concentration change, the distance values are almost the same, with variations within ±0.3 mm when comparing average values. To the best of our knowledge there is no report on μPADs for alkali ion metal detection that has simultaneously achieved equipment-free detection and such high pH independence.

      Selectivity over Other Interfering Ions

      In sensing biological samples, a high selectivity especially over sodium ion is of great importance because Na+ is in large excess. A high selectivity is expected because of the use of valinomycin in the capillary sensor compartment. Still, an inappropriate lipophilicity of the indicator dye might result in its release in the presence of competing ions, and selectivity of this assay must be confirmed. Also, if other ions strongly influence the flow and retention characteristics of ThT they could act as interference. Figure 4 shows the result of selectivity tests operated with separately prepared solutions and mixed K+-Na+ solutions.
      In Figure 4a, no signal for Na+ and Ca2+ was observed, which is attributed to the high selectivity of valinomycin and the adequate lipophilicity of ThT. Even with conservative values of the selectivity coefficient of valinomycin (KK+,Na+opt = −3.6, KK+,Ca2+opt = −5.0) (42) applied, no response for Na+ and Ca2+ in the range of 1–10 mM is expected. In Figure 4b, the level of Na+ in the sample was dramatically changed in the presence of constant concentration of K+. Still, the distance on the μPADs showed almost the same results. The chemical composition of the assay appears to be fit for the purpose at hand.
      On the other hand, a slight variation of color distance was observed in Figure 4b, where higher levels of Na+ slightly shortened the travel distance of ThT. This may be explained with the destabilization of ThT in aqueous solution in highly concentrated NaCl, which can be understood as a salting-out effect. The destabilized ThT is now more weakly solvated and will more preferably transition from aqueous phase to the cellulose surface. Similar principles are important in paper chromatography where compounds are separated on the paper substrate based on the strength of interaction with the mobile phase. (43)Figure S4 shows the effect of Na+ concentration on the travel distance of ThT, where distance decreases with increasing Na+ levels at a constant concentration of ThT. According to Figure S4, distance variation would be negligible for the detection of K+ in serum where Na+ varies from 130–150 mM (1) (−0.89 to −0.82 on a logarithmic scale).
      According to the response principle, Na+ (counterion of TFPB–) and Cl– (counterion of ThT+) should be extracted to the aqueous solution upon aspiration of sample into the capillary, resulting in a NaCl concentration increase of 4.23 × 10–3 M. According to the selectivity data, extraction of this salt into the potassium-selective membrane should be negligible (Figure 4a). This small change of NaCl cannot affect ThT+ interaction with cellulose as seen in Figure 4b and S4.

      K+ Measurement in Serum

      For the detection of K+ in serum, the design of the μPAD was adapted. The paper substrate was modified with poly(acrylic acid) (PAA) to chemically modify the cellulose surface; the detection channel was laminated to avoid evaporation and the temperature of the μPAD was kept constant at 28–30 °C to prevent serum sample from clogging the channel. ThT could not be successfully adsorbed on the cellulose surface and visually distinguished from the aqueous solution without PAA modification as shown in Figure S5a, most probably because of nonspecific protein adsorption. ThT+ adsorption onto the cellulose surface competes with nonspecific protein adsorption. With a higher charge density of the cellulose surface, ThT+ should be more preferentially adsorbed over proteins. Electrostatic interaction between PAA and positively charged organic cations is known to be very strong, capable of deforming the conformation of cationic species. (44,45) For this reason, PAA was utilized here to modify the cellulose surface. Electrostatic interaction, rather than nonspecific adsorption, is believed to be the primary driving force for PAA retention as proteins exhibit much lower charge density. (44,45)
      A temperature control at 28–30 °C resulted in the furthest flow of serum sample (approximately 2 cm), giving the desired sensitivity. Lower temperatures increase serum viscosity and clogged the channel, while a much warmer channel evaporated the serum sample too rapidly and also increased viscosity, again resulting in channel clogging (Figure S5b). Laminating the detection channel is also indispensable to obtain a long travel distance because it prevents the evaporation of serum traveling in the channel as seen in Figure S5c. Making these adaptations to the μPADs improved the feasibility and reproducibility of serum measurements. Under these optimized conditions, serum samples could be quantitatively measured by distance-based analysis as shown in Figure 5. Please refer to Figure S6 for (a) an illustration of experimental setup and (b) an actual image of distance-based analysis.
      The colored area on μPADs showed different distances that depended on K+ concentration in the serum samples. These μPADs clearly differentiated the highest and lowest K+ level for normal homeostasis despite their comparatively small difference (3.3 mM and 4.9 mM), as well as abnormal (hyper- and hypokalemia) levels of K+. According to Figure 5, it is also possible to determine whether potassium levels fall in or outside the normal range by observing whether the color transition is within the distance interval of 1.0–1.5 cm. This capillary-assisted μPADs system can potentially be used to determine K+ levels in serum and to determine whether they are inside the normal range or indicate a health concern.
      This μPAD mechanism may be applied to any aqueous sample of similar viscosity because calibration curves for each sample may be prepared as done for the serum samples in this work. Viscosity of serum does not differ appreciably from patient to patient (46) but can be calibrated for each individual if needed. (47)

      Ion-selective membrane films cast on the inner wall of the capillary have successfully been operated in an exhaustive sensing mode and consequently shown a sensitive response to a narrow range of K+ concentration (1–6 mM). Capillary-assisted μPADs system exhibited a distinct distance-based signal in the same narrow range of K+ concentration. This system gave invariant signals at pH 7.0 to 9.0 thanks to the pH-independent recognition reaction, as well as a high selectivity in separate solutions and mixed solutions tests. On a thermostated support the device showed attractive distance-based responses to serum. But still, efforts to stabilize the flow on the paper will be important for robust system and behavior. A more serum-permeable paper material may likely help avoiding careful temperature control for this type of assay. Importantly, this capillary-assisted μPAD approach is potentially able to detect other ions by replacing valinomycin with another type of selective ligand, although ionic indicator dyes of different lipophilicity may be required. The approach is also applicable to other aqueous samples as long as the flow conditions on the paper substrate are found to be adequate.
