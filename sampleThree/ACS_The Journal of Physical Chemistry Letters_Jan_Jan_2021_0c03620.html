    Abstract

    Helicenes are promising candidates for chiral optoelectronic materials because of their helically twisted π-conjugated system. However, the emission intensity of unsubstituted helicenes is very weak (Φf < 0.05) due to a small oscillator strength for the S1 → S0 transition. In this work, we investigated the substitution position of the [7]helicene framework so that the S1 → S0 transition has a large transition magnetic dipole moment (TMDM) and is partially symmetry-allowed. A [7]helicene derivative thus designed showed a large fluorescence emission rate (kf = 0.02 ns–1) and a large TMDM for the S1 → S0 transition (|m| = 2.37 × 10–20 erg·Gauss–1), which are more than 10 times greater than those of unsubstituted [7]helicene (kf = 0.001 ns–1, |m| = 0.045 × 10–20 erg·Gauss–1). As a result, we achieved the [7]helicene derivative whose dissymmetry factor of CPL and fluorescence quantum yield were both high (|gCPL| = 1.3 × 10–2, Φf = 0.17) in the solution phase.

    Circularly polarized luminescence (CPL) is a photophysical phenomenon seen in chiral molecules, in which right- and left-handed circularly polarized light are emitted at different intensities. (1−3) The magnitude of CPL depends on how efficiently a chiral molecule emits and how biased the intensities of the right- and left-handed circularly polarized light are. Although chiral molecules with both high-emission quantum yields (Φf) and high dissymmetry factors (gCPL) are desired for applications in sensors, bioimaging, displays, and optical communications based on circular polarization, (4−6) there is a trade-off relationship between Φf and gCPL: chiral molecules showing a high-emission quantum yield tend to have low gCPL values.

    A major challenge in the field of CPL is to establish the design strategy for chiral molecules that show the maximum gCPL value (gCPL = ±2) with quantitative emission quantum yield (Φf = 1); however, a reliable guideline to design such ideal molecules is undeveloped at the moment. Lanthanide complexes are known to show high gCPL values, typically of 0.1–1, (7−9) including the highest value ever reported so far of gCPL = 1.38 for a europium complex, Cs[Eu(hfbc)4] (hfbc = 3-heptafluorobutyryl camphorate). (10) The highest gCPL value indicates the 84.5/15.5 ratio of the left-handed/right-handed CPL emitted from the Eu complex [Table S1, Supporting Information, (SI)]. Some lanthanide complexes show high gCPL values, but their emission quantum yields, particularly corresponding to the f–f and d–f transitions, tend to be low because of the Laporte-forbidden nature. On the other hand, organic molecules are potentially preferable for CPL-active materials, because emission wavelength and emission lifetime can be optimized by the design of molecular structures. (11−13) The drawback of organic molecules is low gCPL values, typically on the order of 10–2 or less due to the large transition electric dipole moments (TEDM) in magnitude compared to the transition magnetic dipole moments (TMDM).

    Recently, Sato, Isobe, and co-workers reported a cylindrical π-conjugated organic molecule, which is one of the primary structures of chiral carbon nanotubes, showing Φf and gCPL values of 0.8 and −0.152, respectively. (14) This work clearly demonstrated that the gCPL values of organic molecules can be greatly improved by utilizing large TMDM intensities. Mori et al. reported that the arrangement of TEDM and TMDM—which can be logically designed in terms of molecular symmetry—is a key to achieve high gCPL values, though the CPL properties of the proposed X-shaped double helicene were modest (|gCPL| = 2.5 × 10–3, Φf = 0.02) likely because the TEDM/TMDM ratio was away from 1. (15)

    Helicenes, which are ortho-fused polycyclic aromatic compounds, are expected to show excellent chiroptical properties because of their helically twisted molecular orbitals. (16−19) Crassous et al. have recently reported impressive reviews on the introduction of heteroatoms into helicene frameworks (20) and subsequent coordination to metal ions, (21) which have great potentials to modulate the chiroptical properties of helicene derivatives. (22,23) One critical drawback of helicenes is that the Φf values of unsubstituted carbohelicenes are usually very low; for example, the Φf values of [5], [6], and [7]helicenes are 0.04, 0.04, and 0.02, respectively. (24,25) We have recently reported that the emission efficiency of [5]helicene derivatives was significantly improved up to Φf = 0.23 by introducing electron-donating and -withdrawing groups at appropriate substitution positions. (26,27) The key strategy for the highly luminescent [5]helicenes was the management of a symmetry-allowed electronic transition in the lowest-energy excited (S1) state, by which the emission rate of [5]helicene was accelerated more than 10 times, which was responsible for the improvement of Φf values.

    We envisioned that the symmetry-allowed magnetic transition can be rationally designed using the same strategy based on the management of frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs). In this work, we demonstrate that arrangement of the FMOs so that the S1 → S0 transition is magnetically allowed is a logical design to achieve highly emissive helicenes with a large gCPL value. By this approach with the help of theoretical calculations, we rationally achieved a [7]helicene derivative whose Φf and gCPL are both high (Φf = 0.17, |gCPL| = 1.3 × 10–2) (Figure 1). The observed Φf and gCPL values are one of the most excellent CPL parameters for helicenes reported so far. (11−13,28−30) The quantitative analysis of TEDM and TMDM, utilizing both experimental and theoretical calculation results, provides a reliable guideline for designing ideal CPL emitters.

    One drawback of [5]helicenes is its non-negligible racemization process that proceeds even at room temperature, because of which isolation of their enantiomers is difficult. The rate of racemization strongly depends on the size of carbohelicenes. The activation barriers of helical inversion processes (ΔG‡) were experimentally determined as 100.8, 151.5, and 174.5 kJ mol–1 at 293 K for [5], [6], and [7]helicene, (31,32) suggesting that the time required to decrease 5% of the enantiomers (τ5%) is 1.1 h, 105 years, and 109 years, respectively. Considering the good thermal stability of enantiomers for large helicenes, we focused on [7]helicenes in this work, and circular dichroism (CD) and CPL properties of their isolated enantiomers were investigated.

    In order to characterize the emission property of the unsubstituted [7]helicene (1), time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) calculation was performed at the TD-RB3LYP-GD3BJ/6-311G(2d,p) level. The empirical dispersion correction (GD3BJ) was found to be effective to reproduce the X-ray crystal structure of [7]helicenes in terms of the helical pitch of its molecular spring geometry (Figures S1 and S2, SI). (33) The molecular structure in the ground (S0) state was then optimized at the lowest-energy singlet excited state (S1), and the convergence of the optimized structure was confirmed by no imaginary frequencies on frequency analysis. The electronic transition properties of 1 were calculated by TD-DFT method at the same level of theory using the optimized structure in the S1 state (Figure 2).

    TD-DFT calculation suggested that the HOMO–1 and HOMO as well as the LUMO and LUMO+1 of 1 were nearly degenerate. The lowest-energy transition (S1 → S0) of 1 consists of the LUMO+1 → HOMO–1 and the LUMO → HOMO transitions, and its oscillator strength was close to zero (f = 0.0006). The small oscillator strength of 1 is likely due to the fact that the MOs involved in each transition share the same symmetry—i.e., the LUMO+1 and HOMO–1 are of C2 symmetry, while the LUMO and HOMO are C2 antisymmetric. The same trend of the small oscillator strength was found in the S1 → S0 transition of the unsubstituted [5]helicene, (26) which is responsible for the small Φf of unsubstituted carbo[n]helicenes (Φf < 0.05, for n ≥ 5). (24,25)

    Organic molecules with degenerated FMOs are suitable for optimizing the symmetry and magnitude of their lowest-energy electronic transitions by introducing substituents. The key concept for optimizing FMOs here is that orbital energy significantly changes by introducing substituents at a substitution position with a large orbital coefficient. (26) Among FMOs of 1, the LUMO had large orbital coefficients at the 7 and 12 positions, whereas those in the LUMO+1 were very small, suggesting that the degeneracy of the LUMO and LUMO+1 of 1 can be removed by introducing substituents at the 7 and 12 positions. The S1 → S0 transition of 7,12-dicyano[7]helicene (2) thus designed consists solely of the LUMO → HOMO transition, which is likely due to the removal of LUMOs’ degeneracy. The orbital symmetries of both the LUMO and HOMO of 2 were C2 antisymmetric, and the oscillator strength for the S1 → S0 transition remained small (f = 0.014).

    Interestingly, the symmetries of the HOMO and HOMO–1 were inverted by introducing electron-donating group at the 3 and 16 positions; the HOMO and LUMO of 7,12-dicyano-3,16-dimethoxy[7]helicene (3) are C2-symmetric and C2-antisymmetric, respectively (Figure S3, SI). Because of the different symmetry between LUMO and HOMO, the S1 → S0 transition of 3 is partially symmetry-allowed with a moderate oscillator strength (f = 0.058) that is about 100 times larger than that of 1 (f = 0.0006). Thus, the molecular design using TD-DFT calculations suggested that the symmetry and shape of FMOs involved in the S1 → S0 transition can be rationally altered by introducing electron-donating and electron-withdrawing groups at the appropriate substitution positions of the [7]helicene framework. It is noted that the introduction of both electron-donating and -withdrawing groups was needed to alter the nature of the S1 → S0 transition for [7]helicenes.

    The theory of optical activity was well documented as a review article by Schellman in 1975. (34) According to the theory, the dissymmetry factor for CPL (gCPL) is described as follows

    gCPL=I−−I+12(I−+I+)=4|μ′||m′|cos⁡θμ,m′|μ′|2+|m′|2
    (1)
    where I is the intensity of luminescence emitted from molecules in the excited state, subscripts + and – represent the properties corresponding to the right- and left-handed circularly polarized light, μ′ and m′ are the TEDM and TMDM vectors, respectively, for de-excitation transitions, and θ′μ,m is the angle between the μ′ and m′ (see the SI for details). Eq 1 suggests that the |μ′|, |m′|, and cos θ′μ,m are the three key parameters for evaluating the gCPL values, and the absolute value of gCPL is maximized (i.e., gCPL = ± 2) under the following conditions: (i) |μ′| and |m′| are the same magnitude (i.e., |μ′| = |m′|) and (ii) the directions of μ′ and m′ vectors are parallel or antiparallel (i.e., cos θ′μ,m = ± 1). (35)

    The CGS-Gaussian units are commonly employed to compare the magnitude of electric and magnetic dipole transitions, because the symmetric Gaussian system assumes the same physical dimensions for the electric and magnetic fields (i.e., ε0 = 1 and μ0 = 1). (36) It is noted that the ratio of coefficients between μ′ and m′ is ea0/μB ≈ 274 in the symmetric CGS units. As a numerical example, given a transition that is both electronically and magnetically allowed (i.e., |⟨φi|r|φf⟩| = |⟨φf|r × ∇|φi⟩| ≈ 1), the |μ|2 is ∼75 000 times larger in magnitude than |m|2, suggesting that electrons in molecules are more than 4 orders of magnitude more sensitive to the electric part of electromagnetic wave than the magnetic part (see the SI for details). The ea0/μB ratio is the physical origin of the fact that the TEDM tends to be roughly 300 times larger than the TMDM for common organic molecules, which is consistent with the fact that the gCPL values for common organic molecules are typically on the order of 10–2 or less (Figure S4, SI). (11−13) In this work, we use the term of “magnetically allowed” transitions to describe the electronic transitions having large |m| values, for example, when the magnitude of TMDM is larger than or equal to 1 in atomic unit (i.e., |m|/erg·G–1 = μB·|⟨φf|r × ∇|φi⟩| ≥ 0.974 × 10–20 in the CGS unit) (Table S3).

    kf,calcd,±=16π33hε0ν~3(D+G∓2R)=2πe2ε0mecν~2f±
    (2)
    where D is the electric dipole strength (D = |μ|2), G is the magnetic dipole strength (G = |m|2), R is the rotatory strength (R = Im(μ·m) = |μ| |m|cos θμ,m), ε0 is the vacuum permittivity, me is the mass of electron, f± is the oscillator strength defined as f± = 8π2ṽmec/(3he2)·(D + G ∓ 2R) (see the SI for details). Eq 2 indicates that the kf is large when TEDM and TMDM are large, and the kf value depends on the sign of CPL for chiral molecules when the R is comparable to the sum of D and G (i.e., R ≈ (D + G)).

    The emission quantum yield is described as the fraction of two decay constants, i.e., the fluorescence emission constant (kf) and the nonradiative decay constant (knr) as follows: Φf = kf /(kf + knr), suggesting that the kf value is necessarily larger than the knr value for highly emissive molecules. The theoretically estimated kf,calcd values based on eq 2 were 0.0002, 0.004, and 0.012 ns–1 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively (Table S4, SI). Since the fast spontaneous emission rates are related to high-emission quantum yields, compound 3 is expected to exhibit a higher Φf value than 1 and 2.

    It is noteworthy that the difference in symmetry and shapes of FMOs greatly affected the magnitude of TMDM for the S1 → S0 transition as well as TEDM. The |m′| value of 3 (|m′| = 2.37 × 10–20 erg·G–1) was more than 50 times larger than that of 1 (|m′| = 0.045 × 10–20 erg·G–1). The calculated |m′| value of 3 is comparable to that of the magnetically allowed transition of coronene (|m′| = 3.67 × 10–20 erg·G–1 for the S7 → S0 transition), where coronene can be regarded as a planar molecular analogue of [7]helicene and can be used as a reference molecule in terms of the similar molecular size to [7]helicenes. (39) According to eq 1, the theoretically estimated gCPL value was 0.018 for 3, which was the largest among 1–3 (|gCPL,calced| = 0.008 and 0.005 for 1 and 2, respectively). Thus, the magnetically allowed S1 → S0 transition of 3 with (i) the modest yet relatively large |μ′| value and (ii) the large |m′| value for the S1 → S0 transition is preferable to achieve an excellent CPL emitter whose fluorescence quantum yield (Φf) and gCPL are both high.

    Compounds 2 and 3 were synthesized according to the route shown in Scheme 1 (see the SI for details), which is based on the same synthetic strategy applied to 5,10-dicyano[5]helicenes. (26,40,41) 6-Methoxy-2-naphthaleneacetonitrile (4a) was synthesized by a palladium catalyzed cross coupling reaction of 2-bromo-6-methoxynaphthalene with trimethylsilylacetonitrile. (42) The Knoevenagel reaction of 4a with terephthalaldehyde provided 1,4-bis(2-(6-methoxynaphth-2-yl)-2-cyanoethenyl)benzene (5a). The twofold photocyclodehydrogenation reaction of 5a in the presence of iodine satisfactory proceeded in a regioselective manner, providing 7,12-dicyano-3,16-dimethoxy-[7]helicene (3) in 88% yield, where the naphthyl group was more photoreactive at the 1-position than at the 3-position likely due to the larger orbital coefficients. (43,44) 7,12-Dicyano[7]helicene (2) was synthesized by the same reaction scheme using 2-naphthaleneacetonitrile (4b) as the starting material (see the SI for details). It is noted that compounds 2 and 3 were easily synthesized from commercially available reagents in only two or three reaction steps, with total yields of 73 and 31% for 2 and 3, respectively.

    The structure of 3 was unambiguously determined by X-ray crystallography (Figure 3). The single crystal for X-ray analysis was obtained from an acetonitrile solution of a racemic mixture of 3. The crystal was composed of a 1:1 mixture of helical enantiomers (monoclinic, space group C2/c) and did not contain any cocrystallized solvent molecules (Table S5 and Figures S5 and S6, SI). The two enantiomers, i.e., (P)-3 and (M)-3, were stacked alternately along the c axis so that the two rings B and C were almost fully overlapped each other with the interplanar distance defined by the rings B and C of dBC–BC = 3.76 Å. The alternating stacking structure continues along the c axis via the π–π interactions between the rings E and F for the next racemic dimer pairs, where a short contact C···C distance of 3.314 Å was detected between the cyano (CN) group and the terminal rings A/G (Figure 3b,c). In addition, when viewed normal from the (101) face, a two-dimensional sheet-like structure was found, which was stabilized by two interactions as suggested by short contacts: (i) the N···H distance between the CN group and the H-9/H-10 of the central ring D (2.515 Å) and (ii) the N···C distance between the CN and methoxy (OMe) groups (3.205 Å) (Figure 3c). It is noted that neither significant π–π stacking nor a sheet-like structure is found in the crystal structure of unsubstituted [7]helicene. (45) Thus, the single crystal of 3 takes a unique packing arrangement likely directed by the CN and OMe groups, which is unusual for helicene derivatives reported so far.

    The UV–vis absorption and emission spectra of 1–3 were measured in chloroform (Figure 4). Compounds 1–3 showed strong absorption bands in the short-wavelength region of 250–300 nm, whose absorption coefficients were larger than 104 M–1 cm–1 (i.e., ε = 5–9 × 104 M–1 cm–1). The lowest-energy absorption band of unsubstituted [7]helicene 1 appeared at λmax,abs = 423 nm with very week intensity (ε = 2.0 × 102 M–1 cm–1) (Figure S7, SI). The weak absorption band was red-shifted to λmax,abs = 462 nm for 2 and 3, and their absorption coefficients were increased to ε = 2.0 × 103 and 4.4 × 103 M–1 cm–1 for 2 and 3, respectively. The increase in absorption coefficients is consistent with the theoretically calculated oscillator strengths for absorption: the oscillator strength for the S0 → S1 transitions was almost zero for unsubstituted 1 (f = 0.0003) and increased to f = 0.014 and 0.063 for 2 and 3 (Figure S8 and Tables S7–S9, SI). The effect of solvent polarity was investigated for 3. The shapes of the absorption spectra in toluene, chloroform, and acetonitrile were almost the same, while the emission band was shifted to longer wavelengths by ∼5–10 nm with increasing solvent polarity (Figures S9 and S22, SI), suggesting a moderate charge-transfer property of 3 in the excited state.

    Upon irradiation with UV light (λex = 365 nm), compounds 1, 2, and 3 showed blue, cyan, and greenish-blue emissions whose maximum wavelengths were located at 443, 475, and 477 nm, respectively, in chloroform (Figure 4). The Φf values of 1, 2, and 3 were 0.02, 0.08, and 0.17, respectively (Table 2); thus, the Φf for 3 was 8.5 times larger than that of unsubstituted [7]helicene. From the standpoint of emission color tuning for the CPL-active materials, the Φf value of more than 15% for the greenish-blue emission (λmax,FL = 477 nm) is comparable to the recent examples reported by Zhao (46) and Qiu. (47) The area-weighted mean fluorescence lifetime of 1 was 19.4 ns, suggesting the small oscillator strength for the S1 → S0 transition of unsubstituted [7]helicene. The fluorescence lifetime significantly decreased to 13.3 and 8.5 ns for 2 and 3, respectively (Figures S11–S14 and Table S6, SI). The experimental values of the emission rate (kf) and the nonradiative rate (knr) constants were evaluated using the following equations: kf = Φf/<τf> and knr = (1 – Φf)/<τf>. The resulting kf and knr values for 1–3 were summarized in Table 2. According to the experimentally determined rate constants, the increase in the Φf value from 1 to 3 clearly originates from the increase of the kf value (kf = 0.001, 0.006, and 0.020 ns–1 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively). Although the slight increase in knr in the order of 1–3 contributes negatively to the Φf values, the increase of knr (i.e., knr = 0.05, 0.07, and 0.10 ns–1 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively) was a minor contribution compared to the large increase in the kf value. It is noted that the experimentally determined kf values were well consistent with the theoretically expected kf,calcd values (0.0002, 0.004, and 0.012 ns–1 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively; Table S4, SI), suggesting that the TD-DFT calculations conducted at the B3LYP-GD3BJ/6-311G(2d,p) level well reproduced the emission properties of 1–3 recorded in chloroform. Thus, the measurements of emission quantum yield and lifetime are necessary to establish the reliability of DFT calculations to discuss the origin of g values of CPL-active molecules.

    It is worth noting that the magnetically allowed electronic transitions are preferentially detected in CD spectra with strong intensities. According to a careful examination of the results of TD-DFT calculations, the S0 → S1 transitions of 1 and 2 were assignable as magnetically forbidden-like transitions with weak TMDM magnitudes (|m| < 0.2 × 10–20 erg·G–1). The S0 → S1 transitions of 1 and 2 do not contribute significantly to the experimentally observed CD signals (Figure S8, SI). The first positive CD signals for (P)-1 and (P)-2 are mainly attributed to the higher-energy excited states with large TMDM magnitudes (|m| = 2–3 × 10–20 erg·G–1); the major contributions are the S0 → S2 and S0 → S3 transitions for 1 and the S0 → S2 and S0 → S4 transitions for 2 (Tables S7 and S8, SI). On the other hand, the S0 → S1 transition of 3 was magnetically allowed with a large TMDM magnitude (|m| = 2.2 × 10–20 erg·G–1) and was the main contribution to the first positive CD signal for (P)-3 (Table S9, SI). Thus, the large gCD values of 3 originate from the S0 → S1 transition, whereas the gCD values of 1 and 2 are related to the higher-energy transitions (i.e., the S0 → Sn transitions where n = 2, 3 for 1 and n = 2, 4 for 2).

    In contrast to absorption spectra consisting of multiple electronic transitions involving higher excited states (i.e., the S0 → Sn transitions), fluorescence bands of common organic molecules are attributed solely to the lowest-energy deexcitation (i.e., the S1 → S0 transition), which is known as Kasha’s rule. (49,50) Consequently, the S1 → S0 transition must be magnetically allowed to achieve intense CPL signals with a large gCPL value. The isolated (P)-isomers of 1–3 showed CPL spectra whose sign was opposite to those observed from the (M)-isomers (Figure 4). The gCPL values were +0.006 (450 nm), −0.003 (500 nm), and +0.013 (550 nm), for the (P)-isomers of 1, 2, and 3, respectively (Table 3), whose absolute values were again well consistent with the theoretically estimated gCPL,calc values (gCPL,calc = −0.008, −0.006, and +0.018 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively). The large gCPL value for 3 is likely attributed to the large TMDM magnitude for the S1 → S0 transition as discussed in the previous section (Table 1). By conducting TD-DFT calculations for reference compounds (51−53) at the same level of theory, we confirmed that the recently reported helically twisted molecules showing good CPL properties share the common characteristic of the magnetically allowed S1 → S0 transitions (Figure S23 and Table S13, SI). Therefore, it is reasonable that the design of the magnetically allowed S1 → S0 transitions is a key strategy for creating good chiral molecules showing strong CPL emission.

    The experimentally observed |gCD| and |gCPL| values of 1–3 are summarized in Figure 5. The g values for emission (gCPL) of 3 were close to that for absorption (gCD) in terms both of sign and magnitude (gCPL = +0.013 and gCD = +0.016) and are consistent with the fact that the CD and CPL signals of interest in 3 are both attributed to the lowest-energy HOMO–LUMO transitions. It is noted that the spatial distributions of the HOMO and LUMO of 3 were almost the same between the S0 and S1 states (Figure S26, SI). In contrast, the gCPL values of 1 and 2 were significantly smaller than the gCD values, which is likely due to the involvement of different electronic transitions in absorption and emission. Interestingly, the sign of the CD and CPL signals was opposite for 2, i.e., (P)-2 showed the first positive CD signal and a negative CPL upon photoexcitation (Figure 4b), supporting the conclusion that different electronic transitions are involved in absorption and emission. The product of the emission quantum yield and the |gCPL| value (i.e., Φf × |gCPL| or the CPL efficiency, Figure 5) can be defined as a measure for evaluating the CPL property from the viewpoint of (1) the emission intensity and (2) the selectivity of the left-handed and right-handed circularly polarized light. The ratios of the CPL efficiency were 1.2, 2.4, and 22.1 for 1, 2, and 3, respectively (Table S13, SI). In this context, the CPL efficiency of 3 was substantially improved by a factor of ∼20 times with respect to the unsubstituted [7]helicene, which was achieved by introducing only four small substituents with retaining the [7]helicene main framework.

    The resulting total TEDM and TMDM densities (ρtotalμ and ρtotalm) for the S1 → S0 transition of 1–3 are shown in Figure 6. The directions of μ′ and m′ vectors for 1 and 2 were parallel to the C2 symmetry axis of the helicene framework (Figure 6a). The shape and distribution of ρtotalμ and ρtotalm densities of 1 are similar to those of 2 (Figure 6b,c), which is consistent with the fact that the S1 → S0 transitions of 1 and 2 are mainly attributed to the LUMO to HOMO transition, where the shape of the involved MOs is similar to each other between 1 and 2 (Figure 2). Considering that the spatial integrals of the ρtotalμ and ρtotalm densities correspond to the |μ′| and |m′| values, respectively, the transition moment densities with the opposite sign cancel each other out. Therefore, the small |μ′| and |m′| values of 1 and 2 are represented as a similar size of blue (positive) and red (negative) lobes in ρtotalμ and ρtotalm (Figure 6b,c).

    In contrast, the spatial arrangement of μ′ and m′ of 3 was different from that of 1 and 2; the m′ vector of 3 directs almost parallel to the helical axis of the helicene framework. The μ′ vector of 3 is oriented in the direction from the rings B to F and formed an angle of 60° with the m′ vector (cos θ′μ,m = 0.50). According to the ρtotalμ density of 3, the rings B, C, E, and F as well as the CN group have major contributions to the |μ′| of 3. The spatial distribution of the ρtotalm density has a different trend compared to the ρtotalμ density: the |m′| of 3 is mainly attributed to the central rings C, D, and E, whereas the terminal rings A, B, F, and G of the [7]helicene framework have minor contributions to the TMDM of 3 (Figure 6c). We found that the ρtotalμ tends to have large values in spatial coordinates where the involved MOs have a large orbital overlap, whereas the ρtotalm tends to be large where the bonding and antibonding nature alter between the involved MOs (Figure S32, SI). In addition, the large orbital overlaps between the involved MOs is likely desired to increase |m′| values. Interestingly, the characteristic spatial distribution of the ρtotalm density of 3 was commonly found for the recently reported helical molecules; the TMDM density tends to localize at the central rings of helical geometries, and the terminal rings do not contribute to the magnitude of |m′| for the CPL-active helically twisted molecules (Figures S33–S35, SI).

    In summary, we have achieved a reliable guideline for designing chiral molecules with both a high-emission quantum yield (Φf) and high dissymmetry factor for emission (gCPL). Organic molecules with degenerate FMOs, such as helicenes, have a significant potential to improve their CPL properties. The strategic substitution of electron-withdrawing and electron-donating substituents into a [7]helicene framework allowed us to alter the types of their FMOs as desired. 7,12-Dicyano-3,16-dimethoxy[7]helicene (3)—which was logically designed with the aid of theoretical calculations—showed a good emission quantum yield (Φf = 0.17) with a high dissymmetry factor for emission (|gCPL| = 1.3 × 10–2) and the intense CPL was attributed to the magnetically allowed S1 → S0 transition. We also confirmed that the nature of the magnetically allowed S1 → S0 transition is a common feature to recently reported helically twisted molecules showing good CPL properties (Figure S22 and Table S13, SI).

    To achieve the ideal CPL emitters showing the maximum |gCPL| values (i.e., gCPL = ±2), there are two major approaches to equalizing the magnitudes of TEDM and TMDM (i.e., |μ′| = |m′|). One approach is to decrease |μ′|, which can be achieved (i) by forming H-type aggregates, in which TEDM cancels each other between adjacent dyes, (4,62−64) or (ii) by designing a charge-transfer-type electronic state, in which the overlap integral between the HOMO and the LUMO is small. (43) We note that this approach—to decrease |μ′|—has an inherent drawback: the Φf value is expected to be small with decreasing |μ′| due to the small kf value, because the emission rate constant is proportional to the square of |μ′| as described by eq 2. To observe a significant intensity of emission from a molecule with such small kf values, the decrease of the nonradiative decay constant (knr) is necessary as suggested by the equation of emission quantum yield, i.e., Φf = kf /(kf + knr). The other approach, that is, to increase |m′|, is therefore more promising; however, there is no logical strategy for increasing the magnitude of TMDM at the moment. Our transition moment density analysis has provided important insights into the relationship between the shape of FMOs and the magnitude of TMDM; the TMDM density (ρtotalm) tends to be large where the bonding and antibonding natures alter between the HOMO and LUMO with large orbital overlaps. According to eq 5, ring-shaped molecules with large diameters have the potential to have a large |m| value, because the large coordinate values of the position operators (i.e., x̂, ŷ, and ẑ) are multiplied at the outer edge of the molecules (Figure S23 and Table S13, SI), which is consistent with the tendency for long linear molecules to have large |μ| values. This finding will be a logical guideline for the further investigation of a molecular structure with a large TMDM in magnitude, therefore showing large g values both for absorption and emission. Further molecular design toward highly emitting organic molecules with high gCPL values—including thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF)-type CPL-active emitters (65−67) that can be applicable to circularly polarized organic light emitting diodes (CP-OLED) (68−70)—is currently under investigation in our group.

Here, we report the systematic design of ideal thermally activated delayed fluorescence mols. with near-degenerate 1CT, 3CT and 3LE states by controlling the distance between the donor and acceptor segments in a mol. with tilted intersegment angles. This system realizes very fast RISC with a rate const. (kRISC) of 1.2 x 107 s-1, resulting in org. light-emitting diodes with excellent performance, particularly at high brightness.

        Nanosecond-time-scale delayed fluorescence molecule for deep-blue OLEDs with small efficiency rolloff
        Kim, Jong Uk; Park, In Seob; Chan, Chin-Yiu; Tanaka, Masaki; Tsuchiya, Youichi; Nakanotani, Hajime; Adachi, Chihaya

        Arom. org. deep-blue emitters that exhibit thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) can harvest all excitons in elec. generated singlets and triplets as light emission. However, blue TADF emitters generally have long exciton lifetimes, leading to severe efficiency decrease, i.e., rolloff, at high c.d. and luminance by exciton annihilations in org. light-emitting diodes (OLEDs). Here, we report a deep-blue TADF emitter employing simple mol. design, in which an activation energy as well as spin-orbit coupling between excited states with different spin multiplicities, were simultaneously controlled. An extremely fast exciton lifetime of 750 ns was realized in a donor-acceptor-type mol. structure without heavy metal elements. An OLED utilizing this TADF emitter displayed deep-blue electroluminescence (EL) with CIE chromaticity coordinates of (0.14, 0.18) and a high max. EL quantum efficiency of 20.7%. Further, the high max. efficiency were retained to be 20.2% and 17.4% even at high luminance.
