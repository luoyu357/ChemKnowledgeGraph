    Abstract

    The impedance characteristics and ac conductivity behavior of the silicon oxy-carbide (SiOxCy:H) network have been investigated during its transition from an amorphous dominated to a crystalline populated structure, grown by varying only the growth temperature (TS) from 160 to 180 °C. At elevated TS, the Si nanocrystals (Si-ncs) increased in size and number density, with corresponding elevation in crystallinity from 28 to 45%. Two distinct dielectric relaxations for the individual a-SiOxCy:H and nc-Si components were identified in the low-frequency and high-frequency zones, respectively, for the film deposited at higher TS (180 °C). In contrast, for the lower TS (160 °C)-grown film, a significant fraction of the ultra-nanocrystalline (unc-Si) component contributed only one consolidated relaxation peak at a moderate frequency. The scaling measurements of the imaginary part of impedance (−ZIm) revealed two distinct physical events for a-SiOxCy:H and nc-Si, both being temperature scale-independent. The activation energy of relaxation is low for the ordered nc-Si component and high for the disordered a-SiOxCy:H matrix. According to the Bode plot analysis, the presence of several defects in the grain boundary contributed a poor carrier lifetime to the nc-Si component, while the a-SiOxCy:H matrix with lesser defects had a longer carrier lifetime. An equivalent circuit model formed by parallel resistors and constant phase elements (R||CPE), following Nyquist plot analysis, demonstrated an intense association between the microstructural and charge carrier characteristics. The temperature-dependent ac-conductivity behavior of the mixed-phase material is well explained by the nonoverlapping small polaron tunneling model, at two individual stages across a transition from an amorphous to crystalline configuration around a small span of growth temperature.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Silicon is an attractive material for electronic devices, including solar cells. (1−3) Over the last few decades, scientists have been looking forward to making low-cost solar cells for commercialization on a large scale. (4) In this regard, a thin-film silicon solar cell is a great choice where the few thin layers of Si and its composite material are used to make an efficient solar cell at a low cost. (5) The utilization of a broad spectrum of solar energy typically requires a wide band gap window layer to permit its extensive portion to reach the absorber layers of the solar cells at maximum intensities (6−8) and also a narrow band gap absorber layer to consume the infrared part of the solar spectrum. (9,10) In the past, several studies were carried out on the nanocrystalline silicon oxide and silicon carbide thin films, which were found promising for their wide band gaps and good conductivities via involving Si nanocrystals (Si-ncs). (11,12) Apart from this, nanocrystalline silicon oxy-carbide (nc-Si/a-SiOxCy or nc-SiOxCy) films, in which silicon nanocrystals (Si-ncs) are embedded in the amorphous silicon oxy-carbide (a-SiOxCy) matrix, are also an attractive material for the window layer of Si solar cells. (13) The barrier height of a-SiC (2.5 eV) being significantly lower than a-SiO (9 eV), the SiOxCy matrix, with an intermediate barrier height, could allow an easy flow of electrons. (14,15) On the other hand, the oxygen in the matrix helps form Si-ncs in the amorphous matrix via thermodynamically optimum phase separation and high stability of the amorphous phase. Consequently, the presence of O atoms facilitates the formation of Si-ncs in the SiOxCy matrix compared to that of SiCx and could promote improved electrical conductivities. (16,17)
    Moreover, attaining a significant nanocrystallinity, in general, requires a high substrate temperature and a very high H2-dilution of the reactive plasma. (18,19) However, at high substrate temperature, the quality of the device deteriorates by thermally simulated interdiffusion and cross-contamination inside the stacking layers. (20) Furthermore, a high H2-dilution deteriorates the growth rate of the Si thin films, which is not desirable for cost-effective device fabrication. In this context, the dilution of SiH4 plasma by Ar or He has been reported to increase the plasma density via energy transfer from their individual excited states, for example, Ar* and He*, thereby increasing the film growth rate. (21−23) Accordingly, in the present experiment, the precursor gas SiH4 has been diluted by a mixture of H2 and He to maintain a reasonably good crystallinity at an acceptable growth rate of the silicon thin-film network.
    The dc electrical characteristics of these two-phase materials have been investigated in several studies. (24−27) However, ac electrical transport, impedance spectroscopy, and dielectric properties are studied only in a few studies, including two of the studies reported from our laboratory on B-doped nanocrystalline silicon oxide and P-doped silicon oxide thin films. (28−31) Impedance spectroscopy, dielectric property, and ac electrical transport study can explain various structural evolutions, defect state distribution, band tail defect dynamics, and many more. (28,30) Moreover, the ac electrical transport on nc-SiOxCy thin films is not available in the literature, to the best of our knowledge.
    In this study, intrinsic nanocrystalline silicon oxy-carbide (nc-SiOxCy) thin films are produced in standard plasma enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) at low temperatures and using a combination of (SiH4 + CH4 + CO2 + H2 + He) gases. A narrow substrate temperature window of 160–180 °C has been chosen to specifically concentrate on studying the changes in the impedance characteristics and ac conductivity behavior in the vicinity of the changeover phase from an amorphous to crystalline configuration of the network. It has been attained by maintaining all influential plasma parameters identical and only by making a minor change in the growth temperature, effectively influencing phase switching. The structural and bonding configurations and also elemental characterization have been performed. The ac electrical conductivity, frequency- and temperature-dependent impedance characteristics, and the dielectric properties have been pursued. The influence of the individual components, for example, the nc-Si and amorphous matrix constituents, on the electric and dielectric properties, that is, the changes in resistance, capacitance, and individual dipole relaxation phenomena, is explained. Furthermore, the individual contributions of the short-range, mid-range, and long-range ordering of Si−Si bonds (that is, amorphous silicon oxy-carbide matrix, nanocrystalline silicon grain, and ultra-nanocrystalline silicon on the grain boundary components) on the sample’s overall impedance, dielectric constant, and ac conductivity, have been attempted to correlate. It is undoubtedly a unique investigation on the amorphous to nanocrystalline transition phase of the silicon-oxy-carbide ternary composite material.

    Synthesis Procedure

    The capacitively coupled PECVD technique in 13.56 MHz radio-frequency was employed to prepare the nanocrystalline silicon oxy-carbide (nc-SiOxCy) films on Corning Eagle 2000 glass substrates, n-type (100) Si substrates, and carbon-coated copper microscope grids. A combination of silane (SiH4), carbon dioxide (CO2), and methane (CH4) was used as the reactant source gas, and helium (He) and hydrogen (H2) were used as the diluent gases, maintaining a flow rate proportion of SiH4:He:H2:CO2:CH4 = 0.5 sccm:25 sccm:25 sccm:0.15 sccm:0.15 sccm. The chamber pressure during deposition was kept fixed at 0.5 Torr, while the substrate temperature (TS) was varied from 160 to 180 °C. The plasma was ignited at an electrical RF power of 260 W applied to the parallel capacitor plate electrodes.

    Characterization

    The thickness of the nc-SiOxCy films was preserved within 400–500 nm, as estimated via UV–vis spectrophotometry, following Swanepoel’s method. The Si–H, Si–O, and Si–C bonding configurations of the films were investigated using FTIR absorption spectroscopy (Nicolet Magna-IR 750). The Raman back-scattering spectra of the samples were taken at ambient temperature using a Renishaw inVia micro-Raman spectrometer operated at a wavelength of ∼514 nm and a low power density of ∼2 mW cm–3. High-resolution transmission electron micrographs and elemental mapping were attained using a JEOL-JSM2010 transmission electron microscope operated at 200 kV.
    Electrical characterization of the films was carried out using two thin parallel Al electrodes deposited on the films in a coplanar configuration. The samples’ ac-conductivity and impedance properties were investigated using an HIOKI IM3536 LCR meter over a 4 Hz to 8 MHz frequency regime. Data collection was performed during a cooling cycle from 420 to 300 K while maintaining a steady differential voltage at a small Vrms of ∼1 V. The dielectric constant (ε), conductance (G), impedance (Z), and the phase (Φ) of the samples were recorded as a function of frequency (f). A simple parallel equivalent circuit model was evaluated from the overall samples’ impedance (Z) measured in the coplanar transportation of charges across two-terminal Ohmic contacts.

    Impedance Spectroscopy Studies

    Various electrical properties of the nc-SiOxCy:H films, such as dielectric relaxation, charge carrier conduction, and resistivity, are studied using impedance spectroscopy. The complex impedance (Z = ZRe + iZIm) as varying with frequency was evaluated for all the samples in different temperature environments, where ZRe and ZIm represent the real and imaginary parts, respectively, of the complex impedance Z. The frequency dependence of ZRe at different temperatures for the films prepared at TS = 160, 170, and 180 °C is plotted in Figure 5. The ZRe is more significant at lower temperatures than at elevated temperatures in the low-frequency range, implying the escalation of electrical conductivity with temperature that demonstrates the semiconducting nature of the material. With increasing frequency, ZRe reduced rapidly, in general, and converged in the high-frequency regime, probably due to the discharge of space charges compensating for the lowering of the barrier at the grain boundary. (49) In general, the point at which a reversion in the slope of the curvature occurs is known as the inflexion point. The films prepared at TS = 170 and 180 °C have two inflexion points in the low-frequency and high-frequency regions, signifying two types of relaxation prevailing in the material. (50) The higher-frequency region concerns the relaxation in the relatively ordered section of the matrix, that is, within the nanocrystalline and ultra-nanocrystalline moieties.
    In contrast, the low-frequency regime indicates a relaxation in the disordered parts of the material where the dipoles take a longer time to orient themselves. However, the film prepared at TS = 160 °C demonstrates only one inflexion point, implying that within its a-SiOxCy:H-dominated matrix, the trivial nc-Si phase fraction could not make its different influence on the impedance characteristics. Moreover, a mixed type of mid-range ordering behavior may appear prominent due to the presence of an intermediate phase such as unc-Si.
    In general, the energy of the applied electric field is dissipated within the material via the charge polarization mechanism. At a particular frequency, the maximum polarization is followed by the maximum energy loss of the electric field. At a further increase in the field frequency, the polarization lags behind the field. Hence, the frequency at which the maximum loss of the electric field occurs is called the relaxation frequency of the material complex. The imaginary part of the impedance (ZIm) corresponds to the dielectric loss; consequently, any peak in the frequency versus ZIm curve is called the relaxation peak. Figure 6 depicts the imaginary parts of impedance (ZIm) as a variable of frequency at several temperatures for all the thin films. At elevated temperatures, ZIm decreased. The frequency versus ZIm plots for the films prepared at 170 and 180 °C show two relaxation peaks at separate frequency zones, at low frequency and high frequency. Based on the temperature, the a-SiOxCy:H matrix is responsible for the low-frequency peak below ∼103 Hz. The second one, located beyond ∼104 Hz, is related to the nc-Si relaxation. (51,52) When the temperature increases, a shift toward higher frequencies is noticed for both the low-frequency and high-frequency relaxation peaks. The relaxation frequency is inversely associated with the product of resistance and capacitance. In nc-SiOxCy:H, semiconducting material resistance reduced at elevated temperature and relaxation frequency increased. However, the film prepared at 160 °C shows only one combined peak, indicating no proper phase difference between the nanocrystalline and amorphous counterparts and a mid-range ordering dominates over the impedance characteristics. However, the semiconducting nature of the film matrix is evident from the reduced intensity of the relaxation peak at elevated temperatures and its corresponding shift toward the higher frequency, as for the higher TS cases.     Figure 8 depicts the Bode curve (phase angle vs log f) at various temperatures for all nc-SiOxCy:H films. Information on the charge carrier lifetime in a material can be extracted from such a plot. The average lifetime, that is, the average time span for the excited conduction band electrons to recombine with a hole, has been computed using the equation τe = 1/(2πfm), in which fm is the Bode plot’s mid-frequency peak. (29,55) There are two different peaks in the spectra for films made at TS = 170 and 180 °C. The presence of nc-Si in the conduction process is linked to the appearance of a high-frequency peak, and the a-SiOxCy:H component contributes to the low-frequency peak. At ambient temperature, the charge carrier lifetimes in Si-ncs and a-SiOxCy:H matrices for the film grown at TS = 170 °C are ∼0.46 μs and 2.3 ms, respectively, whereas the same for TS = 180 °C change to ∼0.49 μs and 0.84 ms, respectively.
    On the other hand, the film prepared at TS = 160 °C has only one peak in the Bode plot, and the overall carrier lifetime is 24.6 μs. In this case, the film has low crystallinity, and a large amount of mid-range ordering is present, so a combined carrier lifetime is observed. However, in every case, the carrier lifetime decreased with increased temperature.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The present work deals with comprehensive studies on the structural and electrical properties of nc-SiOxCy:H thin films. PECVD synthesis of Si thin films is a complicated chemical kinetics process. It is determined mainly by the plasma parameters within the reactor system and the state of the growing surface. The diffusion length of the adsorbed SiHx (x = 1, 2, and 3) radicals on the growing surface increase with increasing substrate temperature and also with the assistance of atomic H in the plasma by increasing the mobility of the adsorbed radicals. (18) A longer diffusion length can assist adsorbed radicals in reaching the most energetically favorable position, forming an ordered and very well crystalline structure. Precursors are not mobile enough at low TS = 160 °C to obtain a low energy configuration by diffusing inner sites.
    Furthermore, the low diffusion length of radicals at low TS results in weaker Si–Si bonds. As a result, the films are amorphous, as preliminary structural analyses demonstrate. However, when TS increases, the surface diffusivity of the adsorbed precursors increases and bond rearrangement is also conceivable. These actions help to improve structural order, allowing the transition from the amorphous to nanocrystalline phase to occur at TS ∼170 °C. Again, at elevated temperature, the weakly bonded O, C, and H atoms are removed from the growing surface by thermal energy and only the relatively stronger Si–O, Si–C, and Si–H bonds are left in the network. Thereby, with an increase in substrate temperature, the O and C content in the sample reduces considerably. It leads to optimum favorable conditions for the separation between the amorphous and crystalline phases at TS = 170 °C. The effect of phase separation is prevalent in impedance spectroscopy. The real part of impedance governs the two separate inflexion points for the a-SiOxCy:H matrix at the low-frequency zone and nc-Si grains at the high-frequency zone. The imaginary part of impedance also governs two separate relaxation peaks for the a-SiOxCy:H matrix at the low-frequency zone and nc-Si at the high-frequency zone. The activation energy of the relaxation time (ΔEτ) in the a-SiOxCy:H part reduces with an increase in the TS, referring to the ordering in the amorphous parts as the dipoles in the ordered region relax more quickly than in the disordered region. Again, ΔEτ in the nc-Si part is low compared to the a-Si as the ordering is very high in the nc-Si parts compared to the a-Si. The ΔEτ in the nc-Si part reduces with the increase in substrate temperature due to an increase in nc-Si grain size. However, the film prepared at comparatively low-temperature (TS = 160 °C) has only one relaxation peak. There is no influence of the nc-Si grains separately observed in the relaxation mechanism as the a-SiOxCy:H component primarily dominates the film matrix. For the nearest neighbor sample grown at TS = 170 °C, the moderate activation energy of the dipole relaxation, with magnitude in between the values of the corresponding a-Si and nc-Si phases, indicates that the film possesses ample mid-range ordering of Si–Si bonds as it is identified in the Raman spectroscopy data that the film has high strength of the unc-Si/nc-Si ratio.
    With the increase in TS, the H+ ions’ diffusion length increases, and these penetrate deep into the film network and more profoundly etch out the weakly attached Si atoms. Subsequently, the voids and the defects are generated, and the H-atoms which are contributed to the passivation of the amorphous–nanocrystalline interface are reduced. The consequence is that with the increase in TS, the microstructural factor increases, while the surface passivation index decreases, ultimately reducing the carrier life time in the amorphous matrix. In addition, the nanocrystalline grains have defective grain boundaries and dangling bonds where the charge is recombined; this is why the carrier lifetime in the nc-Si grains always remains much lower than the a-SiOxCy:H matrix.
    In the Nyquist plot analysis (Figure 9), it is observed that the activation energy for the resistance in the nc-Si and a-Si parts is reduced with the increase in TS because the size of the Si nanocrystals increased and the ordering in the a-Si parts elevated due to the enhanced thermal energy, as was observed from the changes in the dc conductivity characteristics in Figure 12d. The ac conductivity of the films, as shown in Figure 12a–c, increased up to a specific frequency, beyond which further increase in frequency corresponds to its gradual decline that might arise due to the intrinsic defect contribution in the biphasic material, for example, oxygen vacancy, dangling bonds, and so forth, and also due to the significant presence of the conducting sp2-hybridized graphitic carbon in the film network, as visible in C 1s core-level spectra of XPS spectroscopy data. (66,67) In the sp2-hybridized graphitic carbon phase with large numbers of free electrons, electron conduction is attenuated at high frequency, leading to the high-resistive region on the path of electrons in the nc-SiOxCy:H network.
    A similar phenomenon was observed in different nanomaterials, for example, annealed ZnS thin films (68) and neutrino-irradiated nanograined SiO2 clusters, (69) where the metal-like characteristics of the material was evident after a threshold frequency. The emission of electrons from the defect states of the biphasic material induced by the high-frequency electrical excitation could create high carrier density and contribute to the significant lowering in ac conductivity at such a high frequency, behaving like the metal following the Drude model.
    The overall ac conductivity of the material depends on three factors: (i) hopping of the charge carriers in the material complex, (ii) free charge carriers from the defect states, which increase the carrier density and induce metal-like conduction at high frequencies, and the (iii) conduction of the free charge carriers in sp2-hybridized graphitic carbon. At the low frequency, the charge carrier hopping dominates the conduction process, leading to an increase in the ac conductivity. As the frequency of the applied field increases in the middle- to the high-frequency zone, free charge carriers generated from the defect states and free electrons in the graphitic carbon dominate over the hopping conduction; finally, the high-frequency ac electron conduction is attenuated following Drude theory. (70) The calculated defect state density [N(EF)] from the NSPT model shows an elevation with the increase in TS due to the increased defects arising from the Si-nc’s grain boundary.     Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    I.

    The present work concerns the comprehensive studies on structural and impedance spectroscopy and their correlation to the intrinsic silicon oxy-carbide thin film prepared in conventional PECVD. The prepared thin films in a low-temperature range of 170–180 °C have good electrical conductivity and enough crystalline volume fraction to be useful for device fabrication.

    II.

    Thin films produced at TS ≥ 170 °C with considerable crystallinity and a reasonable unc-Si fraction have two separate and grossly different components, a-SiOxCy:H matrix and nc-Si component. Finally, two separate dielectric relaxations for the a-SiOxCy:H and nc-Si components were identified in the low-frequency and high-frequency zones, respectively. However, the film prepared at TS = 160 °C with lower crystallinity and a considerable portion of the unc-Si section possesses only one consolidated relaxation peak at a moderate frequency. It reveals that solely the nc-Si phase in the matrix cannot demonstrate its prominent distinctiveness in the entire system, occupied mainly by the a-SiOxCy:H component.

    III.

    The activation energy of relaxation is low for the ordered nc-Si component, while it is high for the a-SiOxCy:H disordered matrix. Nevertheless, the activation energy of relaxation in the nc-Si and amorphous matrix decreased at elevated substrate temperatures. It might be due to the induced ordering in the a-SiOxCy:H matrix and the increased size of the Si-ncs.

    IV.

    nc-Si parts with numerous defects in their grain boundary have a low carrier lifetime, while the a-SiOxCy:H matrix with fewer defects has a high carrier lifetime.

    V.

    Assumed that two parallel R||CPE deduce the individual component (nc-Si and a-SiOxCy:H matrix) contribution to the resistance and the capacitance in the film. With the increase in substrate temperature, the Si-nc’s size increases and the activation energy of resistance in the nc-Si part is reduced. Again, at elevated substrate temperature, ordering is induced in the a-SiOxCy:H matrix and the activation energy of resistance in this part is reduced.

    VI.

    ac conductivity analysis shows that the films follow the Jonscher power-law exponent (s) between 0 and 1. The NSPT model explains the temperature-dependent conductivity mechanism. The density of defect states at room temperature is low for the lower crystalline sample, which decreases at elevated frequency.

    VII.

    The intrinsic defects in the biphasic material, for example, the oxygen vacancy, dangling bonds, and also the conducting sp2-hybridized graphitic carbon present in the film network, deteriorate the dielectric nature of the films, and the films demonstrate metallic behavior after a particular frequency.
