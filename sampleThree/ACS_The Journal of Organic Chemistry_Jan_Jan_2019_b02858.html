      Abstract

      Two 1,2-benzodiazinyl radicals, cinnolinyl radicals by name, were successfully isolated by cascade routes using 1,4-naphthoquinone as a precursor. Reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with hydrazine hydrate promotes a (5e + 5H+) redox cascade affording benzo[g]naphtho[1,2-c]cinnolinyl-7,12,14-trione (Cn•) in 69% yields, while the similar reaction with 2-hydrazinopyridine is a (7e + 7H+) oxidative cascade and furnishes N-pyridinecinnolinyl radical (PyCn•). The cascades are composed of C–N and C–C bond making reactions. The neutral even alternate arenes are always diamagnetic; thus, the isolation of Cn• and PyCn• is a breakthrough. The Cn•/Cn– and PyCn•/PyCn– redox couples are reversible, and the reaction of Cn• with [CuI(PPh3)3Cl] in the presence of hydrazine hydrate and Et3N affords a Cn– complex of copper(I), [(Cn–)CuI(PPh3)2] (1). Similar to phenalenyl radical, PyCn• exists in three redox states, PyCn+, PyCn•, and PyCn–, in a smaller potential range (−0.30 V to −0.60 V vs Fc+/Fc couple) and can be used as an oxidant as well as a reductant. PyCn• acts as a catalyst for the oxidative cleavages of benzil to benzoic and 2,2′-pyridil to picolinic acids in methanol in the presence of air. The molecular and electronic structures of Cn•, PyCn•, and 1·1/2MeOH were confirmed by single crystal X-ray crystallography, EPR spectroscopy, and DFT calculations.

      Paramagnetic neutral odd alternate hydrocarbon (OAH) where the unpaired electron resides on a nonbonding molecular orbital is a threshold in designing nonmetal super conductors proposed by Haddon. (1) In this regard phenalenyl, an OAH, was explored widely in elucidating super conductivity, (2) catalytic activity, (3) and designing memory devices, (4) and the results are promising in many cases. The growth of hydrocarbon radical chemistry visited is based on the skeleton constituted of phenalenyl fragment. The neutral even alternate hydrocarbons, which are abundant, are diamagnetic and lack this important feature. However, the scope remains open for the heteroarene incorporating NH functions that are oxidizable to N• as established in the case of Blatter radical. (5) In such cases neutral even alternate heteroarene can be paramagnetic. In this study, the same was established by isolating two neutral even alternate paramagnetic heteroarenes as 1,2-benzodiazinyl radicals. Benzodiazines are a class of azine compounds that exhibit diverse biological activities. (6) These were worthy in medicinal and agrochemistry. (7) Several routes were developed in synthesizing benzodiazine derivatives. (8) However, no paramagnetic benzodiazine derivative is reported so far.
      In this investigation we succeeded in designing redox cascades that furnish hitherto unknown 1,2-benzodiazinyl radicals. Cascades that have high atom economy with lower chemical waste are worthy in developing the syntheses of functional organic molecules. (9) However, examples of redox cascades are limited in scope. (10) Recently we have disclosed that 1,4-naphthoquinone derivatives are precursors of redox cascades. (11) In this particular search, two stable 1,2-benzodiazinyl radicals as illustrated in Chart 1, benzo[g]naphtho[1,2-c]cinnolinyl-7,12,14-trione (Cn•) and N-pyridinecinnolinyl (PyCn•) radicals by name, were successfully isolated in high yields exploiting 1,4-naphthoquinone as a precursor. It is noteworthy that Cohen in 1966 claimed that benzo[g]naphtho[1,2-c]cinnoline-7,12,14-trione (CnH) was isolated from a reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with hydrazine hydrate in methanol. (12a) Pummerer et al. in 1929 also reported the isolation of CnH from a reaction of 2,2′-dinaphthoqinone with hydrazine hydrate. (12b) Both of them considered that they had isolated the diamagnetic CnH with a paramagnetic impurity and missed to assign the Cn•. In this report, it is disclosed that reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with hydrazine hydrate in air promotes a redox cascade affording Cn• as a major product, and no CnH was isolated from this reaction. The similar reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with 2-hydrazinopyridine yields PyCn•. The structural skeletons of Cn• and PyCn• are different: the former contains an 1,4-iminoquinone fragment. The redox activity of Cn• is notably different from PyCn• that resembles the chemistry of phenalenyl. Notably, the properties of Cn• and PyCn• are different from common hydrazyl radicals. (13)
      Isolation of a stable cinnolinyl radical that belongs to an even alternate hydrocarbon family is a milestone to the growth of the chemistry of neutral paramagnetic aromatics and designing functional materials and devices. The preliminary investigation establishes that PyCn• acts as a catalyst for the oxidative cleavage of benzil to benzoic acid in methanol in the presence of air. Oxidative cleavage of benzil to benzoic acid is not common, generally in the presence of base benzil undergoes rearrangement to benzilic acid. (14) Thus, modeling of a catalyst for this redox reaction is significant in metallo-organic chemistry, and in this regard, the activity of PyCn• is worthy.
      Transition metal complexes of Cn• will be a venue to stabilize other redox states of it and to open up the possibilities of their interconversions detecting the spectral and structural features and the activities. In this context, the first benzodiazinate complex of a transition metal ion is reported. In this particular study, a cinnolinate (Cn–) complex of copper(I), of type [(Cn–)CuI(PPh3)2] (1), was isolated and characterized. The molecular and electronic structures of Cn•, PyCn•, and 1 were confirmed by single crystal X-ray crystallography, EPR spectroscopy, cyclic voltammetry, and density functional theory (DFT) calculations.

      Details of the syntheses of Cn•, PyCn•, and 1 and their characterization data are outlined in the Experimental Section (vide infra). Reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with hydrazine in methanol promotes a redox cascade affording Cn• in 69% yields. The plausible path of the cascade are shown in Scheme 1. The reaction proceeds via 1,4 addition reaction of hydrazine to 1,4-naphthoquinone followed by tautomerization affording A. In presence of air as an oxidizing agent, A undergoes oxidation, tautomerization, oxidation, condensation, and further oxidation, furnishing Cn•. The conversion of A to Cn• in air is faster and no intermediate of this transformation has been successfully detected. In the ESI mass spectrometry, the reaction mixture just after 2 min gives a single m/z peak at 325 due to Cn• (Figure S1). In presence of air, the reaction produces H2O2, which was detected chemically by reacting with KI solution evolving I2. In presence of starch, the broader UV–vis absorption band at 600 nm of the reaction mixture (Figure S2) infers the formation of a starch-I2 complex. (15a) It is similar to the antharaquinone process of H2O2 production. (15b) The study concludes that the conversion of 1,4-naphthoquinone to Cn• in the presence of hydrazine is a redox reaction. It is overall a (5e + 5H+) oxidative coupling reaction as given in eq 1.

      However, in absence of air the reaction is slower and different as depicted in Scheme 1. Analysis of the reaction mixture under argon by ESI mass spectrometry confirmed the formation of A and a hydroquinone derivative B as given in Figure S3. In presence of I2B undergoes oxidation to C that gives the product Cn•, where I2 is reduced to iodide. Pummerer et al. (12b) established that C is a precursor of Cn•, supporting the notion of formation of C as an intermediate of this oxidative coupling reaction in the presence of air and I2. The formation of iodide was authenticated by detecting mass peaks due to iodide and a monoiodo adduct of Cn• in ESI mass spectrometry (Figure S4). The study infers that the conversion of 1,4-naphthoqione → Cn• in the presence of hydrazine needs an oxidizing agent.
      The similar reaction with phenylhydrazine produces naphthalene-1,4-diol evolving nitrogen gas, while the reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinone with less reducing 2-hydrazinopyridine promotes a different cascade affording PyCn• in 71% yields. It is a (7e + 7H+) oxidative dimerization of two 1,4-naphthoquinone units bridged by hydrazinopyridine fragment. The cascade is composed of 1,4 addition reaction (C–N bond formations), tautomerization, oxidation, radical coupling (C–C bond formation) reactions, and oxidation of amine to aminyl radical as depicted in Scheme 2. The intermediates of these reactions were analyzed by ESI mass spectrometry. The concerted 1,4-addition of the hydrazine derivative with two 1,4-naphthoquinones were authenticated by ESI mass spectrometry of the solution obtained after 5 min of both reactions (see, Figure S5). Notably no mass peak due to mono hydrazine-1,4-naphthoquinone derivative was detected.

In this reaction, hydrazine acts as a reductant converting Cn• to Cn–. The reaction is expressed by eq 2.

The molecular geometries in the crystal forms and the atom labeling scheme are illustrated in Figure 1, Figure S6(a,b). The crystallographic data are summarized in Table S1.       Redox Activity

      The redox activities of Cn•, 1 and PyCn• were investigated by cyclic voltammetry in acetonitrile at RT using tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate as a supporting electrolyte, and the potential data referenced to Fc+/Fc couple are listed in Figure 2. The cyclic voltammogram of Cn• displays two cathodic waves respectively at −1.14 and −1.56 V (Figure 2(a)) due to Cn•/Cn– and Cn–/Cn•2– redox couples (Scheme 3).
      The cyclic voltammogram of 1 is exactly similar to that of Cn• (Figure 2(b)), precluding the mixing of the metal with benzodiazine ligand. The anodic Cn•/Cn– redox couple of 1 appears at −1.12 V, while the same for free Cn• is observed at −1.14 V. The cathodic Cn– /Cn2– redox wave of 1 appears at −1.44 V.
      The redox activity of PyCn• is different from Cn• and 1 (Figure 2(c)). Notably, the cyclic voltammogram of PyCn• is similar to that of phenalenyl. PyCn• exhibits a reversible anodic wave at −0.34 V due to PyCn+/PyCn• redox couple. The first cathodic wave at −0.61 V due to PyCn•/PyCn– is quite reversible, while the second cathodic wave at −0.86 V due to 1,4-naphthoquinone/1,4-naphthosemiquinonate anion radical redox couple is not reversible. In comparison to Cn•/Cn– redox couple, the PyCn•/PyCn– couple is positively shifted by 0.53 V, inferring that PyCn• is stronger reducing than Cn•. Similarly the second cathodic wave due to quinone reduction is positively shifted by 0.70 V. The study infers that PyCn• is an oxidizing as well as a reducing agent. It exists in three redox states as PyCn+, PyCn•, and PyCn– as depicted in Scheme 3, in a smaller potential range (−0.30 to −0.60 V), and PyCn• is a stable state and isolable in air.

      EPR Spectroscopy and DFT Calculations

      The X-band EPR spectrum of the powder sample of Cn• exhibits a strong signal at g = 2.002 as illustrated in Figure S7(a), correlating well with those of organic radicals. (18) The isotopic signal of Cn• in CH2Cl2 at g = 2.003 (Figure S7(b)) does not reveal any hyperfine splitting. The fluid solution EPR spectrum of 1+ obtained from a constant potential (at −0.80 V) bulk electrolysis experiment in CH2Cl2 displays a strong EPR signal at g = 2.0014 (Figure S7(c)), authenticating that 1+ is a Cn• complex of copper (1). The powder sample spectrum of PyCn• displays a strong signal at g = 2.003 (Figure 3(a)). The fluid solution spectrum of PyCn• shows a hyperfine splitting due to 14N nucleus (I = 1, AN = 6.4 G) as depicted in Figure 3(b). However, the frozen glass spectrum of PyCn• with a signal at g = 2.003 does not display any hyperfine splitting.
      The spin density distributions of Cn• and PyCn• obtained from Mulliken spin population analyses are illustrated in Figure 4(a,b). In both cases the spin is dominantly localized on the benzodiazine ring. In Cn•, the alpha spin scatters on N(1) (18%), C(3) (27%), and C(11) (15%) atoms, while the beta spin disperses on the alternate C(2), C(12), and N(2) atoms. In addition the alpha spin is significantly localized on C(3) (61%) and O(3) (26%) atoms. In case of PyCn•, the maximum alpha spin bearing atom is C(3) carbon (41%), and the rest is localized on N(1), N(2), C(11), and O(2) atoms, while the beta spin is localized on C(2) and C(12) atoms. It implies that the benzodiazine ring actively participate in both oxidation and reduction reactions. Formation of a C–Cσ bond enhances the quenching of the phenalenyl radical; however, in case of PyCn• no dimerization occurs as the C(3) atom is a member of two fused rings. Two significant frontier molecular orbitals of PyCn•, SOMO and beta-LUMO, of this unique heteroarene dominantly disperse on the benzodiazine ring, while the alpha-LUMO scatters on one of the 1,4-naphthoquinone fragments as depicted in Figure S8.
      It is noteworthy that the calculated electron affinity of PyCn• is relatively larger (2.69 eV), while the calculated ionization energy is smaller (6.50 eV) and comparable to those of alkaline earth metals. The calculated energies of the frontier molecular orbitals of PyCn+, PyCn•, and PyCn– are depicted in Figure 5. The HOMO of PyCn+ is stabilized by 8.74 eV than that of PyCn–. Similarly, the LUMO of PyCn+ is 7.43 eV lower in energy than that of PyCn–. The energy of SOMO of PyCn• is an intermediate between that of the HOMOs of PyCn+ and PyCn–. The analysis predicts that PyCn• is a stronger reducing agent, and the same was established by cyclic voltammetry and chemical activities.
      The calculated bond lengths of 1 and CnH are summarized in Table 2. The calculated bond lengths of Cn– in 1 correlate well with those of CnH; particularly, the N(1)–N(2) lengths are 1.326 and 1.329 Å, respectively, in 1 and CnH. However, the bond lengths of the benzodiazine ring in 1+ are similar to those of Cn•. The N(1)–N(2) lengths of 1+ and Cn• are 1.314 and 1.309 Å, respectively. The spin density distribution of 1+ ion as depicted in Figure 4(c) is also similar to that of Cn•, confirming that 1+ is a Cn• complex of copper(I).

      Electronic Spectra

      The UV–vis–NIR absorption spectra of Cn•, 1, and PyCn• were recorded in CH2Cl2, and the spectra are shown in Figure 6(a,b). The spectrum of Cn• displays an absorption band at 530 nm. The absorption spectra of Cn• and Cn– forms are different. 1 containing Cn– as a ligand exhibits a lower energy broader band at 720 nm and the characteristic bands at 470 and 450 nm. The absorption feature of free Cn– ion was recorded by spectroelectrochemical measurement (Figure 7(a)). Cn– reveals absorption bands at 750, 500, and 480 nm.
      The feature is similar to that of 1, only bands are red-shifted by 30 nm. Thus, the lower energy absorption band of 1 at 720 is due to the Cn– ligand. In Cn• it is absent, and during oxidation of 1 the band gradually disappears (Figure 7(b)). PyCn• exhibits a broader band at 500–550 nm and a weaker NIR band at 800 nm. Both bands are assigned to πAr → πNB (singly occupied) charge transfer transitions (Ar = aromatic and NB = nonbonding). The changes of spectral features during PyCn• → PyCn+ and PyCn• → PyCn– conversions were recorded by spectroelectrochemical measurements and are illustrated in Figure 7(c,d). During both conversions the absorption band of PyCn• at 500–540 nm diminishes significantly. In PyCn– the NIR band at 800 nm is absent, while in PyCn+ it is hypsochromically shifted to 725 nm.

      Catalytic Activity

      PyCn• has been established as a catalyst for the oxidative cleavage of benzil to benzoic acid in methanol in air. Oxidative cleavage of benzil to benzoic acid is not common, generally in the presence of base benzil undergoes rearrangement to benzilic acid. (14) However, the reaction of benzil (2.0 mmol) with PyCn• (0.07 mmol) in methanol in the presence of triethylamine (0.03 mmol) and air at RT for 48 h affords benzoic acid (3.44 mmol), corresponding to 86% conversion (see experimental section in SI). The plausible path of the catalysis is depicted in Chart 3. Reaction of benzil with PyCn• produces PyCn+ and a paramagnetic intermediate G that reacts with O2 molecules and generates intermediate H.
      Reduction of H by another molecule of PyCn• generates a dioxetane intermediate I. I is not stable and undergoes homolytic cleavage affording benzoic acid.       A cascade route in isolating a new family of heteroarene radicals as 1,2-benzodiazinyl radicals (cinnolinyl radicals) is disclosed. Two structurally different cinnolinyl radicals, Cn• and PyCn• were successfully isolated using 1,4-naphthoquinone as a precursor. Reaction of 1,4-naphthoquinoe with hydrazine hydrate is (5e + 5H+) redox cascade and affords Cn•, while the same with 2-hydrazinopyridine is a (7e + 7H+) oxidative cascade and affords PyCn•. The redox properties of Cn• and PyCn• are significantly different. Cn• exhibits only cathodic wave due to Cn•/Cn– and Cn–/Cn•2– redox couple, while PyCn• in addition to the cathodic wave displays a reversible anodic wave due to PyCn+/PyCn• redox couple. Thus, PyCn• is a reducing agent and different from hydrazyl radicals. Similar to phenalenyl radical, PyCn• exhibits three redox states, PyCn+, PyCn•, and PyCn–. PyCn• is a catalyst promoting oxidative cleavage of benzil to benzoic acid in air. A Cn– complex of copper(I) containing PPh3 as coligands was successfully isolated, opening up an opportunity to compare the bond parameters and spectral features of Cn• and Cn–. Cn• and PyCn• are first paramagnetic neutral even alternate heteroarenes and more stable than the phenalenyl radical that has been considered as a functional material in devising electronic devices and catalysts for several organic conversions. Thus, isolation of Cn• and PyCn• is a breakthrough in organic radical chemistry.

          Efficient energy calcns. and structure optimizations employing second-order Moller-Plesset perturbation theory (MP2) are presented.
