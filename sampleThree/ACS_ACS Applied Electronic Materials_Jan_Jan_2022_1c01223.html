    Abstract

    Smart window glazing technology possesses a great potential for reducing the overall energy demand in the building sector. Thin films based on thermochromic vanadium dioxide (VO2) are ideally suited as functional layers of thermochromic smart windows. VO2 itself exhibits a significant change in optical transparency and reflectivity in the infrared spectrum. However, the optimization of VO2-based thin films for application as intelligent window glazing is still ongoing. The improvement is rather tedious because the key parameters set by the application such as transmittance modulation ΔT2500, luminous transmittance Tlum, solar transmittance modulation ΔTsol, and transition temperature ϑc affect each other. Codoping of VO2 with strontium (Sr) and tungsten (W) drives the parameters close to the desired range. However, the requirements set for the use as smart window coating cannot be fully met. Here, we show by a systematic comparison of simulations and experiments that embedding a layer of thermochromic V1–x–ySrxWyO2 between an optimized TiO2 buffer and a TiO2 antireflection layer already adds additional degrees of freedom to simultaneously fulfill all smart window requirements for the optical parameters ΔT2500, Tsol, Tlum, and ΔTsol. In particular, the luminous transmittance Tlum is improved by not less than ≈10% in such a trilayer structure.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Owing to the general increase of global energy consumptions and the evolving need for cooling due to global warming, the world faces severe ecological challenges. Especially the accelerated growth of so-called megacities and the ongoing urbanization yield 30–40% of the world’s energy demand. This significant fraction of the demand almost entirely originates from the climate control, i.e., heating or cooling, of the existing building stock. (1,2) Thus, smart window glazing technology can offer a valuable contribution to reduce the overall energy demand in the future. Such devices are built to modulate the amount of light transmitted in the visible and infrared spectral range in response to an external change of temperature (thermochromism). (3−6) Note that there exist other external stimuli which may trigger the insulator-to-metal transition. (7−9)
    The by far most studied thermochromic material is vanadium dioxide (VO2). Its thermochromic behavior was reported by Morin as early as 1959. (10) It exhibits a rather abrupt switching of its transparency in the infrared (IR) spectral range at a phase transition temperature ϑc of 68 °C. This switching from high transmission of IR light to large IR reflectance is a result of an insulator-to-metal transition accompanied by a structural phase transition from a monoclinic (M1) to a rutile (R) crystal structure. Note that there exist different low-temperature VO2 phases (M1, T, and M2) which may be stabilized by doping, external strain, or stoichiometry engineering. (11)
    The switching from high IR transmittance to high IR reflectance of a thermochromic coating on the glazing forms the basis of using thermochromic smart windows for controlling the heat flux from the environment into the interior of buildings. A thermochromic smart window should switch its transmittance at about 25 °C and exhibit a neutral color impression which should not change at ϑc. However, binary VO2 does not possess ideal properties for this application. First, the phase transition temperature of ϑc = 68 °C is too high, and second, the band gap of VO2 is too low, yielding a brownish color of the glazing which is not desirable.
    Different approaches may be pursued to realize a lowering of the transition temperature of VO2; among others, strain engineering, employing nanosized VO2 or core–shell particles, and doping on the atomic level are feasible. (4,5,12) However, the most common approach of those listed is doping, where other metal atoms substitute vanadium host atoms yielding Me-doped V1–xMexO2 thin films. Tungsten is the most effective dopant reported for this purpose. (12) Alternatively, oxygen host atoms may be substituted by fluorine atoms yielding VO2–xFx with a suitable low ϑc. (8−10)
    Various ways may be anticipated to modify the color impression of the VO2 layer. Examples are the variation of its thickness, employing an effective medium consisting of VO2 nanoparticles in a transparent matrix material, or doping on the atomic level. (12−19) Especially doping of VO2 with earth alkaline metals on vanadium sites widens the band gap significantly and yields a neutral grayish color impression of the corresponding thin films. (16,20) Two key parameters for quantifying the optical properties of the thermochromic layer are the luminous transmittance Tlum and the solar transmittance Tsol. The former characterizes the integral transmission in the visible spectral range weighted with the spectral sensitivity of the human eye. The latter describes the integral transmission across the solar spectrum AM 1.5. (21,22) Values specified by the application should be more than 40% for both parameters. (16) These optical parameters vary with the actual crystalline phase of the same layer ─ rutile at high temperature (HT) and monoclinic at low temperature (LT). Therefore, one also introduces the relative changes of Tlum and Tsol with the phase transition ϑc, namely ΔTlum and ΔTsol as another set of key parameters. The former variation should preferably be zero (virtually no change of color impression), and the latter should be larger than ≈10% (significant switching of the heat flow into the interior of the building). (21,23) Currently, VO2 thin films cannot fulfill simultaneously these requirements set by industry for thermochromic smart windows. (23) However, some of us recently demonstrated that codoping of VO2 by Sr and W yields quaternary V1–x–ySrxWyO2 thin films which simultaneously fulfill almost all requirements. (24) Those thin films were not yet embedded in a multilayer structure.
    Further improvement of the parameters is anticipated by incorporating the thermochromic layer into a multilayer system. (25−27) In this work, we will focus on a trilayer system consisting of a TiO2 buffer layer, an thermochromic V1-x-ySrxWyO2 layer and an additional TiO2 layer serving as antireflection coating across the visible.
    Several materials have been employed as buffer layers for thermochromic VO2. (28,29) The right choice of buffer layer will positively affect the crystallinity and thus the optical modulation capability of the VO2 layer deposited on top. Especially TiO2 gained attention as buffer layer, as it stabilizes the high-temperature rutile structure of VO2 during deposition, which should be advantageous in lowering ϑc. (30,31) In particular, we will study the effect of the surrounding layers on the set of key parameters (Tlum, Tsol, ΔTlum, ΔTsol,) and compare with corresponding simulations of the optical properties using the Essential Macleod software package. The aim is to demonstrate that such a trilayer system offers “intrinsically” the possibility to fulfill simultaneously all requirements set by industry. Indeed, we find that (Tlum, Tsol, ΔTlum, ΔTsol) can be improved in the layer system with virtually no effect on ϑc. In giving this proof, we, for the time being, ignore additional features desirable from an application point of view such as low growth temperatures and the use of float glass substrates. Instead, we use quartz glass substrates and comparatively high growth temperatures of 450 °C and higher. Furthermore, we employ ion-beam sputter deposition (IBSD) and conventional radio-frequency (rf) sputtering in the layer growth as this allows us to realize multilayers of high quality, which indeed may be compared with the simulations.

    Experimental Procedures

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    TiO2 buffer layers are grown on quartz glass in a custom-made IBSD setup based on an rf ion source of the type RIM-10. (32) The ion beam is directed on to a sintered ceramic TiO2 target. The plume of sputtered material is directed on to the quartz glass substrate where the TiO2 thin film is formed. A series of buffer layers of different thickness are deposited for three different heater temperatures of the substrate, i.e., room temperature, 250 °C, and 650 °C (cf. Figure S1). The heater temperature is controlled by a Eurotherm temperature controller. We choose rutile buffers grown at 650 °C for the studies described below. Functional thin films of binary VO2, ternary V1–yWyO2, ternary V1–xSrxO2, and quaternary V1–x–ySrxWyO2 are rf-sputtered from a pure vanadium target on to substrates with the rutile TiO2 buffer layers. Codoping is established by tungsten screws and a piece of strontium attached to the same vanadium target. The temperature of the substrate heater is set to 450 °C. More details about the growth can be found elsewhere. (16,24) In some cases, an antireflection (AR) coating is deposited by IBSD using the same growth parameters as for the TiO2 buffer, but a lower growth temperature of 450 °C, which is the same as for the thermochromic layer. This is done to minimize structural and compositional changes of the latter when overgrown by the TiO2 AR coating. A scheme of the investigated multilayer structure is shown in Figure 1a.
    Optical transmittance measurements are performed by using a Lambda 900 UV–vis–NIR spectrometer from PerkinElmer. For the temperature-dependent measurements, we use a resistively heated sample holder operated by a Eurotherm heating controller combined with a Peltier cooling device to establish defined heating/cooling rates. A PT100 sensor is used to monitor the actual sample temperature. An ellipsometer (SENresearch 4.0 from Sentech) in combination with a temperature stage (Linkam THMS60) is employed to measure the optical response of the thin films and multilayers with the VO2-based layer in its LT as well as its HT state. The ellipsometry data are analyzed in terms of real and imaginary part of the refractive index n and k in both cases. Furthermore, the reflectance of the thin films and the multilayers is measured with the VO2-based layer in either its LT or HT state. Corresponding optical simulations are performed by using the Essential Macleod software and are based on the optical constants of the constituent materials taken from the Essential Macleod database for rutile TiO2 and ellipsometry data for VO2 (cf. Figure S2). The composition of the doped thin films is determined by using a PHI VersaProbe II spectrometer utilizing a monochromized Al Kα (1486.6 eV) X-ray anode to excite under an angle of 45° to the surface normal. Charge compensation is achieved with a combination of an e– gun and an Ar+ gun. The samples are exposed to a focused Ar+ beam with an acceleration voltage of 1 kV to remove adsorbed impurities.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Crystalline TiO2 may occur in two main polymorphs: the tetragonal anatase structure and the rutile structure. The latter is the desired one for our purpose as it possesses the same crystal structure as HT VO2. It usually is obtained at higher temperatures. For the three deposition temperatures used, only the samples of the series deposited at 650 °C exhibit the rutile structure. Furthermore, it is found that the root-mean-square (rms) surface of the samples of this series is better than 0.5 nm, independent of layer thickness up to thicknesses of about 100 nm. In this range of thicknesses, the layer thickness varies linearly with the deposition time, allowing us to adjust accurately the layer thickness in the growth process. More details about the IBSD of the TiO2 buffer can be found in Figure S1.
    Figure 1b shows a color plot of the values of the simulated modulation of the transmittance ΔT2500 at a wavelength of 2500 nm between the LT and HT phase of M1-VO2 (transmittance modulation, ΔT2500) as a function of the thicknesses of VO2 film and the rutile TiO2 buffer layer of a corresponding multilayer structure without AR coating. Such a simulation is instructive, as layer thicknesses will undoubtedly affect the overall optical performance due to interference of multiple reflections at the interfaces. The simulation results reveal that a VO2 layer thickness of 35–45 nm yields the highest transmittance modulation ΔT2500 independent of the choice of rutile TiO2 buffer layer thickness. Because sputtering typically yields polycrystalline material of small grain size, a certain minimum thickness is required to obtain closed layers approaching the ideal situation. Thus, we decided to select the upper limit of ≈45 nm VO2 thickness for our further experimental studies.
    Furthermore, an additional TiO2 buffer will always reduce ΔT2500. However, in experiments there is a trade-off as the rutile buffer enhances the structural quality of the VO2 deposited on top. Thus, there should be an optimum finite thickness of the rutile buffer layer for the experimental layer stack. To determine this optimal buffer thickness, we deposited 45 nm VO2 layers on quartz substrates with rutile TiO2 buffer layers with thicknesses ranging from 0 to 130 nm. Figure 1c shows the corresponding absolute experimental T2500 values (squares) for both the LT (blue) and the HT (red) phase in comparison with the corresponding simulated values (asterisks) versus layer thickness. Figure 1d depicts the corresponding ΔT2500 values versus layer thickness. The overall agreement between experiment and simulation is very good. However, the comparison confirms the behavior anticipated for the experimental data. Below a thickness of about 15 nm (shaded gray area) the experimental ΔT2500 results deviate from the simulation; above they closely follow the simulated results. Thus, the optimum rutile TiO2 buffer layer thickness turns out to be about 15 nm. Therefore, we also decided on using a 15 nm TiO2 buffer layer sputtered by IBSD for all codoped VO2 samples discussed in what follows.
    Figure 2 shows examples of (a) the transmittance spectra, (b) transmittance hysteresis, and (c) optical key parameters representative for the evolution from undoped VO2 to quaternary V1–x–ySrxWyO2 thin films. The stoichiometry of all the functional thin films was determined by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy. The codoped V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin film on the TiO2 buffer exhibits a higher transmittance (red HT/blue LT) than the corresponding undoped VO2 thin film (brown) of the same thickness on the same TiO2 buffer. (24) In particular, the increase in the visible range is due to Sr incorporation. On the other hand, ΔT2500 is somewhat lower for the quaternary material. The transmittance hysteresis curves at 2500 nm of the two samples depicted in Figure 2b reveal the effect of W doping, which brings the transition temperature ϑc down to temperatures close to room temperature.
    As reference, results of ternary and quaternary thin films on SnO2 buffers, reported previously in ref (24), are shown as dashed and dashed-dotted lines. The trends found for those samples on SnO2 buffers are essentially the same than those for the binary VO2, corresponding ternary V1–yWyO2 or V1–xSrxO2 (not shown), and the quaternary V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin films on TiO2 buffers. Incorporating W reduces ϑc, and incorporating Sr increases the band gap independent of the type of buffer layer used. The differences in transmittance values between the two series occur because the VO2-based layers of the samples of the previous series possess a higher Sr content of 11.9% compared to the new samples on TiO2 buffer and are thinner.
    It is worth noting that the values of ϑc are only little affected by the choice of buffer layer.     The key parameters ϑc, Tlum in the LT and HT phase, and ΔTsol (and ΔT>800, see definition below) are summarized in Figures 2c, 2d, and 2e. The corresponding target ranges set for ϑc, Tlum, and ΔTsol by the use in thermochromic smart windows are indicated in green. The data points again confirm the trends in the key parameters due to Sr and W doping, as indicated by arrows. Furthermore, it is apparent that the key parameters of primary importance, ϑc, Tlum, and ΔTsol are closest to the target range for the quaternary V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin film on the optimized TiO2 rutile buffer. Only ΔTsol is still somewhat low but considerably improved compared to the other samples. The improvement of ΔTsol for the V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin film on the optimized TiO2 buffer has its origin in the nearly 10% larger ΔT2500 value (cf. Figure 2a). In more detail, ΔT2500 is 35.22% for this V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin film on TiO2 as compared to 28.20% for the optimized quaternary V0.872Sr0.119W0.009O2 thin film on SnO2. (24) The increase in ΔT2500 directly causes the ΔTsol ≈ 9.0%; the value almost doubled compared to previous work. This boost of the switching of the IR transmittance is best reflected by the following newly defined key parameter T>800, which solely takes into account the solar radiation in an interval between 800 and 2500 nm:(2)We denote the corresponding change of this parameter between the HT and LT phase as ΔT>800. We obtain ΔT>800 = T>800,LT – T>800,HT = 10.2% for the V0.872Sr0.119W0.009O2 thin film on SnO2 and a larger ΔT>800 = 14.5% for the V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 thin film on TiO2. The ΔT>800 values of all layers discussed are depicted as open symbols in Figure 2e. Obviously, the functional layer of composition V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 exhibits by far the best IR performance of the codoped V1–x–ySrxWyO2 samples.
    The trends in the three key parameters of the samples clearly demonstrate that a trade-off between the values of Tlum and ΔTsol is unavoidable. It is anticipated that completing the trilayer structure by a properly designed AR coating will help to raise Tlum again. This will help to remedy the restrictions given be the opposite trends for Tlum and ΔTsol which makes it difficult to shift the values of all three key parameters into the corresponding target ranges set by the application as a smart window.
    An additional AR coating layer on top of a functional V1–x–ySrxWyO2 thin film can be used for suppressing the reflection of the layer system by destructive interference in a specified wavelength range (here, the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum, i.e., 550 nm as center wavelength λ0) and thus enhancing the transmission in this range. However, as we will see below in more detail, it will also affect the reflectance in other wavelength regions (here, the IR spectral range). Essentially the impact on the reflectance is determined by the layer’s thickness and its refractive index contrast with respect to the surrounding media, i.e., air with refractive index nair = 1, and the underlying thermochromic thin film whose refractive index depends on whether the layer is in the LT or the HT phase. Analyzing a simple three layer model air/AR coating/VO2 by using the Fresnel equations yields the following relations for the maximal reflectance suppression at λ0:(3)where d is the thickness of the AR coating and nAR its refractive index at λ0; nVO2 is the refractive index of VO2 at λ0. The first relation defines the criterion for selecting the “best suited” material for the AR coating in dependence on the surrounding media, and the second relation then simply determines the AR coating’s thickness. The refractive index at 550 nm of VO2 in its LT and HT phase is 2.95 and 2.53, respectively. (33) If we consider the HT phase and account for nair = 1, a value ≈ 1.59 would be ideal for the refractive index of the AR coating. Several transparent materials may be considered such as SiO2 (nSiO2 = 1.55), MgO (nMgO = 1.64), MgF2 (nMgF2 = 1.384), Al2O3 (nAl2O3 = 1.76), or TiO2 (nTiO2 = 2.64). (34−37) Restricting the materials employed in the multilayer stack in this way may be favorable in terms of low-cost mass production. Thus, although being somewhat unfavorable, we decided to use TiO2 as material for the antireflection coating, since we already use rutile TiO2 as a buffer layer. The second relation in eq 3 yields a thickness d ≈ 50 nm for the TiO2 AR coating.
    Figure 3 illustrates simulated transmittance (a–d) and corresponding reflectance spectra (e–i) obtained for different (multi)layer systems for the low temperature (blue) and the high temperature state (red). Here, the wavelength dispersions of the optical constants of the constituent materials were taken from the Essential Macleod database (TiO2) or ellipsometry data (VO2 and V1–x–ySrxWyO2 (cf. Figure S2).
    Figure 3a shows the transmittance behavior of a single 45 nm layer of binary VO2 on a quartz substrate. Adding a 15 nm TiO2 buffer layer (cf. Figure 3b) does not induce a significant change in transmittance behavior, and thus, the key parameters ΔTsol and Tlum are affected by less than 2%. Both spectra very much resemble the experimental spectra given in Figure 2a. The absolute values of the experimental transmittance are somewhat lower, most likely caused by deviations from the ideal such as interface roughness, intrinsic porosity of sputtered thin films, or surface oxidation of VO2, which were neglected in the simulations. Figure 3c reveals that a 15 nm TiO2 capping on top of VO2 film starts to affect the transmittance. A relative maximum in the transmittance shows up at about 420 nm for the spectrum where VO2 is in the LT phase. Upon increasing the TiO2 capping layer thickness to 50 nm (cf. Figure 3d), the transmittance maximum becomes more pronounced and shifts as anticipated to higher wavelengths in the visible spectral range. This means that the 50 nm TiO2 enhances Tlum considerably. The value of Tlum of the trilayer structure 15 nm TiO2 buffer/45 nm VO2/50 nm TiO2 capping compared to that of the single 45 nm VO2 thin film is increased from 25.19% to 48.94% when VO2 is in the LT phase and from 33.17% to 52.56% when it is in the HT phase. The effect of the introduction of the TiO2 capping on visible light is also even more pronounced in the corresponding reflectance spectra e–h. The maxima appearing in the transmittance spectra c and d for a 15 and 50 nm TiO2 capping, respectively, are accompanied by clear minima in the corresponding reflectance spectra where the reflectance virtually drops to zero. This proves that the 50 nm TiO2 coating really acts as an AR coating for visible light centered at λ0 ≈ 550 nm.
    The introduction of 50 nm TiO2 capping of the VO2 layer also affects the transmittance spectra in the IR spectral range and, thus, the corresponding key parameters. In particular, ΔTsol is adversely affected as can be seen in the evolution of the transmittance spectra a–d and the corresponding reflectance spectra e–h. In both series, the intersection point of LT and HT spectra shifts to higher wavelength upon adding the TiO2 capping. This shift is accompanied by a reduction in the transmittance modulation ΔT2500 from 65.30% for the single VO2 layer to 52.29% for the trilayer structure. The same holds for ΔTsol.
    Finally, we simulated the robustness of the TiO2 AR capping layer design against a variation of the stoichiometry of the thermochromic layer. Figure 3i shows the calculated reflectance spectra for two trilayer structures differing only in the stoichiometry of the embedded thermochromic layer. In one case, it is binary VO2 (solid lines) and in the other V1–x–ySrxWyO2 (dashed lines). Both calculations were performed for the thermochromic layer in its LT (blue) and its HT phase (red). Although the dispersions of the optical constants n and k differ (cf. Figure S2), the impact of the variation of the stoichiometry is rather small compared to the overall effect of introducing the capping layer. Thus, we tentatively assume that our conclusions drawn from the modeling of the optical response of trilayer systems involving VO2 as thermochromic layer can to a first approximation be transferred to trilayers employing V0.912Sr0.076W0.012O2 as active material. Also in this case, the optimization of Tlum by adding an AR layer optimized for visible light is not straightforward as it will affect the key parameters for the IR spectral range. Nevertheless, the trilayer structure introduces additional degrees of freedom for tuning the key parameters.
    To verify these findings experimentally, we deposit a 50 nm thick TiO2 layer on top of a quaternary V0.920Sr0.076W0.004O2 (cf. Figure 4). The 50 nm TiO2 AR coating causes an enormous increase in the transmittance in the visible wavelength region from 40% without capping to 60% with capping (cf. Figure 4a). However, for λ > 1200 nm in the same graph, the impact of the TiO2 capping on the transmittance in both cases, when the thermochromic layer is in LT phase and when it is in the HT phase, is rather weak. Correspondingly, the luminous transmittance increases from 41% without to 50% with 50 nm TiO2 capping in the LT case and from 40% to 48% in the HT case. At the same time, ΔT2500 and ΔTsol are only marginally reduced by 2% and 0.4%, respectively. However, the newly introduced, IR-sensitive key parameter ΔT>800 shows the undesired impact of the TiO2 capping anticipated on the basis of the simulations. Its value drops from 8% to 6%, but the effect is less than the theoretical prediction.
    The corresponding reflectance measurements in Figure 4b, in particular, for the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum, are in accordance with the simulations in Figure 3i. Especially, the predicted minimum in reflectance at 550 nm is clearly developed in the reflectance spectra of the trilayer structure (dashed lines) in the HT and LT case compared with those of the sample without AR coating (solid lines). This reduction in reflectance goes hand in hand with the increase in transmittance in this spectral range and, consequently, the enhanced luminous transmittance. In contrast, we observe a strong discrepancy between simulation and experiment for λ > 1000 nm; that is, the impact of the TiO2 capping on the IR key parameters is less adverse in the experiment than predicted by the simulations. This also is reflected in the overall positive impact on the corresponding key parameters set by the application as a thermochromic smart window.     Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Trilayer structures of V1–x–ySrxWyO2 thin films embedded between a rutile TiO2 buffer and a TiO2 capping are grown on quartz substrates, and their potential as coatings for thermochromic smart windows is assessed. Variation of the layer thicknesses of the three layers of the trilayer structure adds additional degrees of freedom for tuning key parameters set by the application in smart windows. In particular, the tunability of the trilayer offers handles to overcome the otherwise tight adverse correlation between the three optical parameters Tlum, Tsol, and ΔTsol, which hinders a simultaneous fulfillment of the set specifications. The trilayer structure has no impact on the phase transition temperature ϑc, which is determined solely by the stoichiometry of the quaternary thermochromic material in our structures. The so far best combination found for buffer and thermochromic layer consists of a 45 nm V1–x–ySrxWyO2 layer on a 15 nm rutile TiO2 buffer. The transmittance modulation in the IR range, which determines ΔTsol, is largest in this case. A 50 nm TiO2 capping layer serves as antireflection coating in the visible range of the electromagnetic spectrum and enhances Tlum significantly by only reducing ΔTsol marginally. The optical design is constructive but needs to be optimized carefully as it affects the optical response in the visible as well as the IR spectral range of interest. When embedding the thermochromic V1–x–ySrxWyO2 layer into a trilayer structure, a range of Sr and W contents exists for which the corresponding coating will fulfill the requirements set by the smart window application. The interplay of stoichiometry and optical design of the surrounding layers still offers room for additional improvement. For example, a better understanding of the interplay of Sr content x and AR coating in the visible has the potential to further enhance the value of Tlum while keeping ΔTsol well above its specified limit. Furthermore, such trilayer coatings will need to be optimized in the future for the use on large surfaces on a mass product scale. This typically requires a trade-off between performance, economical production, and ecological aspects. Almost all the chemical elements making up the suggested trilayer coating can be considered as noncritical in terms of their abundance, in particular, taking into account that Mg and F may serve as substitutes for Sr and W, respectively. A major remaining technological challenge is the transfer of the layer growth onto large-scale square-meter-sized substrates as the sputtering methods as well as the substrate ─ float glass instead of quartz glass ─ employed will be different. The homogeneity of the layers, in particular, in terms of the composition of the quaternary material as well as the layer thicknesses will have to be guaranteed to avoid spatial variations of the color impression of the glazing obtained. Furthermore, it is desirable to reduce the growth temperatures to save production costs. However, lower growth temperatures will affect the structural quality, which in turn will affect the thermochromic performance. This comprises quite a list of technological challenges which will need to be considered and overcome in the future. Thus, technological issues remain, but from a physical point of view, it can be stated that the suggested coatings redeem the promise of realizing thermochromic smart windows based on quaternary V1–x–ySrxWyO2.
