    Abstract

    A series of D−π–A+ pyridinium compounds, in which D = −NPh2 and A+ = −PyMe+ are linked by various amounts of linear phenyl spacers, were strategically designed and synthesized. Their characterization revealed the presence of excited-state intramolecular charge transfer (ESICT) that triggers a corresponding response from the counterion. In medium and strong polar solvents, the fast solvent relaxation occurring after ESICT overwhelms the counterion effect, showing typical emission solvatochromism. In weakly polar solvents, ESICT induces counteranion migration for electrostatic stabilization, the time scale of which is dependent on the radius of the counteranion, the length of the π-linker, and the viscosity of the solvent. In low-viscosity organic solvents such as toluene, counteranion migration occurs within several tens to hundreds of picoseconds, resulting in a time-dependent continuous emission that can be resolved from the spectral temporal evolution. Concrete evidence for this is provided by the chemical synthesis of a D−π–A+ pyridinium–sulfur trioxide– zwitterion, where anion migration is restricted due to its internally locked ion pair. As a result, only a single emission band can be observed. These comprehensive studies prove that the ion migration process may be significant for a wide range of ESICT-type ionic fluorophores. Such an ionic movement, triggered by optically pumped ESICT of the D−π–A+ dyad, is similar to the molecular machine driven by the redox reaction, but with a facile access and fast response.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Artificial molecular machines/motors function by mimicking the naturally evolved biological function. Further, they have attracted considerable attention due to their immense potential in various applications. (1−6) This has promoted a rapid development of a variety of systems capable of undergoing molecular motion in response to external impulses and stimuli. In chemistry, the ability to harness mechanical motion at the molecular level has triggered a boom in the design of smart molecules. Examples of prototype molecular machines are the Stoddart’s dumbbell-shaped systems known as rotaxanes. They consist of a molecular macrocycle as a wheel that is mechanically interlocked onto the axle of the molecules. (7−11) Multiple recognition sites can be designed along the axle of dumbbell-shaped molecules, allowing the macrocycle to travel between these stations. (12−14)
    Various external stimuli such as acid–base reactions, (11,15−18) cation recognition, (19−21) redox reaction, (22−26) and photoexcitation (14,27−32) have been employed as driving forces to break the equilibrium temporally and drive the molecular motion in the designated direction. The redox reaction, which is the most common reaction, generates a potential gradient that acts as the driving force for the molecular motion. Inducing such molecular motion is useful, but it is inevitably influenced by the formation of byproducts. Further, the response to the redox process is relatively slow, (30) thus complicating the fulfilment of the criterion of switching response time. Alternatively, a photon-activated switch provides a fast response time and may not involve the formation of byproducts. This also enables the conduction of a kinetics study by means of the time-resolved probe, helping us to gain more insight into the associated mechanism. (14,33−39)
    Most of these approaches require a sophisticated molecular architecture and an advanced synthesis process, which may hamper further development. The current study on a series of cationic type electron donor (D)-bridge-acceptor (A+) molecules may provide an alternative pathway. These systems consist of a common diphenyl amino group as the electron donor and a phosphonium moiety as the acceptor linked by various numbers of phenyl π-conjugated bridges. The cationic moiety is charge-balanced by a counteranion. (35) Upon electronic excitation, these D−π–A+ systems undergo the expected excited-state intramolecular charge transfer (ESICT) from the diphenyl amino group to the phosphonium moiety via the π-bridge, producing regular emission solvatochromism in medium and strong polar solvents. However, in weak polar solvents such as toluene, exceedingly broad and even dual emission bands were observed. The lack of solvent stabilization in toluene led us to tentatively propose an ESICT that triggers the counteranion migration toward the Dδ+ site (the diphenyl amino group) for further stabilization. (35) The trajectory of photoinduced anion migration has been scarcely reported previously. (40,41) In addition, a number of supramolecular and receptor systems exhibiting pH-, redox-, or photoinduced metal cation translocation with similar mechanisms have been developed over the past decades. (31,32,42−44) However, ion migration driven by intramolecular charge transfer has not received sufficient recognition for paving a facile avenue to enable a fast and conspicuous optical response (e.g., dual fluorescence) in chemically simple donor–acceptor species. (45)
    While the unification of the theorem for counterion-triggered luminescence is imperative, two important themes need to be addressed. First, a general proof for the validity of ion motion in cationic D−π–A+ systems is required. Second, if the D−π–A+ cation and its counteranion can be confined by chemical bonds that restrict ion migration, the corresponding photophysical properties may prove the proposed mechanism. The first point relies on the cationic D−π–A+ system, where both D and A+ are conventional organic chromophores. While the most popular electron donor (D) belongs to the dialkyl amino groups, the pyridinium moiety is one of the ubiquitous cationic acceptors in chemistry. (46,47) In this study, a series of D−π–A+N-methylpyridinium salts (Scheme 1), in which D = −NPh2 and A+ = −PyMe+ are linked by different numbers of linear phenyl spacers, were successfully synthesized and characterized. In addition, different counteranions (Scheme 1) are incorporated into this system to fine-tune the dynamics of anion motion, if any. (35) Importantly, the neutral form of the acceptor, namely, the pyridine ring (A = −Py) with D = −NPh2 forms a D−π–A system, which was synthesized and further underwent the ESICT process (vide infra). (48) Thus, a fair comparison can be made between NPh2–π–PyMe+ and NPh2–π–Py systems to highlight the difference of the former regarding the counterion migration. The comprehensive studies herein pave a simple and generalized way to design D−π–A+ ionic composites capable of optically triggered molecular motion. The results and discussion are elaborated in the subsequent sections.

    The synthetic routes for neutral D–A, D−π–A and cationic D–A+, D−π–A+N-methylpyridinium salts, specified as N0, N1, N2 and C0[X], C1[X], C2[X], respectively, are illustrated in Schemes 1 and S1. Here, capital N and C denote neutral and cation moieties, respectively. Arabic numerals 0, 1, and 2 represent the number of phenyl linkers, and [X] denotes the counteranion. In brief, compound N0 was prepared from 4-bromopyridinium hydrochloride reacted with 1.2 equiv of diphenylamine under the standard Buchwald amination conditions (see Scheme S1). (49) Subsequently, N0 was reacted with methyl iodide (CH3I) at room temperature to afford the N-methyl iodide salt C0[I] in a good yield of 61%. (47) As for the counteranion substitution, C0[I] was stirred with three potassium salts (K[X]) to proceed with the ion-exchange, giving the desirable pure product C0[X] in 43–55% yield. (50) N1 and N2 were synthesized from their corresponding precursors (see Scheme 1b) by the Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling reaction with 4-pyridylboronic acid catalyzed by a heterogeneous Pd(PPh3)4 catalyst. (51,52) Subsequently, N1 and N2 were reacted with CH3I, followed by the above-mentioned ion-exchange reaction with K[X] (K: potassium), giving the products C1[X] and C2[X], respectively. Apart from C0–2[X], zwitterions (denoted as Z) Z1Bs and Z2Bs were also synthesized for comparison (Scheme 1c). As shown in Scheme 1c, the zwitterionic dyes Z1Bs and Z2Bs can be obtained by reacting N1 and N2 with 1,4-butane sultone (Bs) in good yields. (53) However, to overcome the poor solubility of Z2Bs in the nonpolar or weak polar environment, we incorporated tert-butyl groups, forming t-Z2Bs (Scheme 1c), which possess high steric hindrance. Further, they suppress intermolecular self-aggregation in solution to increase the solubility. t-Z2Bs were synthesized by reacting t-N1 with 1,4-butane sultone (Bs) (see Schemes 1c, S4, and S6). The structures of all intermediates and products have been well characterized by NMR (Figures S1–S48), X-ray crystallography analyses (Figures S49–S52 and Tables S1–S3), and mass spectroscopy (Figures S53–S59). The definitive structural assignments of representative C1[OTf] and C2[OTf] were verified by X-ray diffraction (XRD) of crystals grown from a CH2Cl2/hexane mixture. The XRD structures shown in Figures 1 and S49–S52 confirm the methylation on the pyridine −N atom. This, along with the existence of a counterion, affirms the formation of the pyridinium moiety.
    The steady-state absorption and emission spectra of N0, N1, N2 and C0[BPhCl], C1[BPhCl], C0[BPhCl] salts in toluene, dichloromethane, and acetonitrile are shown in Figure 2. The photophysical data are summarized in Tables 1 and S4. The lowest-lying absorption bands for N0 are located at 291 nm with the onset at ca. 340 nm in toluene (Figure 2a). The absorption peaks for N1 and N2 are red-shifted to 345 and 351 nm in toluene (Figure 2b,c), respectively, which can be attributed to the elongation of π-conjugation, partially due to the additional phenylene spacers. (54,55) The lowest-lying absorption band of C0[BPhCl] (pyridinium compounds) shifted to 309 nm with a slightly red-shifted onset at ca. 380 nm in toluene, as compared to N0 (Figure 2a,d). Similarly, the lowest-lying absorption bands in toluene of C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] shifted to 452 and 462 nm bathochromically, as compared with N1 and N2, respectively (see Figure 2b–e). This bathochromic shift in the absorption wavelength between neutral and ionic compounds is attributed to enhancement of the acceptor ability. This enhancement lowers the energy level of LUMO with a minor effect on the HOMO. Thus, it decreases the energy gap between the S1 and S0 states (see Figures S60, 61 and Tables S5, S6 for the computational approaches). (56) The relatively small absorption coefficient of the lowest-lying absorption peak (Figure 1 and Table 1) and lack of vibronic progression imply that the lowest-lying absorption bands, in part, possess a charge-transfer (CT) character. Time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) computation also shows that the lowest lying transition is attributed to the charge transfer from the diphenyl amino (DPA) moiety to the pyridinium, which supports the above-mentioned hypothesis (see Figure S61). Note that C1[BPhCl] possesses a larger absorption coefficient than C2[BPhCl] in all solvents. This can be attributed to the stronger orbital overlap between HOMO and LUMO, allowing more S0 → S1 transition and, hence, a qualitatively larger absorption coefficient. This aspect is supported by the computed S0 → S1 oscillator strength, where the value of C1+ is calculated to be larger than that of C2+ (see Table 1). For C1[BPhCl], shown in Figure 2, the absorption peak wavelength (λab) is red-shifted from toluene (452 nm) to medium polar CH2Cl2 (465 nm). However, it is then blue-shifted back to the shortest wavelength in a highly polar solvent, that is, CH3CN (421 nm). C2[BPhCl] also exhibits similar absorption solvatochromism as the solvent polarity increases. This is in sharp contrast to their neutral counterparts N1 and N2, where the absorption spectra are nearly solvent independent of the three solvents under study. For the ionic D−π–A+ chromophore, the irregular solvent polarity-dependent absorption spectra are not unusual and can be attributed to the alternation of electrophilicity between donor and ionic acceptor in response to the different solvent polarity. (57,58) This was corroborated in a recent computational study. (59)

    Upon photoexcitation, N0, N1, and N2 exhibited a single, structureless emission band, which undergoes regular solvatochromism. It is red-shifted with increasing solvent polarity from toluene to CH3CN (see Figure 2a-c). The results indicate the charge-transfer (CT) property in the first excited (S1) state, that is, the shift of electron density from DPA to the pyridine moiety. This leads to a large change of dipole moment versus the ground state. As a result, a red-shifted emission occurred in more polar solvents due to solvent stabilization (see Figure S60). As shown in Figure 2d–f, a similar trend of the solvatochromic red shift in the emission profile can be observed for C0[BPhCl], C1[BPhCl], and C2[BPhCl] from the medium polar solvent, CH2Cl2, to a more polar solvent CH3CN. However, drastically different emission spectra were observed in weak polar solvents such as toluene (see Figure 2d–f), where two aspects dominate: 1. The Stokes shift for C0[BPhCl] (∼16786 cm–1 absorption peak versus emission peak) is twice that for N0 (∼7554 cm–1 in toluene) (Figure 2a,d and Table S4); 2. For C1[BPhCl] in toluene, the Stokes shift and the full width at half-maximum (fwhm) of the emission band are significantly larger than those of the neutral counterpart N1 (cf. Figure 2b and e). This becomes very obvious in C2[BPhCl], where the emission becomes exceedingly broad and two maxima appear (Figure 2f). A qualitative dual-band fitting gives two peaks at ∼623 nm and ∼750 nm, specified as F1 and F2, respectively (see Figure 2f). The excitation spectra of two emission bands are identical, and they are also similar to the absorption profile. It implies that the two emission bands share the same ground-state origin (see Figure S62). Therefore, the anomalous steady-state emission spectra for the Cn[BPhCl] system in toluene may be due to the photoinduced phenomena, most plausibly the structural transformation in the electronically excited state. The absorption and emission spectra of the target compounds in the solid state are depicted in Figure S63. As shown in Figure S63A, the emission of C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] in the solid state exhibits a normal emission band with a much smaller fwhm (see Figure S63A) than that in toluene. Hence, the anomalous emission is only observed in solution (vide supra).
    To gain further insight, time-resolved measurements were performed using a time-correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) technique. It was coupled with multichannel plates and femtosecond (∼150 fs) excitation pulses and resulted in a temporal resolution of ∼15 ps. The time-resolved data are summarized in Tables 1 and S4. Independent of the solvents used, all Nn (n = 0, 1, 2) systems and C0[BPhCl] exhibited one decay time constant of several nanoseconds at the corresponding emission maxima (see Table S4). The rates of solvent relaxation in the three low-viscosity solvents studied are within 1–3 ps. (60,61) It could not be resolved in this study. Similarly, both C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] salts exhibit only one decay component in high polarity solvents such as CH2Cl2 and CH3CN (Table 1). However, a distinct difference is seen in toluene where two decay time constants of 86 ± 5.0 and 582 ± 5.0 ps for C1[BPhCl] salts could be well resolved in the short wavelength region of ∼570 nm. At a longer emission wavelength of ∼720 nm, the faster 86 ps decay component nearly disappears. Instead, a rise component of ∼102 ± 4.0 ps can be resolved, followed by a population decay of 562 ± 3.0 ps (see Table 1 and Figure S64). Similar results were obtained for C2[BPhCl], which clearly exhibited dual emission in toluene. At the F1 band at ∼570 nm, a fluorescence decay time constant of 110 ± 5.0 ps was resolved. At the F2 band at ∼740 nm, the relaxation dynamics consist of a rise component of 130 ± 3.0 ps. This is followed by a slow population decay of 441 ± 5.0 ps (Table 1 and Figure S64). Evidently, the complex decay dynamics for C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] in toluene are considerably different from those of the single exponential decay component for the fluorescence of their neutral reference compounds N1 and N2. The observation of a rise component is reminiscent of the precursor-successor kinetic model. However, the fast decay time observed at 570 nm (for C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl]) did not exactly match the rise time at 720 nm (C1[BPhCl]) or 740 nm (C2[BPhCl]). Therefore, instead of a two-state (precursor-successor) relaxation kinetics, it is more plausible to propose a type of continuous relaxation dynamics. The solvent (toluene) relaxation, although a continuous event, can be eliminated owing to its rather weak polarity. In addition, the relaxation time of toluene of ∼5.6 ps (62) cannot account for the ≫80 ps relaxation time constants observed in this study.
    We then measured the fluorescence relaxation dynamics of C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] at different wavelengths. They were further integrated to obtain wavelength-dependent intensity at every specific time delay. A spectral temporal evolution for C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] in toluene was constructed (Figure 3). The temporal evolution spectra of C1[BPhCl] (Figure 3a and b) show a continuous relaxation process rather than a spectrum consisting of two distinct emissive states. This implies that multiple emissive species may exist during the relaxation process. It is also evidenced by the lack of an iso-emissive point (Figure 3b). Similar spectral temporal evolution was observed for C2[BPhCl] (see Figure 3c and 3d). These results are akin to the solvent relaxation process in polar solvents, resulting in emission solvatochromism. However, the difference lies in the fact that the overall relaxation times, which are 86 and 110 ps for C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl], respectively are much slower than the solvent relaxation time of 5.6 ps in toluene (62) (vide supra).
    The continuous relaxation process may originate from a large conformation change in the excited state, such as excited-state twisted intramolecular charge transfer (TICT) (63,64) or harpooning effect (65,66) for D–A compounds. However, it requires a semiflexible bridge, such as cyclohexyl linking D with A, for a harpooning mechanism. This is different from our case. As a result, the harpooning mechanism can be excluded. To check the possibility of the TICT mechanism, we first used DFT and TDDFT approaches for the target cationic chromophores in an attempt to obtain the geometry information at S0 and S1 states. In this approach, the torsion angle between the DPA moiety and pyridinium group is defined as the angle between the Ph–N–Ph plane (see Figure 4 highlighted in red) and pyridinium plane (highlighted in blue), denoted as θr–b. As shown in Figure 4a, θr–b for C0+ is estimated to be 12.8° at the optimized ground-state geometry. Photoexcitation led to the torsion angle θr–b drastically increasing to 89.7° at the optimized S1 geometry. As a result, the anomalously large Stokes shift for C0[BPhCl] may be rationalized by the occurrence of TICT. Further, the emission band maximized at 640 nm in toluene can be assigned to the charge transfer band associated with TICT. (63,64)
    θr–b for C1+ and C2+ can be derived from several torsion angles (θ1, θ2, and θ3) shown in Figure 4b and c, which are calculated to be 43.1° and 38.5° at the ground state for C1+ and C2+, respectively. Photoexcitation led to θr–b values of 71.5° and 42.3° for C1+ and C2+, respectively, in the optimized S1 state. In sharp contrast to those of C0+, the optimized S0 and S1 geometries C2+ have no major structural modifications (Figure 4c). As a result, the observed dual emission (see Figure 2) should not arise from TICT. In addition, the computed emission wavelength for C2+ is located at 534 nm (see Table 1). This matches the F1 emission band well but is far shorter than the F2 band. This supports the statement that the conformational change in cationic chromophore has an insignificant effect on the emission property for C2+. C1+ exhibits moderate conformational change between optimized S0 and S1 (Figure 4b). It increases the torsion angle between the DPA moiety and phenyl linkage (θ1) from 18.8° to 56.5° and θr–b from 43.1° to 71.5°. Therefore, structural changes affecting the photophysical properties of C1+ salts may be considered. In fact, several similar structures with the pyridinium moiety have been reported to occur in TICT. (67−69) For example, the TICT time constant for a compound named ref. (see Scheme 1c) was reported to be <5 ps and competitive with solvent relaxation. (69) It is much faster than the observed continuous relaxation process (≫80 ps), observed in this study. Therefore, the TICT process contribution toward the observed slow spectral temporal evolution for C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] can be neglected.
    Further experimental evidence to eliminate the TICT responsible for the anomalous emission of C1+ and C2+ in toluene is provided by their distinct counteranion-dependent emission features and corresponding relaxation dynamics. Figure 5 shows the emission intensity ratio for the F2 versus F1 band, that is, F2/F1 in toluene, which reduces drastically from C2[BPhCl] to C2[OTf] and C2[PF6] (Figure 5). This trend is consistent with the relaxation rate (measured by the decay of the F1 band), being in the order of C2[BPhCl] (110 ps)−1 < C2[PF6] (94 ps)−1–C2[OTf] (86 ps)−1 (Table S7 and Figure S65). Qualitatively, the result may correlate with the size of the anion, which is in the order of BPhCl– (6.22 Å) > OTf– (2.48 Å) ∼ PF6– (2.42 Å). (35) Similar results were observed for C1+, and the relaxation rate was in the order of C1[BPhCl] (86 ps)−1 < C1[OTf] (53 ps)−1 < C1[PF6] (40 ps)−1 (Table S7 and Figure S66). The correlation of the excited state dynamics only with the size of the anion is undoubtedly an oversimplified approach as the ion pairing behavior might depend on other factors such as the interaction of anion with the nearest solvent molecules, anion shape, the distribution of electrostatic potential, and mutual anion–cation disposition. Nevertheless, the combination of these considerations clearly indicates that the excited-state relaxation rate for C1+ and C2– is strongly counterion-dependent in toluene.
    The above approaches are all in low-polarity solvents such as toluene, where solvent stabilization does not play a major role in the relaxation dynamics. In high-polarity solvents such as CH2Cl2 and CH3CN, the anion and cation are, respectively, solvated and stabilized by the surrounding solvent molecules. This separates the anions and cations rather than promote ion-pair formation. After forming the ICT state, the nearest shell solvent molecules would undergo a fast reorganization process, governed by dipole–dipole interaction for stabilization.
    This solvent relaxation process converts the Franck–Condon state into a dipolar relaxed state within a few picoseconds in a low-viscosity solvent (see Scheme 2a). To verify this, a femtosecond fluorescence up-conversion study was carried out for both C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] inCH3CN, for which ultrafast decay time constants of 0.28 and 0.63 ps were resolved at the onset of the emission at 620 and 650 nm, respectively (see Figure S67), supporting the fast solvation relaxation dynamics in CH3CN.
    In toluene, C1+ and C2+ are subject to poor dipole–dipole interactions with solvent molecules but have appreciable electrostatic interactions with their counteranions. Hence, the ion-pair configuration exists dominantly in toluene. This, along with the continuous relaxation pattern and the strongly counterion-dependent property, allows us to generalize the mechanism of charge-transfer induced anion migration to the target ionic D−π–A+ system. (35) ESICT leads to the distribution of the electron density toward the ionic acceptor A+ site and leaves a positive charge, in part, at the D site. This, along with the neutralization of A+ (which weakens the A+[X]− ion pair), renders the driving force to execute the anion [X]− migration for stabilization (Scheme 2b). Consequently, the fast decay rate at the F1 band for both C1[X] and C2[X] can be ascribed to the initial rate of ion migration. Unlike the solvent relaxation process, ion migration, due to its electrostatic interaction, is slow enough so that the radiaive decay can compete during the process. As a result, multiple emissions were observed, leading to the continuous time-resolved spectra (Figure 3). The enhanced relaxation rate with decreasing anion size may be because a smaller anion surrounded by toluene molecules leads to better solvation. It decreases the electrostatic interaction with the cation core, resulting in a faster transport rate. Moreover, the time-resolved results show that the decay time of C1[BPhCl] at the emission onset (e.g., 540–570 nm) is faster than that of C2[BPhCl] (see Table 1). This result indicates that a longer phenyl spacer results in a slower ion migration rate, probably due to a greater D–A length. The length reduces the Coulombic attraction between the counteranion and donor group (DPA), thereby decreasing the driving force for migration (Scheme 2b).
    Based on this mechanism, such a large ion motion must be influenced by the solvent viscosity, and it must decrease with increasing viscosity. In a designated experiment, we titrated different quantities of p-dioxane, which has almost the same polarity as toluene but larger viscosities (2.2 cP) (cf. 0.59 cP for toluene at 298 K), into a C2[BPhCl]-containing toluene solution. As shown in Figure 6, the intensity of F2/F1 decreases with increasing molar ratio of p-dioxane, supporting the proposed mechanism.
    We then made further attempts to affirm the above mechanism via computational and chemical approaches. In the theoretical approach, we chose C2[OTf] as a prototype because C2+ is less prone to structural variation in the excited state (vide supra) and OTf– is smaller in size than BPhCl–, for the convenience of calculation. The optimization of S1 and S1′ state geometry for C2[OTf] was then performed using the TDDFT under the M062X/6-31+G(d,p) (70,71) level in combination with a polarizable continuum solvation model (PCM) (72) in toluene. In the ground state (S0), we intentionally adjusted OTf– at the proximity of C2+ and optimized the ion-pair structure in a toluene environment. We then executed the Franck–Condon excitation (vertical transition), followed by further optimization to obtain the S1 state. At this stage, the structure of core C2+ and its distance/orientation with respect to OTf– remained similar to that of the ground state. On the basis of this optimized S1 state, we then artificially moved the OTf– anion to the donor (diphenyl amine) site and optimized the ion pair to obtain the minimum energy state S1′. Figure 7 shows the HOMO and LUMO as well as the energy gap between the HOMO and LUMO for S1 and S1′. Apparently, charge transfer takes place for C2[OTf], as evidenced by the HOMO and LUMO located at diphenyl amine and pyridinium moieties, respectively. The energies for S1 and S1′ calculated qualitatively at 458 and 686 nm, respectively, are consistent with the emission onset of ∼500 and ∼650 nm, respectively (see Figures 5 and S68).
    Chemically, restricting the anion motion is expected to prohibit the migration behavior, which would change the photophysical properties drastically. Hence, compounds with a sulfonic anion linked to the N atom on pyridine by an alkyl chain were synthesized and are named as Z1Bs and Z2Bs (see Scheme 1c). Unfortunately, both Z1Bs and Z2Bs exhibit poor solubility in toluene, especially Z2Bs, which are nearly insoluble in this solvent. To avoid aggregation, the analogue t-Z2Bs were also synthesized for reaffirmation. Here, tert-butyl was introduced to increase the solubility (see Scheme 1c, vide supra). The emission spectra of Z1Bs and t-Z2Bs in toluene are shown in Figure 8a and b, respectively. For comparison, the absorption and emission spectra of C1[OTf] and t-C2[OTf] are also shown in Figure 8. They are much longer than that of all C1[X] and C2[X] salts at a short-wavelength (F1 band) region (Figure S69 and Table S8).     Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, a series of linear D−π–A pyridinium salts of C0–2[X] were synthesized and investigated. C0–2[BPhCl] salts exhibit a conspicuous solvatochromic effect, attributed to the nature of the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) state. In toluene, C0[BPhCl] without a phenyl bridge exhibits anomalously large Stokes-shifted emission, probably due to TICT. The possibility of anion migration cannot be resolved due to the much shorter D–A distance. Upon adding phenylene spacers, ESICT in the case of C1[BPhCl] and C2[BPhCl] in toluene is accompanied by anion migration for electrostatic stabilization. Such intraion pair motion is approximately 2 orders of magnitude slower than that of polar, low viscous solvent relaxation, resulting in multiple emissions. The proposed mechanism has been rigorously verified by time-resolved spectroscopy, theoretical studies, and, importantly, a chemical approach. In the chemical approach, the intramolecular ion pairs Z1Bs and t-Z2Bs were synthesized and were seen to lack anion migration due to structural restrictions. The rate of ion motion decreases with an increase in the anion size and the length of the π-bridge. This likely establishes a general methodology to harness the switching time by optically triggering the ESICT of ionic D−π–A+ dyads. At this stage, it might be premature to coin these dynamic ion pairs as molecular machines in the classical sense. (44) Even so, the fast, reversible, and distinct photophysical signal associated with photoinduced counterion migration (translocation) is a unique phenomenon. It could provide a basis for unconventional optical functionalities. The ion size-, temperature-, and viscosity-dependent dual luminescence properties offer versatile possibilities for the design of sensing and stimuli-responsive systems. This is pertinent if the systems are realized within a confined space (e.g., ionic cages or frameworks). It is also true for two-chromophore salts (with both cationic and anionic emissive components). Thus, we believe that this study paves another avenue for constructing simple and fast photoinduced molecular motion and machines.

The needs for future development comprise: (i) confirmation of the validity of the QY values of many commonly used std. materials, preferably in the form of SI traceable stds.; (ii) extension of the set of std. materials to the UV and near-IR spectral ranges; and (iii) good stds. or robust protocols for the measurements of low QYs.
