      Abstract

      The reaction rates for the mononuclear rearrangement of the Z-phenylhydrazones of 3-acyl-1,2,4-oxadiazoles 3a–c into the relevant 2-phenyl-2H-1,2,3-triazoles (4a–c) have been measured in dioxane/water at different temperatures in a large range of proton concentrations. The occurrence of two different reaction pathways (one uncatalyzed, water assisted, and the other general base catalyzed) has- been observed. The obtained results have been able to furnish information about the effects of the nature of the 3-acyl structure and of the 5-substituents in the 1,2,4-oxadiazole ring on the reactivity of the examined rearrangements: they are well in line with the previsions carried out considering some our previous computational results as well as experimental kinetic ones.

      Nitrogen-containing penta- and hexa-atomic heterocyclic compounds represent an important class of compounds. (1) They play a fundamental role in organic, medicinal, and biological chemistry: in fact, they are involved in several organic syntheses, are present in important drugs, and are used in the study of different reaction mechanisms (for example, in ANRORC as well as in azole-into-azole interconversions). Moreover, they give nanomaterials and participate in fundamental biological processes (from chlorophyllian synthesis to hemoglobin oxygen transfer, to DNA/RNA structure and processes, and so on). Therefore, the Beketov definition of heterocycles as “jewelry rings studded with precious stones” appears largely justified, in line with the fact that heterocycles constitute the most common motif in several drugs and bioactive compounds. On the whole, we can say that the study of synthesis and reactivity of nitrogen-containing heterocyclic compounds has been at the basis of the organic chemistry in the last decades.
      Along this line, we have turned our attention from the study of SNAr in halogenonitrothiophenes (2) to the examination of nitrogen-containing heterocycles looking at the synthesis, the reactivity, and the biological (pharmacological) activity of five- and six-membered rings (eventually containing other heteroatoms, such as oxygen or sulfur, in the ring).
      Special interest has been devoted by us to synthetic applications of the Cusmano–Ruccia reactions (3) also applied to condensed nitrosoimidazoles, (4) thus obtaining compounds with different biological properties, from moderate activity versus cells of colon cancer, melanoma, and breast cancer (5) to L-type calcium channel (LTCC) blocking activity (in some cases comparable to, or higher than, that of diltiazem). (6)
      Considering that some literature data indicate that L-type calcium channel blockers (e.g., verapamil, diltiazem, and nifedipine) can affect MDR1 activity (thus increasing the efficacy of antitumor chemotherapy), (7) we have tested several of these compounds to gain information about their “ability” in this field. We have observed that they are able to affect MDR1 activity, significantly augmenting the efficacy of antitumor chemotherapy. (8)
      Finally, we have focused our attention on synthetic applications and mechanistic study of the “mononuclear rearrangements of heterocycles” (MRH) (9) and to the synthesis of derivatives of condensed heterocycles containing several heteroatoms (nitrogen, oxygen, and sulfur), deeply investigating their biological activities (as antimicrobial, (10a) anticonvulsive, and psychotropic agents). (10b)
      MRH represents a rearrangement (Scheme 1) studied a long time ago by some Italian researchers (11) and about 50 years ago classified by Boulton and Katritzky (12) and, therefore, usually named the Boulton–Katritzky reactions (BKR). They play an important role from a synthetic point of view in being able to give several penta-atomic heterocyclic rings containing nitrogen: (9a) after the submission of the current paper, a Russian group published another application of BKR. (12b)
      In the field of the mechanistic studies of BKR we have focused our attention on the kinetic study of the rearrangements of several substituted Z-arylhydrazones of 3-benzoyl-1,2,4-oxadiazoles (9,13,14)3 into the relevant 2-aryl-4-benzoylamino-2H-1,2,3-triazoles 4 (see Scheme 2) under different experimental conditions (in dioxane/water at different pS+, in benzene, or in toluene in the presence of different bases). We have also studied the reactivity of some isoxazole derivatives, (15) and we have observed that the lower aromaticity of 1,2,4-oxadiazole (Bird’s index IA 48) (16) with respect to isoxazole (IA 52) makes its reactivity higher. We like to call 1,2,4-oxadiazole a “magic” heterocycle: in fact, because of its very low aromatic character it can behave as a masked functionality (17) (for example, it can furnish ester and amide groups) and its derivatives can easily rearrange to give other more aromatic/stable heterocycles (see BKR above, 2H-1,2,3-triazoles, IA 109) or providing examples of ANRORC processes. (18) On the other hand, the presence of a 1,2,4-oxadiazole group in a drug can represent a hydrolysis-resistant bioisoster of an ester or of an amide functionality. (19)
      The kinetic results obtained by studying the mononuclear rearrangement of compounds 3 have allowed us to conclude that it is an SNi process occurring via a quasi-aromatic transition state involving 10 π-electrons in a bicyclic structure: (20) it can occur via different pathways (base-catalyzed, specific or general, uncatalyzed, and acid-catalyzed, specific) (9,21) as a function of the structure of the starting substrate, of the used solvent, and generally speaking, of the experimental conditions. We have studied the kinetic behavior of several Z-arylhydrazones of 3-benzoyl-1,2,4-oxadiazoles under different experimental conditions (in dioxane/water at different proton concentrations, in benzene, or in toluene in the presence of different amines or of organic acids). (9,21) We have also evidenced a possible acid-catalyzed pathway exerted by copper salts in methanol. (21e)
      Looking at the structure of the rearranged Z-arylhydrazones 3, one can see that they can be “shared” in two parts, where A represents the nucleophilic side of the substrate and B represents the electrophilic counterpart in the SNi process.
      It is evident that the ability of Nα (the nucleophilic center) to behave as a nucleophile essentially depends on the nature of the substituents present in the aryl group (13a) as well as on the nature of the R part, which can affect the electronic density on Nα. Of course, the nature of the far substituent G can hardly/scarcely affect the nucleophilicity of Nα.
      Naturally, the electrophilic character of the N-2 atom of the 1,2,4-oxadiazole ring (the electrophilic center) is affected by the nature of G (in this case, the nature of R and of the substituents in the aryl part of the Z-arylhydrazones should not be able to significantly affect the electrophilicity of N-2). G substituents at C-5 can influence the reactivity in BKR in different manners: (a) an aryl can cause a diaryloid effect (thus affecting/increasing the stability of the starting partner and then lowering its reactivity); (22) (b) an electron-donating or electron-withdrawing substituent G (present in a quasi-para position) would lower or increase the electrophilic character of the N-2 atom, thus influencing the reactivity and also affecting the 1,2,4-oxadiazole ring stability/reactivity; (23) and moreover, (c) an amino group (G = NH2, NHCH3, N(CH3)2, NHCOCH3) “can” cause the occurrence of a new reaction pathway in the presence of acids. (21)
      To gain new information about the influence of the nature of the A and B parts of 3 on its reactivity, we have now examined the behavior of compounds 3a–c in dioxane/water in a large range of pS+ and at different temperatures (see below).
      Considering part A of the substrate, we can evaluate the influence of a modification of R (that is the nucleophilic part of the starting substrate) from the previously ones examined where R = C6H5 to the new Z-arylhydrazones where R = H, CH3 (3a,b).
      Looking at part B of the substrate, we have now examined the reactivity of 3c (G = CH2 Cl; R = C6 H5) to see how a weak electron-withdrawing substituent [(σp)CH2Cl = +0.12] (24) can affect the electrophilic character of N-2 and then the rearrangement rate.

      In Figure 1, the log kA,R for the Z-phenylhydrazones 3a,b vs the pS+ are reported. For the sake of comparison, data concerning the rearrangement of the Z-phenylhydrazone of 3-benzoyl-5-phenyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole (3d) (22) have been included.
      In Figure 2, the log kA,R for the Z-phenylhydrazone 3cvs the pS+ are reported. For comparison, data concerning the rearrangement of the Z-phenylhydrazones of 3-benzoyl-5-methyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole (3e) and of 3-benzoyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole (3f) (23) have been included.
      The data shown in Figures 1 and 2 evidence in every instance the occurrence of the expected two reaction pathways: an uncatalyzed path (water assisted) at lower pS+ and a base-catalyzed path at higher pS+. The shapes of the plots are in line with the occurrence of a general base catalysis, as observed in the rearrangement of several Z-arylhydrazones of 3-benzoyl-5-phenyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole.
      To confirm this statement, we have examined the rearrangement of the Z-phenylhydrazone of 3-formyl-5-phenyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole (3a) in the presence of borate buffer at different concentrations. The obtained values are collected in Table S4 and are summarized in Figure 3: the calculated values of the kinetic constants, differing in function of the buffer concentrations (in the pS+ interval 9.5–11.5, changing the total buffer concentrations from 0.0125 to 0.0500 M leads to an increase of the reactivity in the range 1.8–2.2), confirm the occurrence of a general base catalysis. (25,26)
      Now, looking at the data of Figure 1 we can carry out the following considerations. Starting from the data concerning the rearrangement of the Z-phenylhydrazone of 3-formyl-5-phenyl-1,2,4-oxadiazole (in 1, R = H; 3a) one can see  that changing R to CH3 and C6H5 a variation in the reactivity has been observed in both uncatalyzed and base-catalyzed ranges, larger in the first than in the second. In fact, in the uncatalyzed range, for example, at pS+ 5.0, the reactivity sequence is 3a < 3d < 3b with reactivity ratios of 1:5.3:85. In contrast, in the base-catalyzed one, for example, at pS+ 11.0, the observed (inverted with respect that one observed in the uncatalyzed range) sequence is 3b ≤ 3d < 3a with a significant variation of the reactivity (about 10 at pS+ 11.0) only between 3a and the other two Z-phenylhydrazones (3b ≃ 3d).
      What is the rationale of the above behavior? Why and how do these small structural modifications cause a variation of reactivity, especially large, in the uncatalyzed pathway? We think that phenyl and methyl groups can exert, with respect to hydrogen, a significant electron-repelling effect (compare the σR substituent constants for H, C6H5, and CH3: 0.00, −0.11, and −0.11, respectively): (24) this effect causes an increase in electron density on the Nα atom and then augments its nucleophilicity in the uncatalyzed range. (13a) Of course, in contrast, the increase of the electron density on the Nα atom makes the hydrogen linked at it less prone to be removed by a base: that is, it makes its interaction with a base less easy. Therefore, if the interaction >Nα–H···B becomes less probable, then the base-catalyzed pathway becomes more difficult to occur, (13b) as we have observed.
      In conclusion, we can say that electron-repelling substituents in R increase the nucleophilic character of Nα and then the reactivity in the uncatalyzed range. Of course, the same substituents decrease the acidity of the hydrogen atom linked to Nα and then the reactivity in the base-catalyzed range. We think that we can generalize this result: every R-substituent able by its electronic effects to donate electrons increases the nucleophilic character of Nα (thus augmenting the reactivity in the uncatalyzed pathway of the rearrangement) and at the same time decreases the acidic character of the hydrogen atom linked to it (thus reducing the reactivity in the base-catalyzed pathway of the rearrangement). We can reasonably say that every R-substituent able to attract electrons shall exert an opposite effect.
      The behavior that we have observed by changing the R-substituent is well in line with data collected studying the effect of substituents present in the aryl moiety of Z-arylhydrazones: for example, looking at the uncatalyzed pathway, (13a) an electron-withdrawing substituent (for example a 4-NO2 group) strongly decreases the reactivity (kH/kNO214.9 at pS+ 3.80) while an electron-donating substituent (for example, a 4-OCH3 group) increases the reactivity (kH/kOCH3 0.636 at pS+ 3.80).
      As expected, looking at the base-catalyzed pathway, an opposite effect has been observed: that is, a 4-NO2 group strongly increases the reactivity while an electron-donating substituent (such as a 4-OCH3 group) decreases the reactivity. (13b)
      In addition, the results obtained by examining the rearrangement reactivity of 3c appear very interesting when compared with those observed for the Z-arylhydrazones 3e and 3f. In fact, looking at Figure 2, on going from 3e to 3f and 3c, one can observe a large increase of the reactivity in both the uncatalyzed and base-catalyzed ranges. For example, at pS+ 3.80 and at pS+ 11.0 the reactivity ratios are (kA,R)3e/(kA,R)3f/(kA,R)3c 1:5.75:19.5 and 1:9.3:53.7, respectively. This large growth of the reactivity must be related to the different effect that the substituent present at C-5 of the 1,2,4-oxadiazole ring can exert on the electrophilic character of N-2 of the ring. In fact, on going from 3f (at C-5, G = H) to 3e (at C-5, G = CH3) the introduction of an electron-repelling substituent [(σp)CH3 −0.17] (24) in a quasi-para position causes an increase of the electron density on N-2 and then makes the SNi process less easy. In contrast, on going from 3f (at C-5, G = H) to 3c (at C-5, G = CH2 Cl), the introduction of an electron-attracting substituent [(σp)CH2Cl +0.12] (24) causes a decrease of the electron density on N-2 and then makes the SNi process easier. Of course, the electron-attracting group CH2Cl is also able to increase the polarity of the O-1/N-2 bond and then the leaving group ability (nucleofugacity) of the N-4/C-5/O-1 system: all of these factors are able to increase the rearrangement reactivity in 3c with respect to 3e and 3f.
      The large increase of the reactivity in both reaction pathways on going from 3f to 3c (see Figure 2) appears well in line with the fact that our attempts to synthesize the Z-arylhydrazone of 3-benzoyl-5-trichloromethyl 3g in different experimental conditions failed. In fact, we obtained the triazole 4g deriving from the rearrangement. Considering the value of the substituent constant of the trichloromethyl group [(σp)CCl3 +0.46] (24) and then its effect on the electron density at N-2, one can forecast an increase of the reactivity at least of a factor 104, and this well explains our incapacity to synthesize 3g.
      Now we shall give a short comment on the slopes of the lines concerning the general-base-catalyzed pathway and the break points between the two lines (catalyzed and uncatalyzed pathways).
      As expected, quite similar quasi-unitary slopes have been calculated (s +0.918, +0.907, and +0.913): they are usually scarcely affected by the structure of the rearranged Z-arylhydrazone and then are well in line with those measured for a lot of other Z-arylhydrazones of several and several 3-acyl-1,2,4-oxadiazoles (average value: +0.917 ± 0.009) and once more in line with the expectations for a process occurring via a general-base-catalyzed pathway.
      The pS+ break points at which a changeover of mechanism occurs (from uncatalyzed to base-catalyzed pathways) are, as expected, significantly affected by the structure of the rearranged Z-phenylhydrazone when the structure variations determine significant variations of the electronic density (that is of the nucleophilic character) on the Nα atom. In A, when we change R from H to C6H5 and to CH3, that is we go from 3a to 3d and 3b, as we have seen, we cause a significant increase of the nuclophilicity of the Nα atom and, at the same time, a lowering of the acidity of the hydrogen atom linked to Nα. Therefore, we favor the uncatalyzed pathway and disfavor the base-catalyzed pathway strongly affecting the value of the pS+ break point, which shifts from 6.25 (for 3a) to 7.39 (for 3d) and then to 9.35 (for 3b).
      As we have pointed out previously, the structural variations on part B cause only a small electronic effect on the Z-phenylhydrazone part: as a consequence, we have observed only a modest effect on the values of the breakpoints on going from 3f to 3e and then to 3c (from 7.10, to 7.28, and then to 6.98).
      Finally, we shall give a short comment on thermodynamic parameters. In the base-catalyzed pathway, the measured rate constants are composite values because all of the bases present in the buffer act as catalysts and then the measured apparent kinetic constants represent the sum of all possible catalyzed pathways. Of course, the situation concerning the uncatalyzed path instead is more simple.
      Thus, in the uncatalyzed pathway for all of the examined Z-phenylhydrazones 3a–c both activation enthalpy and entropy stay practically unchanged, showing activation enthalpy quite similar between themselves (ΔH* = 100, 99, and 100 kJ mol–1 for 3a, 3b, and 3c, respectively). The significant reactivity differences between the examined Z-phenylhydrazones 3a–c are essentially activation entropy dependent (ΔS* = −50, −80, and −30 JK mol–1 for 3a, 3b, and 3c, respectively).
      In the base-catalyzed range, the activation enthalpy for each Z-phenylhydrazones 3a–c stays practically unchanged (ΔH* = 82, 87, and 84 kJ mol–1 for 3a, 3b, and 3c, respectively), while the activation entropy largely changes with the pS+ variations (ΔS* = from −50 to −8, from −50 to −12, and from −42 to +3 JK mol–1 for 3a, 3b, and 3c, respectively).

      The results of a kinetic study of the mononuclear rearrangement of the Z-arylhydrazones 3a–c, compared to data previously collected on compounds 3d–f, have been able to gain further information about the effects that substituents present in parts A or B of the Z-arylhydrazones can exert on the rearrangement rates.
      In fact, because the examined reaction occurs via a SNi process, a substituent R in the part A of the Z-arylhydrazone can influence the reactivity “increasing or decreasing” the electronic density and then the nucleophilic character of Nα and the rate of the uncatalyzed (water assisted) pathway. Of course, an opposite effect shall be exerted on the base-catalyzed one by the presence of substituent in A because when the electronic density on Nα is “increased or decreased”, it decreases or increases the possibility for the hydrogen linked at it to interact with the present bases.
      At the same time, a substituent G in part B of the substrate can affect the reactivity “increasing or decreasing” the electronic density and then the electrophilic character of N-2 and its polarity (and also the polarizability of the O-1/N-2 breaking bond of the 1,2,4-oxadiazole ring).
      On the whole, we can say that the results we have now collected are well in line with the previsions that one could make on the basis of our previous theoretical calculations, once more confirming the validity of the computational results obtained by us some years ago. (20) The present kinetic data appear able to give/gain a clearer and clearer picture of the effects that the structural variations can exert on the reaction rates in the Boulton–Katritzky rearrangement.

      General Methods

      The 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on an Inova 600 (Varian, Palo Alto, CA) spectrometer operating at 600 MHz (for 1H NMR) and at 150.8 MHz (for 13C NMR).       Water (W) and dioxane (D) were purified according to reported methods. (27) For notes on the pS+ scale see ref (22). From the measured kinetic constants, the relevant thermodynamic parameters and then the apparent first-order kinetic constants for the rearrangement [(kA,R)3] at 313.15 K were calculated. It works using a Kaleidagraph program which gives directly the values of the kinetic constants. In every instance, the experimental kinetic constants have been obtained with correlation coefficients ≥0.9997. The concentrations used are reported in the footnotes of Tables S1–S3. The borate buffer concentration was 0.0125 M for standard kinetics. In the instance of the kinetics of the rearrangement of 3a to gain information on the occurrence of general base catalysis, the used buffer concentrations were 0.0125, 0.0250, and 0.0500 M (the relevant kinetic constants are collected in Table S4 in the Supporting Information).
