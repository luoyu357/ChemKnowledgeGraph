    Abstract

    β-Nickel oxyhydroxide (β-NiOOH) is a promising electrocatalyst for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER), which is the more difficult half-reaction involved in water splitting. In this study, we revisit the OER activities of the two most abundant crystallographic facets of pristine β-NiOOH, the (0001) and (1010) facets, which expose 6-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated and 5-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni sites, respectively. To this end, we model various active sites on these two facets using hybrid density functional theory, which includes a fraction of the exact nonlocal Fock exchange in the electronic description of the system. By evaluating thermodynamic OER overpotentials, we show that the two active sites considered on each crystallographic facet demonstrate OER activities remarkably different from each other. However, the lowest OER overpotentials calculated for the two facets were found to be similar to each other and comparable to the overpotential for the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni site on the (1211) facet of β-NiOOH previously examined in J. Am. Chem. Soc. 2019, 141, 1, 693–705. This finding shows that all of the low-index facets investigated so far could be responsible for the experimentally observed OER activity of pristine β-NiOOH. However, the lowest overpotential active sites on these three crystallographic facets operate via different mechanisms, underscoring the importance of considering multiple OER pathways and intermediates on each crystallographic facet of a potential electrocatalyst. Specifically, our work demonstrates that consideration of previously overlooked active sites, transition-metal-ion oxidation states, reaction intermediates, and lattice-oxygen-stabilization are critical to reveal the lowest overpotential OER pathways on pristine β-NiOOH.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Water splitting is a potential synthesis route for renewable-energy-based, CO2-free hydrogen production and typically is carried out in the presence of a (photo)electrocatalyst. (1) Hydrogen is used in a number of important applications, such as fertilizer production, methanol and hydrocarbon synthesis, hydrotreating of biomass and petroleum, and in fuel cells. Its yearly production in 2014 was around 60 million metric tons. (2) Because hydrogen is produced primarily via steam reforming of methane, (3) a process that releases CO2 into the atmosphere, alternative, carbon-emission-free methods of hydrogen production are sorely needed. Over the years, vigorous research efforts have investigated (photo)electrochemically splitting water, (4) resulting in the knowledge that the anodic oxygen evolution reaction (OER) is more sluggish than the cathodic hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) (5) and that the most effective catalysts typically involve rare, expensive transition metals. Accordingly, in recent years, nickel and cobalt oxyhydroxides have received considerable attention as inexpensive, noble-metal-free electrocatalysts for the OER. (6−11) In particular, β-nickel oxyhydroxide (β-NiOOH) in its pure and iron-doped forms is a promising electrocatalyst in alkaline media for the OER. (10,12−14) However, despite many investigations, a debate still rages as to what the active site is for the OER on both pure and doped β-NiOOH, prompting a number of theoretical studies using first-principles quantum-mechanical calculations. (15−22) Some theoretical studies also have considered pure and doped γ-NiOOH, (23,24) another active phase of NiOOH, which contains intercalated species such as hydroxide, sulfate, and carbonate ions. (25) Nevertheless, although several crystallographic facets exist on the surface of any material at equilibrium, there have been few studies focused on unraveling the facet- and active-site-dependent OER activity of β-NiOOH. Here, we address this knowledge gap in the literature.
    Recently, Martirez and Carter developed a theoretical framework to study the OER activity of electrocatalyst materials using a real-space partitioning scheme that combines hybrid and semilocal density functional theory (DFT) calculations, in order to study the OER activity of a 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni active site on the (1211) facet of β-NiOOH. (22) Hybrid DFT involves a fraction of the exact, nonlocal Fock electron exchange energy, which is essential to describe properly oxidation-state changes during redox reactions in transition-metal-containing ionic compounds. Earlier use of the less costly Hubbard-corrected DFT (DFT+U) engendered errors because the Hubbard correction U is oxidation-state dependent, yet constant values of U must be employed for each transition metal to define a Hamiltonian that conserves energy.
    The theoretical OER activity of a material typically is quantified in terms of the thermodynamic overpotential, which represents the minimum voltage required, over the standard potential of the OER, to render nonpositive the standard Gibbs free energies of the electroactive elementary steps involved in the OER. Here, we calculate the thermodynamic overpotentials for the OER on the two most abundant crystallographic facets of pristine β-NiOOH, as determined earlier using DFT+U and the Wulff construction method: (26) the (0001) facet containing 6-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni and the (1010) facet containing 5-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni. (It is valid to use DFT+U to optimize facet structures and evaluate surface energies because the Ni oxidation state on pristine surfaces is unchanged compared to the bulk solid.) The (0001) and (1010) facets of β-NiOOH are depicted in the left and right panels in Figure 1, respectively. Taken together, they are predicted to comprise almost 70% of the surface area of β-NiOOH. (26) Two active sites are considered on each facet; different potential-determining steps (PDSs) are found on each of the four active sites considered. The PDS is the electroactive step that incurs the largest standard Gibbs free energy change per electron in the OER mechanism. Based on our investigation, we find that the OER activity of undoped, pristine β-NiOOH can be attributed to either of its two most-abundant crystallographic facets. We also find that the active sites with the lower OER overpotential on these two facets have overpotentials similar to each other and to the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni active site on the (1211) facet investigated previously by Martirez and Carter. (22) Thus, multiple mechanisms on multiple facets give rise to the threshold overpotential. Finally, we discuss the role of the Ni oxidation state and the oxygen coordination number in allowing for, on two of the four active sites investigated, comparatively low OER overpotentials.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The OER, when balanced using protons in solution, can be written as 2H2O → O2 + 4(H+ + e–) and it involves a standard Gibbs free energy change of 1.23 eV per electron transferred (or 4.92 eV per O2 molecule produced) at room temperature. This reaction usually is assumed to occur through a series of successive proton-coupled electron transfer steps, i.e., steps involving the transfer of (H+ + e–). (27) At each step of the OER mechanism, one of two things can happen: either a water molecule splits with the OH group attaching to the surface, the proton entering the solution, and the remaining electron escaping into the anode; or a proton adsorbed on the catalyst surface enters the solution with an electron escaping into the anode. In our modeling framework (see the Computational Methods section), standard reaction Gibbs free energies are calculated for each of the elementary steps comprising the OER mechanism; the PDS then corresponds to the most endoergic, electroactive step. The thermodynamic overpotential is defined as the difference between the standard Gibbs free energy change incurred during the PDS and 1.23 eV, per electron transferred. As defined, it is independent of the pH of the system and the species used to balance the OER (protons or hydroxide ions). (22) Accordingly, we calculate standard reaction Gibbs free energies (arbitrarily) at pH = 0.0 on thick periodic slabs using Hubbard-corrected DFT, (28) with electronic energy corrections using hybrid DFT (29,30) on thinner slabs incorporated using the “our own N-layered integrated molecular orbital and molecular mechanics” (ONIOM) model (31) (see the Computational Methods section). (22) Hubbard-corrected DFT is implemented based on the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation (XC) functional (32) and is referred to as the PBE+U level of theory. Hybrid DFT is implemented based on the Heyd-Scuseria-Ernzerhof XC functional (33) and is referred to as the HSE06 level of theory. Note that, as determined by Martirez and Carter, (26) the (1010) facet has half a monolayer of water adsorbed onto the surface in its pristine form (Figure 1, right panel), as opposed to the (0001) facet where the formation of an ordered monolayer or submonolayer of water is unfavorable (Figure 1, left panel).

    Discussion of the Oxidation State Changes during the OER Cycle on the ▲ Site of the (0001) Facet

    As discussed in the Supporting Information, oxidation states of the oxygen and the surface nickel atoms were inferred using atomic magnetic moment projections (see Tables S3 and S4 for the mapping between atom-projected magnetic moments and oxidation states for surface Ni atoms). Note that hydrogen does not change its oxidation state during the OER; it remains constant at +1 throughout the mechanism. On the other hand, oxygen oxidizes from a −2 oxidation state (in H2O) to a 0 oxidation state (in O2). As a result, during each elementary step in the OER mechanism on β-NiOOH, electrons are lost by an oxygen ion and/or gained/lost by a surface nickel ion. Note that, as the oxygen atoms are oxidized, the O–O bond lengths (at the PBE+U level of theory) change from around 1.45 Å in the hydroperoxo intermediate (oxidation state: −1) to around 1.30 Å in the superoxo intermediate (oxidation state: −1/2), and to around 1.23 Å in molecular oxygen (oxidation state: 0). Step #1: On the ▲ site, the OER mechanism begins with the addition of water converting the oxo group (▲O) to a hydroperoxo group (▲OOH), leading to the oxidation of the oxo oxygen (−2) and water molecule oxygen (−2) both to hydroperoxo oxygens (−1). Two electrons thus are lost by the oxygen ions, with one of the electrons reducing a surface Ni3+ to Ni2+ and the other electron moving into the anode as the proton from the water molecule enters the solution. Step #2: The hydroperoxo group (▲OOH) is converted to a superoxo group (▲O2), with the hydroperoxo oxygens (−1) oxidizing to superoxo oxygens (−1/2), and the lost proton and electron escaping respectively into the solution and the anode. Step #3: The superoxo group is released as O2 gas in a non-electroactive step. Accordingly, the superoxo oxygens (−1/2) are oxidized to O2 oxygens (0), with the lost electron reducing another surface Ni3+ to Ni2+. At this stage, two surface Ni ions are in the +2 oxidation state. Step #4: This step is another non-electroactive step and involves no change in the oxidation state of the surface Ni, with a water molecule simply adsorbing at the empty ▲ site, producing the ▲OH2 intermediate. Step #5: The adsorbed water molecule (▲OH2) is deprotonated to a hydroxo species (▲OH), causing no change in the oxygen oxidation state (−2), but instead causing one of the surface Ni2+ ions to oxidize back to Ni3+. Step #6: The hydroxo group (▲OH) converts back to the oxo group (▲O) via a second deprotonation step, with no change in the oxygen oxidation state (−2) accompanied by the oxidation of the remaining surface Ni2+ ion back to Ni3+.

    Discussion of the Oxidation State Changes during the OER Cycle on the ■ Site of the (0001) Facet

    Step #1: On the ■ site, the OER mechanism begins with the deprotonation of the hydroxo group (■OH) to produce the oxo group (■O), with no change in the oxygen oxidation state, but with a surface Ni3+ ion oxidizing to Ni4+. Step #2: The oxo group (■O) is converted to the hydroperoxo group (■OOH) by the addition of a water molecule that singly deprotonates, with the oxygens in the oxo group (−2) and the water molecule (−2) being oxidized to two hydroperoxo oxygens (−1). While one of the two electrons is lost to the anode, the other electron reduces the previously formed Ni4+ ion back to Ni3+. Step #3: The hydroperoxo group (■OOH) is deprotonated to form the superoxo group (■O2), with the two oxygen ions oxidizing from the −1 to the −1/2 oxidation state, and the lost electron departing into the anode, as the lost proton moves into the solution. Step #4: The superoxo group (■O2) is released as O2 gas in this non-electroactive step, with the oxygen ions being oxidized from the −1/2 to the 0 oxidation state. The lost electron reduces a surface Ni3+ ion to Ni2+. Step #5: Water adsorbs on the empty ■ site to form the ■OH2 intermediate in a non-electroactive fashion. This step involves no change in oxygen or nickel oxidation states. Step #6: The adsorbed water molecule (■OH2) deprotonates to yield a hydroxo group (■OH), with the oxygen atom remaining in the −2 oxidation state, but with the surface Ni2+ ion (produced in step #4) oxidizing back to Ni3+. The electron originating from the latter oxidation and the proton originating from the deprotonation form a proton–electron couple that escapes respectively to the solution and the anode.

    Comparison of the Thermodynamic OER Overpotentials on the ▲ and ■ Sites of the (0001) Facet

    The thermodynamic overpotential is determined to be 0.99 V on the initially oxo site, ▲ (Figure 3, dashed red lines, with the standard reaction Gibbs free energy of the PDS being 2.22 eV) and 0.44 V on the initially hydroxo site, ■ (Figure 3, dashed blue lines, with the standard reaction Gibbs free energy of the PDS being 1.67 eV), indicating remarkably different overpotentials for different active sites on the (0001) facet of β-NiOOH. As seen in Table 1, the PDS for the ▲ site is found to be the step converting ▲O to ▲OOH. From Table 2, on the ■ site, both the steps converting ■OH to ■O and ■O to ■OOH are potential-determining, as they incur similar standard Gibbs free energy changes (only differing by 0.03 eV). The key difference between the two active sites is that during the PDS(s) a surface Ni reduces from Ni3+ to Ni2+ on the ▲ site (with the oxo group oxidizing instead into a hydroperoxo group) whereas a surface Ni oxidizes from Ni3+ to Ni4+ and reduces back to Ni3+ on the ■ site (with a proton–electron couple escaping each time as the hydroxo group converts to an oxo group, and as the oxo group oxidizes to a hydroperoxo group when a water molecule is added).

    Investigation of the OER Activity of the (1010) Facet

    On the (1010) surface, two different active sites are considered: the ⬟ site (which initially is in the hydrated form, ⬟OH2, on the pristine surface), labeled as site “3” in the right panel of Figure 1, and the ⬣ site (which initially is in the hydroxo form, ⬣OH, on the pristine surface), labeled as site “4” in the right panel of Figure 1. Note that there exists the possibility of considering a third site, the ⬢O site, involving an oxo group, rather than a hydroxo group (⬣OH) bridging two surface Ni atoms. However, we excluded this site from our analysis because this site lies deeper in the surface (∼0.4 Å below the plane of the surface Ni atoms), whereas the ⬣OH site lies exposed to the surface-water interface (∼0.6 Å above the plane of the surface Ni atoms). Accordingly, the access of water molecules to the ⬢O site is expected to be sterically hindered, as can be readily observed from a space-filling model of the (1010) facet (see Figure S1 in the Supporting Information).     Discussion of the Oxidation State Changes during the OER Cycle on the ⬟ Site of the (1010) Facet

    Step #1: On the ⬟ site, the OER mechanism begins when an adsorbed water molecule (⬟OH2) deprotonates to form a hydroxo group (⬟OH), causing no change in the oxygen oxidation state (−2). Instead, one of the surface Ni3+ ions oxidizes to Ni4+ as a proton–electron couple leaves the catalyst surface. Step #2: The hydroxo group (⬟OH) oxidizes to a lattice-oxygen-stabilized oxo (or a lattice peroxo) group (⬟OOl), with the hydroxo and the lattice oxygens (−2) oxidizing to two lattice-peroxo oxygens (−1). One of the two electrons thus released reduces the surface Ni4+ ion back to Ni3+, while the other electron is lost to the anode. Step #3: The lattice peroxo group (⬟OOl), upon addition of a water molecule, converts to an adsorbed O2 molecule (⬟O2), with one of the hydrogen atoms from the water molecule transferring to another lattice oxygen (Ol’). This lattice oxygen (Ol’) already was bonded to a proton; this proton moves over to an oxygen atom in the same layer (equivalently, the adjacent layer in the [0001] direction) of β-NiOOH (Oγ). Note that we have not mapped out the reaction pathway explicitly; thus, it is not known whether the addition of the water molecule and the hopping over of the lattice proton occur sequentially or simultaneously. Nevertheless, in this step, the lattice-oxygen-stabilized oxygen (−1) and water molecule oxygen (−2) are oxidized to O2 oxygens (0). Of the three electrons thus released, one reduces the lattice oxygen (Ol) back from −1 to the −2 oxidation state, another one escapes to the anode as the water molecule is deprotonated, and a third reduces a surface Ni3+ to Ni2+. Step #4: The other lattice oxygen (Ol′) deprotonates; the escaping electron is taken from the surface Ni2+, which gets oxidized back to Ni3+. Further, the proton that previously moved to Oγ jumps back to Ol′, as in the pristine state of the catalyst. Again, explicit kinetic modeling would be required to predict whether the deprotonation of Ol′ and the hopping over of the proton from Oγ to Ol′ occur sequentially or simultaneously. Step #5: The adsorbed O2 molecule (⬟O2) simply escapes in this non-electroactive step, producing an empty ⬟ site, with no change in the oxidation states of any of the atoms. Step #6: The OER cycle is completed when a water molecule from the bulk adsorbs onto the empty ⬟ site in another non-electroactive step. It is worth noting that out of the four active sites investigated in this work, the ⬟ site is the only one involved in the formation of an adsorbed O2 molecule rather than an adsorbed superoxo group. The reason for this is that, on the ⬟ site, none of the oxygen atoms in the O2 group are part of the β-NiOOH lattice (i.e., all of them came from incoming water molecules). On the other hand, at the other three active sites, one of the oxygen atoms in the O2 molecule belonged to the β-NiOOH lattice.

    Discussion of the Oxidation State Changes during the OER Cycle on the ⬣ Site of the (1010) Facet

    Step #1: On the ⬣ site, the OER mechanism begins when the hydroxo group (⬣OH), upon the addition of a water molecule, converts to a hydroperoxo group (⬣OOH), with the proton from the hydroxo group moving over to a lattice oxygen in the same layer (equivalently, the adjacent layer in the [0001] direction) of β-NiOOH (Oγ) and the second proton from the water molecule leaving into the solution. Note that the ⬣OOH + OγH intermediate thus formed is the relaxed version of the ⬣O(OH)H intermediate. In this step, both the hydroxo oxygen (−2) and the water oxygen (−2) are oxidized to hydroperoxo oxygens (−1). One of the two electrons lost escapes to the anode, while the other electron reduces a surface Ni3+ ion to Ni2+. Step #2: Subsequently, the proton attached to the lattice oxygen (Oγ) desorbs and an electron is lost from a surface Ni2+ that oxidizes back to Ni3+. Step #3: The hydroperoxo group (⬣OOH) is converted to an adsorbed superoxo group (⬣O2) with the release of a proton. In this step, the hydroperoxo oxygens (−1) are oxidized to superoxo oxygens (−1/2), thereby releasing an electron into the anode. At this stage, all of the surface Ni atoms are in the +3 oxidation state. Step #4: The superoxo group (⬣O2) is released as an O2 molecule, with the superoxo oxygens (−1/2) getting oxidized to O2 oxygens (0). The lost electron reduces one of the surface Ni3+ ions to Ni2+ in the empty ⬣ state, thereby resulting in a non-electroactive step. Step #5: An incoming water molecule dissociatively adsorbs onto the ⬣ site, leading to the formation of the hydroxo group (⬣OH) and a proton on the lattice oxygen Oγ. There are no oxidation state changes for any of the atoms during this non-electroactive step. Step #6: The OER cycle is completed when the proton adsorbed on the lattice oxygen Oγ is released, forming the pristine state of the catalyst with the hydroxo group (⬣OH). In this step, the surface Ni2+ is oxidized back to Ni3+, thereby providing an electron that is lost to the anode.

    Comparison of the Thermodynamic OER Overpotentials on the ⬟ and ⬣ Sites of the (1010) Facet

    The thermodynamic overpotential is determined to be 0.43 V on the initially hydrated ⬟ site (Figure 5, dashed green lines, with the standard reaction Gibbs free energy of the PDS being 1.66 eV) and 0.73 V on the initially hydroxo ⬣ site (Figure 5, dashed purple lines, with the standard reaction Gibbs free energy of the PDS being 1.96 eV). This finding indicates that different active sites can exhibit remarkably different overpotentials on the (1010) facet of β-NiOOH, as well. As seen in Tables 3 and 4, respectively, the PDSs for the ⬟ and ⬣ sites are found to be the steps converting ⬟OH2 to ⬟OH and ⬣OH to ⬣OOH + OγH, respectively. The key difference between the two active sites is that a surface Ni3+ is oxidized to Ni4+ on the ⬟ site (with the adsorbed water deprotonating to a hydroxo group) whereas Ni retains its +3 oxidation state on the ⬣ site (with the hydroxo group oxidizing to a hydroperoxo group and the Ni3+ attached to Oγ reducing to Ni2+) during the PDS.

    Role of the Ni3+ to Ni4+ Transition in Ensuring a Low OER Overpotential on the ■ and ⬟ Sites

    Similar to the ■ site on the (0001) facet and the ⬟ site on the (1010) facet, on the undoped β-NiOOH (1211) surface investigated by Martirez and Carter, (22) the lowest thermodynamic overpotential of 0.48 V was found when one of the PDSs involved a 6-fold-coordinated surface Ni3+ being oxidized to Ni4+. (In the latter case, the formation of Ni4+ is accompanied by the conversion of a terminal hydroxo group to a terminal dihydroxo group, upon the addition of a water molecule. The other PDS in the latter case, occurring immediately after the first, incurs an identical standard Gibbs free energy change of 1.71 eV and involves a reduction of the surface Ni4+ back to Ni3+, with the dihydroxo group oxidizing to a hydroperoxo group.) This observation, made across multiple facets of β-NiOOH, indicates that the oxidation of surface Ni3+ to Ni4+ is a thermodynamically favorable step that is the key to a relatively low overpotential OER mechanism on undoped β-NiOOH. Moreover, as all the facets on the β-NiOOH surface involve either 4-, 5-, or 6-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni active sites (e.g., the (0001), (1010), and (1211) facets, respectively), (26) our results point to the OER activity of β-NiOOH being facet-independent. On the other hand, the reduction of surface Ni3+ to Ni2+ during the PDS results in a comparatively higher OER overpotential, as seen on the ▲ and ⬣ active sites. The importance of Ni4+ in ensuring a low overpotential mechanism on iron-doped NiOOH was discussed in previous theoretical work, e.g., by Shin et al. (23) and Xiao at el. (24) in their work on the OER activity of doped γ-NiOOH (1010). Our work instead considers the role of Ni4+ in the OER activity of undoped β-NiOOH.

    Role of the Oxygen Coordination Number in Ensuring a Low OER Overpotential on the ■ and ⬟ Sites

    The PDS on the ■ site of the (0001) facet, i.e., the deprotonation of a hydroxo group to an oxo group, incurs a lower standard Gibbs free energy change (ΔGr0 = 1.67 eV) than the same step on the ⬟ site of the (1010) facet (ΔGr0 = 2.17 eV, see Table S7 in the Supporting Information) or on the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni site of the (1211) facet (ΔGr0 = 2.07 eV, see ref (22)). During this step, a surface Ni3+ is oxidized to Ni4+ on the ■ site, while a surface Ni4+ is oxidized to Ni5+ on the ⬟ site and all surface Ni ions remain as Ni3+ on the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni site of the (1211) facet. Apart from differences in surface Ni oxidation state changes between these cases, the active-site oxygen coordination number also differs between the three active sites. The surface hydroxo and the oxo intermediates on the ■ site are triply coordinated to three Ni ions, as opposed to being doubly coordinated on the ⬟ site of the (1010) facet and singly coordinated on the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni site of the (1211) facet. The above observation indicates that, along with changes in surface Ni oxidation states, the oxygen coordination number can also affect the OER mechanism by (de)stabilizing intermediates on certain facets as opposed to others. In line with this assertion, the involvement of a lattice oxygen atom was found to be essential in stabilizing the ⬟OOl intermediate on the (1010) facet, because a terminal oxo ligand is unstable on Ni. Such lattice-afforded stabilization results in the low thermodynamic overpotential of 0.43 V, by circumventing the formation of a singly coordinated O atom in the ⬟O intermediate, in favor of a doubly coordinated O atom in the ⬟OOl intermediate (see Figure 6, wherein the structures of the ⬟O and ⬟OOl intermediates are compared; the O atom of interest is outlined with a pentagon). Indeed, the thermodynamic overpotential associated with the OER mechanism involving the formation of ⬟O was found to be 0.94 V (see the Supporting Information, Table S7).

    Other OER Mechanisms at the Various Active Sites Considered in This Work

    Mechanisms with higher thermodynamic overpotentials than the ones discussed above were found by considering the dihydrogenperoxo intermediate (*O(OH)H) on the ▲, ■, and ⬟ active sites. These mechanisms are discussed in the Supporting Information (Tables S8–S10) and either involve highly endoergic, non-electroactive elementary steps (▲ site, Table S8) or have larger thermodynamic OER overpotentials (1.87 V for the ■ site, as seen in Table S9, and 0.61 V for the ⬟ site, as seen in Table S10). On the ⬣ site, the lowest thermodynamic overpotential mechanism involved the ⬣O(OH)H intermediate (to be more precise, its relaxed form, the ⬣OOH + OγH intermediate), as seen in Table 4. For this active site, the mechanism involving the ⬣O intermediate has a larger thermodynamic overpotential of 0.92 V, as seen in the Supporting Information (Table S11), with the PDS being the step converting ⬣OH to ⬣O. Note that another way to form the ⬣O intermediate from the ⬣OH intermediate, by intra-NiOOH proton hopping, i.e., ⬣OH + Oγ → ⬣O + OγH, also cannot occur at room temperature as it is a non-electroactive step incurring a large free energy change of 0.84 eV (obtained by subtracting step #6 from step #1 in Table S11).

    Role of Intra-NiOOH Hydrogen Bonding in Differentiating the Initially Hydroxo ⬣ and ■ Sites

    The 0.73 V thermodynamic overpotential found on the initially hydroxo ⬣ site on the (1010) surface (Table 4) is higher than the 0.44 V thermodynamic overpotential on the initially hydroxo ■ site on the (0001) facet (Table 2), leading one to examine the reason for the dissimilar OER overpotentials at these two active sites. The key difference between these active sites is that the formation of the hydroperoxo group on the ⬣ and ■ sites (the PDS for the former and one of the PDSs for the latter) is accompanied by the reduction of the surface Ni3+ bonded with Oγ to Ni2+ for the former and of the primary adsorption site Ni4+ to Ni3+ for the latter. This finding highlights the importance of forming Ni4+ prior to the formation of the O–O bond for obtaining a lower OER overpotential. One might ask why the above-mentioned difference in oxidation states arises between the ⬣ and ■ sites, which initially are both in the hydroxo form. In terms of local bonding, the key difference between the ⬣ and ■ active sites is that the H atom in ⬣OH forms a hydrogen bond to the β-NiOOH layer on which it is adsorbed (equivalently, the adjacent β-NiOOH layer in the [0001] direction) (Figure 4, lower panel), whereas no such hydrogen bond is formed by the H atom in ■OH (Figure 2, lower panel). The presence of this hydrogen bond allows for intra-NiOOH proton-hopping in the vicinity of the ⬣ active site, thereby allowing changes in oxidation state to occur away from the active site (at the Ni bonded to Oγ instead of at the active Ni). Indeed, proton-hopping close to the active site can bring about a significant change in the lowest overpotential mechanism by altering the relative stability of the adsorbed intermediates. To demonstrate this phenomenon, at both the ■ and ⬣ active sites, we examined the deprotonation of *OH (* = ■, ⬣) to *O and the conversion of *OH to *O(OH)H through the addition of a hydroxyl group, as competing elementary steps. On the ■ site, the favorable pathway was found to be the ■OH to ■O transition, yielding a thermodynamic overpotential of 0.44 V (Table 2), while consideration of ■O(OH)H yielded a higher thermodynamic overpotential of 1.87 V (see Table S9 in the Supporting Information). At the ⬣ site, however, when a hydroxo group was added to the ⬣OH intermediate, the H atom in the resulting ⬣O(OH)H intermediate hopped over to the Oγ atom in the same layer (equivalently, the adjacent layer in the [0001] direction) of β-NiOOH to form the ⬣OOH + OγH intermediate, yielding a comparatively lower thermodynamic overpotential of 0.73 V (Table 4). On the other hand, the thermodynamic overpotential to form ⬣O at this site, despite the oxidation of Ni3+ to Ni4+ during the PDS, was found to have a higher value of 0.92 V (Table S11), due to the effect of the oxygen coordination number (vide supra).

    Effect of Considering Implicit Solvation by Water on the Calculated OER Overpotentials

    We also considered the effect of incorporating water solvation in an implicit manner, by making use of a dielectric polarizable continuum model (PCM), as implemented in the VASPsol package. (34,35) For this purpose, a dielectric constant of 78.4 was used to simulate the solvation provided by water. Since the inclusion of implicit solvation along with hybrid DFT is computationally very intensive, we applied the PCM framework to study its effect on the OER free energetics calculated at the PBE+U level of theory using the thicker slab models. The ensuing results for the ■ and ⬟ sites are provided in the Supporting Information, Tables S12 and S13, respectively. From Table S12, we see that the thermodynamic overpotential of the ■ site goes up by around 0.09 V upon inclusion of implicit solvation effects and from Table S13, we see that the thermodynamic overpotential of the ⬟ site is unchanged upon inclusion of such effects. Since the maximal change of 0.09 V is roughly equal to any experimental errors in the measurement of OER overpotentials, we conclude that the inclusion of implicit solvation does not affect our results significantly. Further, as detailed in section SI6 of the Supporting Information, we are also able to physically rationalize the relative differences in the effect of implicit solvation on the thermodynamic overpotentials at the ■ and ⬟ sites in terms of the polarity of the surface adsorbates involved as reactant/product in the PDS.

    Understanding the Present Results in Light of Previous Experimental and Simulation Data

    The experimental OER overpotential on rigorously Fe-free β-NiOOH was measured to be 0.53–0.60 V at a current density of 10 mA/cm2. (13) Detailed microkinetic modeling, including the electric-potential-dependence (under constant potential conditions) of the reaction free energies and the corresponding activation barriers would be required to convert the thermodynamic overpotentials discussed above to kinetic overpotentials. (36−38) Nevertheless, our calculated values for the thermodynamic overpotential of 0.44 V on the ■ site of the (0001) facet and 0.43 V on the ⬟ site of the (1010) facet provide reasonable lower bounds for the kinetic overpotential of undoped NiOOH measured experimentally. On the other hand, the thermodynamic overpotentials on the ▲ site of the (0001) facet and the ⬣ site of the (1010) facet were found to be 0.99 and 0.73 V, ruling them out as being responsible for the experimentally observed OER activity of undoped β-NiOOH. Supporting our proposed mechanism on the ■ site of the (0001) facet, previous experimental work by Morales et al. demonstrated the role of the deprotonation of NiOOH in the OER cycle, while also observing the presence of a superoxo species on the surface, using in situ surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy. (39) Further, our calculated values of the thermodynamic overpotential at the ■ and ⬟ sites on the (0001) and (1010) facets, respectively, are among the lowest values for any facet of undoped β-NiOOH. For example, Selloni et al. determined values of 0.46 and 0.52 V, respectively, for the thermodynamic overpotentials on the (1015) facet of undoped β-NiOOH and the (1010) facet of γ-NiOOH, using PBE+U-based calculations (U = 5.5 eV). (15) Tkalych et al. determined a value of 0.75 V for the thermodynamic overpotential on the (0001) facet of undoped β-NiOOH using PBE+U-based calculations with the same supercell size (U = 5.5 eV). (18,19) Note that this value compares well with the value of 0.70 V reported in Table 1 for the ▲ site using PBE+U-based calculations; the lower value of 0.57 V reported by Tkalych et al. in ref (20) for the same active site was due to an inadvertently multiplied factor of 0.5 in the evaluation of the vibrational free energies. Doyle et al. reported values of 0.42 and 0.68 V, respectively, for the ▲ and ■ sites on the (0001) facet using PBE+U-based calculations (U = 6.64 eV), (17) as compared to our values of 0.70 and 0.69 V using PBE+U calculations (U = 5.5 eV). The differences in the overpotentials on the ▲ and ■ active sites in the work by Doyle et al., (17) as compared to our work, are attributable to a higher value of U for Ni utilized in ref (17). In recent work, Xiao et al. calculated the thermodynamic OER overpotentials to be 1.16 and 1.38 V, respectively, on the (1010) facet of γ-NiOOH, using PBE- and B3PW91-based calculations. (24) Note that the overpotential values reported in their paper are lower, as they are derived based on a microkinetic model taking into account the O–O coupling step as the rate-determining step. As already discussed, Martirez and Carter determined the thermodynamic OER overpotential to be 0.67 and 0.48 V, respectively, using PBE+U- and HSE06-based calculations (U = 5.5 eV), on the (1211) facet of undoped β-NiOOH (vide supra). (22) There also is experimental support for the role of Ni4+ in the OER cycle on NiOOH. For example, at Fe content below 4%, the Ni atoms in NiOOH were found to be present as Ni4+ under catalytic conditions, using operando differential electrochemical mass spectroscopy (DEMS) data. (40) In another study, Zheng et al. showed that the OER overpotential on NiOOH can be reduced by modulating the free energy of formation of Ni4+ by doping with Co, Fe, and P, both experimentally and in silico, again demonstrating the importance of Ni4+ in the OER cycle in NiOOH. (41) Although Ni4+ plays an important role in the OER cycle on γ-NiOOH as that phase in its pristine form contains Ni in the +4 oxidation state, (42) our study shows that Ni4+ also plays a role in the oxygen evolution activity of β-NiOOH, by forming during the OER cycle. Finally, the role of lattice oxygen atoms in leading to low overpotential OER pathways has also been investigated in previous experimental and theoretical work, mainly in the context of perovskite materials. (43−46) In terms of studies focused on NiOOH, the OER mechanisms investigated by Li and Selloni using PBE+U calculations on undoped β-NiOOH (0115) and γ-NiOOH(1010) involved lattice-oxygen-stabilized oxygen adatoms. (15) In that work, however, the mechanisms involving lattice-oxygen-stabilization were not compared and contrasted with mechanisms not involving such stabilization.

    By investigating two different active sites on the two most-abundant crystallographic facets of β-NiOOH, we conclude that the two facets considered can exhibit similar levels of OER activity in the solution phase, despite remarkably different reaction chemistry. Notably, various elementary steps, such as the conversion of ▲O to ▲OOH on the ▲ site, ■OH to ■O and ■O to ■OOH on the ■ site, ⬟OH2 to ⬟OH on the ⬟ site, and ⬣OH to ⬣OOH + OγH on the ⬣ site, were found to be potential-determining, indicating considerable chemical diversity in PDSs among the four active sites considered in this work. Despite the rich variation in preferred mechanistic pathways, the highest OER activity sites on the (0001), (1010), and (1211) facets share two key features in common: all three achieve low overpotentials via the oxidation of Ni3+ to Ni4+ prior to the formation of the O–O bond, and at least one of the PDSs on all three of these sites involves the oxidation of 6-fold-coordinated Ni3+ to Ni4+. We also illuminated the role of lattice-oxygen-stabilization in ensuring the lowest OER overpotential observed on the ⬟ site of the (1010) surface, as well as how local bonding phenomena (e.g., oxygen coordination number and hydrogen bonding) can affect the OER activity by (de)stabilizing certain intermediates and consequently (increasing) decreasing the OER overpotential. Furthermore, the use of hybrid DFT calculations showed that even the most-abundant basal plane contains active sites for electrocatalyzing the OER on pristine, undoped β-NiOOH. Finally, our prediction of a crystallographic-facet-independent OER overpotential for undoped β-NiOOH means that its OER activity should be uniform across various crystallographic facets on the electrode surface. Our findings further motivate a deeper investigation of potential electrocatalytic materials by considering previously overlooked active sites and reaction intermediates (e.g., *O(OH)H and *OOl) using hybrid DFT calculations, and elucidating the results in terms of differences in metal oxidation states, surface oxygen coordination numbers, and hydrogen bonding.

    PBE+U Calculations

    Electronic energies of all of the intermediate species were obtained using the PBE XC functional (32) with a Hubbard correction, based on the rotationally invariant formalism proposed by Dudarev et al., (28) i.e., at the PBE+U level of theory. A U value of 5.5 eV, adapted from the linear response theory calculations (48) of Li and Selloni, (15) was utilized for Ni. The projector-augmented-wave (PAW) formalism (49) was utilized as implemented in the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP), version 5.4.4, with a plane-wave basis kinetic energy cutoff of 750 eV and a Monkhorst–Pack (50,51)k-point mesh of size 3 × 3 × 1, leading to total energies converged to less than 1 meV/atom error. For PBE+U and HSE06 calculations using a thinner slab (vide infra), a plane-wave cutoff of 650 eV was utilized. The exchange kernel was sampled with a single k-point in the reciprocal space. The self-consistent energy convergence criterion was set to 10–5 eV. Gaussian smearing with a width of 0.01 eV was used to aid the convergence of the calculations. Periodic boundary conditions were applied in all directions. Reaction adsorbates were added to both sides of the slab in order to minimize the dipole moment of the simulation box. Moreover, dipole corrections were applied in the direction perpendicular to the slab using the LDIPOL=.TRUE. and IDIPOL=3 tags in VASP. For molecules (i.e., H2O and H2), dipole corrections were applied in all three directions using the LDIPOL=.TRUE. and IDIPOL=4 tags in VASP. Geometry optimizations were carried out for all cases (PBE+U and HSE06) until the forces on all atoms were less than 0.01 eV/Å. Normal modes were calculated for all slab surface atoms (i.e., the entire first layer of atoms on one side of the slab) and for entire molecules (see the Supporting Information for the normal modes of isolated water and hydrogen molecules) using the system Hessian evaluated through central difference derivatives, with a perturbation of ±0.02 Å. The (0001) surface was modeled principally using a five-layer (5L) slab so as to reproduce the bulk density of states in the middle layer, (26) with exchange interaction corrections (vide infra) applied using a thinner three-layer (3L) slab (vide infra). On the other hand, the (1010) surface was modeled principally using a seven-layer (7L) slab for a similar reason, with exchange interaction corrections applied using a thinner five-layer (5L) slab (vide infra). For both surfaces, 2 × 2 supercells containing 4 Ni atoms in each layer were utilized. About 15 Å of vacuum space (measured from the outermost oxygen atoms on each side of the slab) was used in the direction perpendicular to the surfaces.

    Discussion on the Choice of 15% Exact Exchange for the HSE06 Functional

    Although most hybrid functionals, such as HSE06, (29,33) PBE0, (52) and B3LYP, (53,54) were initially proposed with 20–25% exact Fock exchange incorporated, some recent studies have rationalized the use of 15% (or even lower) exact Fock exchange in the electronic description of transition-metal-containing compounds (e.g., LaMO3 perovskites (M = Ti to Cu), (55) iron oxides, (56,57) and ceria (58)). Indeed XC functionals for transition metals with a lower fraction of exact exchange have been developed. (59,60) Previous work from our group showed that the HSE06 hybrid functional, whether using 15% or 25% exchange, improved the description of the thermochemistry of water, oxygen, and hydrogen, as compared to using PBE+U. (22) Moreover, HSE06 with 15% exact exchange is superior in terms of modeling the energetics and electronic structure of iron oxides, (22,56,57) which justified its use in modeling OER free energetics on Fe-doped β-NiOOH. (22) However, there is less clarity with respect to the choice of 15% exact exchange for modeling pure β-NiOOH. A straightforward method to benchmark the fraction of exact exchange in hybrid functionals is to compare the predicted Kohn–Sham band gap with the measured optical gap of the desired material. This comparison is not exact because the Kohn–Sham band gap does not correspond to a real ground-state-to-excited-state transition and does not include excitonic effects. Nevertheless, the Kohn–Sham band gap often is the only metric amenable for direct comparison with experimental data. Accordingly, as reported in the Supporting Information, we estimated from density of states calculations the Kohn–Sham band gap for β-NiOOH using PBE+U and HSE06 with 15% and 25% exact exchange incorporated. The Kohn–Sham band gaps were found to be 0.2, 1.0, and 1.7 eV, in the three cases, respectively (the calculations using PBE+U and HSE06 with 25% exact exchange for pure β-NiOOH were reported already by our group in ref (26)). These three values from the three different theories compare very well with what is calculated for β-NiOOH substitutionally doped with 12.5% Fe, indicating that the host β-NiOOH defines the band gap alone even when (lightly) doped with Fe. (22) In terms of experimental data, the optical gap of NiOOH has been reported to be 1.7–1.8 eV in ref (61) and 1.5 eV in ref (62), both using absorption spectroscopy, indicating that HSE06 with 25% exact exchange more closely approximates the measured optical gap. Given that time-independent (hybrid) DFT is a ground-state theory, such perfect matching should be viewed cautiously. Notably, however, in both measurements, the onset of light absorption occurred at lower energies around 1.5 and 1.0 eV, respectively, indicating that HSE06 with 15% exact exchange modeled this aspect of light absorption by the material better. Finally, the choice of HSE06 with 15% exact exchange, as opposed to PBE+U and HSE06 with 25% exact exchange, can also be rationalized on a more empirical footing based on OER free energetics. Examination of the thermodynamic overpotential data presented in this work and in previous work from our group (22) reveals that the overpotentials predicted using PBE+U or HSE06 with 25% exact exchange, for a wide range of active sites and both undoped and Fe-doped β-NiOOH, are always higher than the measured values (13) (see Tables S1, S2, S5, and S6 in the Supporting Information and data in ref (22)). On the other hand, using HSE06 with 15% exact exchange incorporated, we are able to identify a few active sites that exhibit lower thermodynamic overpotentials than the measured values (e.g., the ■ site on the (0001) facet and the ⬟ site on the (1010) facet of undoped β-NiOOH, and the 4-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni site on the (1211) facet of Fe-doped β-NiOOH (22)), thereby being more consistent with experimental data.
