    Abstract

    We develop a new express methodology for modeling excited-state dynamics occurring in dense manifolds of electronic states in atomistic systems. The approach leverages a modified Landau–Zener formula, the neglect of a back-reaction approximation, and the highly efficient density functional tight-binding method. We study the hot electron dynamics in a series of H- and F-terminated silicon nanocrystals (NCs) containing up to several hundred atoms. We explain the slower electron cooling dynamics in F-terminated NCs by the larger energy gaps between the adjacent electronic states in these systems as well as their slower fluctuations. We conclude that both the mass and chemical identity of the surface termination groups equally influence the electron dynamics, on average. However, the mass effect becomes dominant for higher-energy excitations. We find that the electron decay dynamics in F-terminated NCs has a greater sensitivity to the mass of the surface ligands than do the H-terminated NCs and explain this observation by the details of the electron–phonon coupling in the systems. We find that in the H-terminated NCs, electronic transitions in the cooling process occur predominantly between the surface states, whereas in F-terminated Si NCs, both surface and NC core states are coupled to the nuclear vibrations. We find that electron energy relaxation is accelerated in larger NCs and attribute this effect to the higher densities of states and smaller energy gaps in these systems.

    Nonadiabatic (NA) molecular dynamics (NA-MD) is a powerful tool used to predict the outcomes of chemical processes involving multiple quantum states. It describes the coupled evolution of classical and quantum degrees of freedom in the regime where the standard Born–Oppenheimer approximation breaks down. Trajectory surface hopping (TSH) approaches such as the fewest switches surface hopping (FSSH) (1) method of Tully have been among the most widely used schemes for NA-MD simulations thanks to their conceptual simplicity, computational efficiency, and great robustness. (2−6) Among the TSH techniques, the class of methods that neglects the effects of electronic excitations on nuclear dynamics is known as the neglect of back-reaction approximation (NBRA). (7−9) This technique has enabled numerous studies of real-time NA processes in a wide variety of challenging systems, including nanostructures (10−20) and periodic solids. (21−27) Despite the great computational savings of the NBRA-based methods, the corresponding calculations are still demanding. Even with the computational savings of the NBRA, performing computations on systems containing several hundred atoms or for as long as tens of picoseconds is often nearly impractical. This limitation becomes even more severe if one is interested in the role of various control parameters (chemical doping/defects, temperature, size, etc.) on the NA dynamics in these systems or in screening a large number of potential materials. Such limitations are associated with the need to compute properties that require explicit operations on the wave functions of the systems, such as time-derivative nonadiabatic couplings (tNACs). Although such matrix elements are relatively inexpensive if electronic wave functions are represented by molecular orbitals, these extra costs are not negligible. More importantly, they become quite demanding if one is interested in tNACs between many-body wave functions.

    The Landau–Zener (LZ) (28) formula for probabilities of NA transitions is a convenient alternative to the TSH methods. Unlike the latter that rely on the wave-function-derived properties such as tNACs, the LZ approach requires only the diabatic matrix elements. The LZ-based surface hopping (LZSH) approach (29) was developed by Tully and Preston back in 1970 and was demonstrated to be useful for modeling reaction cross sections of H+ + D2 collisions. Conceptually, the LZ-based approach is attractive because it naturally prevents the overcoherent dynamics typical for FSSH-type methods. Indeed, the coherent evolution is not even considered in the LZSH, leaving no mechanisms for developing mixed states in the asymptotic regions where one expects the system to adopt only one of the pure quantum states. The “fewest switches” criterion is accounted for by the condition that the surface hops are attempted only at the intersections of diabatic surfaces. Through this mechanism, one avoids the situation with too frequent state transitions, which on average would be comparable to a system’s evolution on the adiabatic potential energy surface (PES) in Ehrenfest-like dynamics.

    One potential difficulty of the LZSH method is its reliance on diabatic properties, which are not always readily available. Most electronic structure packages yield the adiabatic properties, leaving one to take an additional ad hoc diabatization step, which may be nonunique. A number of authors have undertaken this path. For instance, Yu et al. (30) developed a diabatization algorithm to obtain the diabatic properties at the avoided crossing region and utilized it within the Zhu-Nakamura generalization (31) of the LZ state transition probabilities to study photoisomerization in azobenzene. In a later development, Hanasaki et al. (32) improved the diabatization algorithm of Yu et al. for the regions of smoothly varying energy gaps. It should be noted that although the Zhu-Nakamura formulation is more general than LZ, it requires excited state energy gradients, which may be unavailable or computationally intractable for extended systems. Thus, the methods of Yu and Hanasaki may be limited to relatively small systems. Such formulations would also be incompatible with the philosophy of the NBRA-based methods. In addition, the application of the Zhu-Nakamura formula requires knowing the topology of multidimensional seams for the diabatic PES crossings, which may be challenging to obtain for large systems. Keeping in mind our goal of having an inexpensive approach that would require only adiabatic properties and would not require derivatives of the wave function, we choose to build a methodology starting with the Belyaev and Lebedev reformulation of the LZ hopping probabilities. (33)

    In 2011, Belyaev and Lebedev (BL) demonstrated how the original LZ formula could be expressed in terms of only adiabatic properties, such as energy gaps and their time derivatives. (33) They demonstrated the utility of the method by computing scattering probabilities for the inelastic collision of Na + H in the gas phase. Their LZ-based calculations showed reasonable agreement with the fully quantum mechanical results as long as one compares the average values of the cross sections as a function of the angular momentum quantum number. The method could not resolve the oscillatory features observed in the fully quantum calculations. Later, Xie et al. (34) used the BL surface hopping (BLSH) approach to study the photodissociation of phenol. They demonstrated that the BLSH method compared well to reference FSSH and fully quantum calculations. Analogously to Belyaev and Lebedev’s results, they observed that while the behavior of the individual BLSH trajectories was different from that of the FSSH trajectories, the results averaged over the swarms of trajectories were comparable.

    In this work, we report the development of a new TSH-like approach that relies on the BL formulation of the LZ state transition probabilities and utilizes it within the NBRA framework of NA-MD. Although formulated in terms of stochastic surface hops, the method is fully deterministic and does not require an extensive sampling of stochastic trajectories, which provides an additional significant computational savings critical to the express modeling of NA-MD in large systems. The resulting NBRA-BL-NA-MD scheme enables the modeling of nonradiative charge carrier cooling in dense manifolds containing hundreds of electronic states, as is typical in nanoscale clusters. The approach is advantageous in a number of ways. First, it requires only energy levels of the involved states as a function of time, which is the typical type of data used in NBRA-NA-MD calculations anyway. Such calculations can in principle be made using any level of electronic structure theory, which opens an avenue for systematically refining calculations made at a lower level of theory with more sophisticated and accurate approaches, such as those yielding many-body states. Second, derivatives of the wave function are not needed, which both enables a greater range of electronic structure packages that can be used and helps save computational time. The calculation of NACs may become an increasingly demanding task as the size of the systems increases. In the present approach, the calculation of the state transition probabilities matrix would scale only quadratically, N2, with the number of states, N, and only a very small prefactor. The calculation of NACs would require matrix–matrix multiplications and would scale cubically, N3, and would have a larger prefactor. The NAC calculations become even more challenging if one is to use many-body wave functions to compute NACs. The N3 scaling could be increased by at least a factor of M2, where M is the number of determinants in the many-body wave function. The approach herein needs only adiabatic energies and therefore avoids the need for diabatization. Third, inheriting the benefits of the LZ formula, the method naturally accounts for decoherence effects, which may become challenging to properly account for in an ad-hoc fashion in regimes of densely packed states. Therefore, the NBRA formulation makes all of the properties one-parametric (time), which reduces the complex task of searching for the multidimensional crossing seams to the trivial task of finding the minima in energy gaps along the precomputed trajectories.

    Our approach is formulated as follows. According to Belyaev and Lebedev, (33) the hopping probability is computed as

    PijBL=exp(−π2ℏZij3Z¨ij)
    (1)
    Here, Zij= |Ei – Ej|, is the magnitude of the energy gap between the adiabatic states with energies Ei and Ej, and Z¨ij=∂2Zij∂t2 is the second time derivative of the energy gap magnitude. A critical point in our scheme is to determine the time derivatives of Zij. In addition, eq 1 should be evaluated at the minimum in the energy gap Zij and not at any arbitrary point. As is typical for many NBRA-type calculation schemes, the energy gaps Zij for all pairs of states are known for the duration of precomputed nuclear trajectories at a given set of discrete time points, {tn, n = 1, K}. In order to take the second-order time derivatives and to determine the location of the energy gap minima, we need Zij to be a sufficiently smooth function of time, Zij(t). We choose to represent Zij(t) via an N-point (N ≤ K) Lagrange interpolation:
    Zij(t)=∑n=1NZij(tn)∏m=1,m≠nN(t−tmtn−tm)
    (2)
    For any particular choice of N, the local minimum for a given interval can be found via standard differentiation. In the case of multiple roots (as is likely to occur when choosing large values of N), one can restrict the search for the minima to be in the interval [tn−[N2],tn+[N2]] for each “middle” time step n. Here, brackets in the subscript denote taking the integer of the corresponding number. In the current implementation, we find it convenient to work with the N = 3-point Lagrange interpolation scheme, in which only three consecutive values of the energy gap are considered in evaluating eq 1, Zij(tn – Δt), Zij(tn), and Zij(tn + Δt). If the minimum of Zij does exist in this interval, then its location is determined unambiguously as
    tmin=(Zij(tn−Δt)−Zij(tn+Δt))Δt2(Zij(tn−Δt)−2Zij(tn)+Zij(tn+Δt))
    (3)

    By evaluating eq 1 at the minimum point, eq 3, one finds (Supporting Information, Section A) the final expression:

    Pi→jBL,proposal(t,t+Δt)=exp(−π2ℏΔtZij3(tmin)Zij(tn−Δt)−2Zij(tn)+Zij(tn+Δt))
    (4)

    The probabilities in eq 4 do not distinguish the transitions up and down in energy and therefore cannot properly partition energy between electronic and nuclear degrees of freedom on their own. In the present formulation, we account for this effect by accepting the transitions up in energy with the Boltzmann factor probability, as commonly used within the NBRA formulations of NA-MD: (35,36)

    Pi→jacceptance(t,t+Δt)={1,Ei≥Ejexp(−Zij(tmin)kBT),Ei<Ej
    (5)
    Here, kB is the Boltzmann constant, and T is the effective bath temperature. Also, note that the minimal gap is used in the Boltzmann factor calculation, since such a correction would be consistent with the notion that the transition is assumed to occur at time tmin. As shown by eq 5, the probability to transition downward in energy is not scaled by the Boltzmann factor. Recently, we have demonstrated that such a hop acceptance probability allows reaching the correct equilibrium populations within the NBRA framework, but only when decoherence is accounted for. (37)

    The overall hops are determined by the interplay of the hop proposal and hop acceptance probabilities, eqs 4 and 5:

    Pi→jBL(t,t+Δt)=Pi→jBL,proposal(t,t+Δt)Pi→jacceptance(t,t+Δt)
    (6)

    In practical calculations, especially in the case of large densities of states, one may face a situation where the sum of Pi→jBL over all target states, j, may exceed 1. Indeed, for very small energy gaps (which is the case for dense manifolds of states), the argument of the exponent, in both eqs 4 and 5, may be approaching zero and therefore the hopping probabilities may be approaching 1. If there are several energetically close states, the sum, ∑j≠iPi→jBL can easily exceed 1. In such situations, the probability to stay in the source state, 1−∑j≠iPi→jBL, would be negative. In the spirit of the FSSH methodology, such situations are handled as follows. If ∑j≠iPi→jBL<1, then the probabilities to hop into any of the target states, j, are taken directly as Pi→jBL and the probability to stay in the source state is given by 1−∑j≠iPi→jBL. If ∑j≠iPi→jBL≥1, then the probabilities to hop into any of the target states, j, are taken as Pi→jBL∑k≠iPi→kBL, and the probability to stay in the source state is taken to be 0. This approach ensures that the relative rates of transitioning via different channels are independent of the total flux out of the source state and that the total probability to leave or stay on each source state remains 1 all the time.

    One can choose to use the matrix PBL constructed according to eq 6 and adapted as described above as a matrix of hopping probabilities within the standard TSH algorithm. However, since within the NBRA the electronic transitions do not affect the following evolution of nuclei, the matrices PBL(t, t + Δt) stay unchanged for all stochastic realizations of the surface hopping. Therefore, instead of computing thousands of such hopping trajectories, we use the matrix PBL as the Markov process transition matrix to directly obtain the time evolution of the state populations, P(t) = (p0(t), p1(t), ..., pN–1(t))T:

    P(t+Δt)=PBL(t,t+Δt)⁡P(t)
    (7)

    Such an approach could in principle be used with other NBRA-like approaches (38,39) as long as there are no additional mechanisms that can introduce branching, such as those present within certain decoherence schemes. (40,41) In this work, the use of a Markov process transition matrix reduces the computational efforts of the NA dynamics step enormously, allowing one to complete the dynamics in a matter of a few minutes on a single processor, provided the Markov-state transition matrices, P, have been precomputed.

    The NBRA-BL-NA-MD scheme is implemented within the open-source Libra (42) package, which is used for executing and analyzing the NA-MD calculations. In particular, all of the calculations reported in this work as well as the implementation of the current methodology are available in the Libra versions starting from v4.4.0. (43) The details of the simulations are given in Section B of the Supporting Information document.

    We apply the developed NBRA-BL-NA-MD methodology to study the dependence of nonradiative decay time scales for a series of silicon NCs on their size and surface termination type. Previous computational studies (44,45) suggest that the radiative and nonradiative recombination of the charge carriers in Si QDs occurs much more slowly than does the hot electron energy relaxation within the conduction band studied in this work. Thus, these additional pathways can be neglected. Other effects such as multiple exciton generation become important for initial excitation energies exceeding twice the system’s band gap. (46,47) Auger-like processes are facilitated by a weak dielectric screening of the electrons, which leads to strong Coulombic interactions. This effect is prevalent in metallic nanoparticles and is of less concern for semiconducting nanoparticles such as the Si NCs considered in this work. Finally, modeling the dynamics of multiple exciton generation and Auger scattering would require a much more sophisticated many-body description of the electronic states. Such a dynamical methodology is currently out of practical reach, and further progress in this direction would be highly desirable.

    We consider NCs with core sizes of approximately 0.8, 1.5, and 2.2 nm, as defined by the greatest distance between the NCs core atoms of Si (Figure 1a–c) in the optimized NCs. The NCs expand slightly during their NVT nuclear dynamics. For each NC size, we consider the relaxation of a hot electron from three initial excitation energies: 1, 2, and 3 eV above the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). In the present work, the electronic states are obtained from the density functional tight-binding (DFTB) (48) calculations and are approximated by one-electron molecular orbitals, as is common to do when modeling such complex systems. (15,16,49−51) The electronic structure calculations are performed within the very efficient DFTB+ package, (52) allowing one to complete the entirety of the NA-MD calculations for the largest systems considered in only just a few days. In comparison, the DFT- and NAC-based implementations of NA-MD (38,39) may require weeks to complete, even for smaller systems. Importantly, it should be remembered that eq 1 is general, so one may further improve the current methodology by considering the energies of many-body states (or other levels of approximation in general) by using them in eq 1. In order to be able to properly describe the cooling dynamics, the number of electronic states needed to describe such processes increases rapidly both with the systems’ size and the initial excitation energy level (Figure 1). For example, approximately 300 states are needed to model hot electron cooling in our largest NC with a diameter of approximately 2.2 nm (Figure 1c,f). We also consider hydrogen and fluorine atoms to be the surface termination options for the Si NCs.

    We quantify the dynamics of the hot electron cooling by analyzing the time-dependent excess electronic energy, which is computed as

    ⟨E⟩(t)=∑i⟨pi(t)⁡Ei(R(t))⟩T
    (8)
    Here, pi(t) represents the time-dependent state populations as determined by propagating eq 7, and Ei(R(t)) represents the energies of the electronic states, parametrically dependent on the nuclear geometries sampled by the NVT-MD procedure. The ⟨*⟩T notation indicates averaging over distinct nuclear trajectories and reflects a thermal sampling of initial conditions. The decay of the excess electronic energy is fitted to the stretched–compressed exponential function of the form
    E(t,E0)=E0⁡exp(−(tτ)β)+B
    (9)

    The fitting function, eq 9, was utilized in the works of Wong et al. (19) for the characterization of the hot electron relaxation in similar systems. The only distinction of our fitting function is the presence of additional fitting parameter B, which is needed to account for the situation of an incomplete energy decay due to the presence of low-energy bottlenecks. Physically, the stretched/compressed exponential fitting function accounts for changes in the type of kinetics occurring at different densities of states. For instance, for high densities of states, the coherent dynamics may play a notable role in the overall kinetics. The relaxation dynamics would then follow the cosine (early part of it) or Gaussian curves. On the contrary, for lower densities of states, decoherence becomes dominant, changing the dynamic regime to a single-exponential one. The stretched/compressed fitting curve is meant to accommodate both of these regimes.     On the basis of our NA-MD calculations (Table 1, Figure S1), we conclude that the decay of excess electronic excitation energy is generally faster (a) in larger Si NCs (Figure S1, any column) and (b) in H-terminated Si NCs as opposed to F-terminated ones (Figure S1, red lines as opposed to blue ones). The dependence of the energy relaxation time scales on the initial excitation energy level is nonmonotonous. Both of the observed trends (dependence on the NC’s size and surface termination type) are consistent with the expectations from Fermi’s golden rule (FGR), according to which the rates of quantum transitions are proportional to the density of the acceptor states at the donor energy level. Mathematically, the larger densities of states (DOS’s) imply that the energy gaps between pairs of adjacent states are smaller than for the lower DOS, which directly makes the proposed hop probabilities larger according to eq 4. Indeed, the density of electronic states increases in larger NCs, and when the NC surface is terminated with H atoms, as supported by the computed distributions of the energy gaps between adjacent states, Zi,i+1= |Ei – Ei+1| (Figure 2). For the smallest NC considered, the Zi,i+1 values can vary in an interval as large as 1 eV (Figure 2a,b). For both surface atom termination types, the distributions are bimodal with the two maxima located at 0.05–0.1 and 0.4 eV for 0.8 NC nm. The positions of the maxima shift toward smaller values for the larger, 1.5 nm NC (Figure 2c,d). In addition, the width (full width at half-maximum, fwhm) of these subdistributions decreases from approximately 0.1 and 0.4 eV in 0.8 nm NCs to nearly 0.05 and 0.1 eV in the 1.5 nm NC. Thus, the instantaneous energy gaps, Zi,i+1(t), can be substantially larger in smaller NCs than in larger ones, contributing to the decreased energy relaxation rates in smaller NCs. Comparing H- and F-terminated NCs, one can observe that the relative populations of the subdistributions of Zi,i+1 are different in the two groups of the systems. The occurrence of the higher energy gap is more probable in the F-terminated systems than in the H-terminated ones, contributing to slower energy relaxation in the F-terminated NCs.

    The second observed trend in the computed energy relaxation rates can also be rationalized by the differences in energy gap fluctuations in H-terminated vs F-terminated Si NCs. Faster energy gap fluctuations lead to larger time derivatives, ∂2Zij∂t2. According to eq 4, larger time derivatives lead to larger proposed hop probabilities. The energy-level fluctuations are driven by the dynamics of the nuclei. One can expect that the lighter and faster-moving H atoms can cause more frequent perturbations of the electronic energy levels and therefore lead to faster energy gap fluctuations than heavier F atoms. Such faster fluctuations of energy levels in the H-terminated Si NCs would cause faster electronic transitions in these systems than in the F-terminated systems. Thus, one can hypothesize that the main reason for the observed difference in the dynamics of H- and F-terminated systems is the difference in masses of the H and F atoms. To test this hypothesis, we consider the dynamics in the smallest (0.8 nm) Si NC terminated with either H or F atoms with either regular (mH = 1 amu and mF = 19 amu) or modified (mH = 19 amu and mF = 1 amu) masses (Figure 3).

    As our NA-MD calculations suggest (Figure 3), the mass of the surface-terminating atom is a dominant factor that affects the decay kinetics of the excited electrons. When increasing the mass of hydrogen to be that of fluorine, we observe an increase in the decay time (Figure 3a,b). Depending on the initial excitation energy, the dynamics slows down by factors of 3.7 (initial excitation to 1 eV) and 3.9 (3 eV) when changing the mass of H from 1 to 19 amu. Analogous effects are observed when changing the mass of F from 1 amu to its regular value of 19 amu (Figure 3c,d). The slow-down ratios are 5.4 (1 eV)–8.2 (3 eV) in this case. One can also look at the effects caused by the changed chemical identity of the surface atoms while keeping their masses constant. When changing H to F, one obtains slow-down factors of 2.3 (3 eV)–5.4 (1 eV) for a surface atom mass of 1 amu and 4.8 (3 eV)–8.1 (1 eV) for a surface atom mass of 19 amu. We conclude that both the chemistry of the surface termination atoms and their masses are equally important on average when considering various initial excitation energies. However, we observe a clear trend that for higher-energy excitations the mass becomes the dominant factor, whereas for lower initial excitation energies the chemical identity of the surface termination atoms has a stronger influence on the energy relaxation dynamics.

    To further investigate the effect of the capping atoms’ mass and chemical identity on the decay of excess excitation energy, we compute the influence spectra for the four types of surface termination/mass choices considered above. The influence spectra (53,54) are computed as the Fourier transforms of the autocorrelation of the energy gap fluctuations for all pairs of adjacent states:

    I(ω)=∑i|FT[⟨δZi,i+1(0)⁡δZi,i+1(t)⟩T]|2
    (10)
    The influence spectra show the characteristic frequencies for each energy gap fluctuation. These frequencies may be interpreted as the nuclear modes that couple each particular pair of states and drive the NA transitions between them. We have also assigned the modes observed in the influence spectrum to the modes obtained from the normal-mode analysis (Supporting Information, Section E). As is expected and as was alluded to earlier, the increase in the surface atoms’ masses lowers the characteristic frequencies that drive the corresponding NA transitions. For instance, for H-terminated NCs, the dominant peaks are at about 250 cm–1 for mH = 1 amu (Figure 4a). The change in the mass to mH = 19 amu shifts the dominant peak toward a lower frequency of about 100 cm–1 (Figure 4b). Although the current NA-MD methodology does not directly involve NACs, the changes in the energy levels are intimately related to the changes in the wave functions’ properties. (55) As such, one may analyze the observed trends using the machinery involving NACs. According to this analysis, the 2.5-fold decrease in the frequencies that drive NA transitions is equivalent to a 2.5-fold decrease in the average NAC magnitude. Recalling that the rates of NA transitions depend quadratically on the NAC magnitudes, one would expect an approximately 6-fold slowdown of the electron cooling. This result is on par with our calculations that suggest approximately a 4-fold slowdown (e.g., Figure 3a,b).

    The analysis of the dynamics in F-terminated Si NCs follows straightforwardly from the H-terminated NCs, although the influence spectra are more complicated (Figure 4c,d). For the F atoms with mass mF = 1 amu. (Figure 4c), the dominant peak is found to be near 400 cm–1. The spectrum contains a higher-energy frequency mode closer to 500 cm–1. The relative intensity of the modes at 400 cm–1 for the F atoms with mass mF = 1 amu red shifts upon the mass increase to mF = 19 amu to approximately 150 cm–1 (Figure 4d). Additionally, many new modes in the frequency range of approximately 50 to 200 cm–1 emerge upon the increase in fluorine’s mass. At the same time, the mode closer to 500 cm–1 for mF = 1 amu slightly blue shifts as the mass is increased. We find it informative to consider the overall shifts of the spectral centers of mass rather than the positions of individual peaks of the influence spectra. The centers of mass change from approximately 400 to 150 cm–1 upon the F mass reduction from mF = 19 to 1 amu. Following the NAC argument above, one would expect an approximately (400150)2≈7.1-fold acceleration of the hot carriers’ relaxation caused by such a mass change. This values indeed agrees very well with the results of our direct calculations, suggesting the mass increase of F would result in a 5.4 (1 eV)–8.2 (3 eV)-fold reduction in the energy relaxation rates.

    The analysis of the influence spectra provides a way to interpret the enhanced sensitivity of the decay dynamics in the F-terminated Si NCs with respect to the choice of the capping atom masses in relation to such dynamics in the H-terminated systems. As we argue above, the qualitative origin of such a sensitivity difference may be boiled down to the types of phonons present in each system and under each dynamic condition (lighter, faster-moving atoms vs heavier, slower-moving atoms). In the F-terminated NCs, there are two distinct groups of phonons (at both high and low frequencies) that are well coupled to electronic transitions across the unoccupied molecular orbitals (UMOs), whereas in the H-terminated NCs, only the low-frequency modes are the main “drivers” of the corresponding electronic transitions. To gain further insight into the origin of the observed differences in the influence spectra, we analyze the orbital charge densities in the H- and F-terminated Si NCs (Figure 5). The charge density of the UMOs in the H-terminated NCs has notable surface localization (Figure 5a–c), whereas in the F-terminated NCs, it has both the “core” and surface localization character (Figure 5d–f). The distinction between the predominantly surface localization in the H-terminated NCs and the mixed core/surface localization in the F-terminated NCs becomes more apparent for larger Si NCs (Supporting Information, Section D). We conclude that the presence of the two groups of modes in the influence spectrum for F-terminated NCs can be attributed to the mixed surface/core localization of UMOs in these systems, whereas the presence of mainly only one group of peaks in the influence spectra of H-terminated NCs can be attributed to the predominant surface localization of UMOs in these systems. It is also logical to conclude that the lower-frequency modes (present in both H- and F-terminated NCs) are mainly coupled to the surface states, whereas the higher-frequency modes (present more dominantly in the F-terminated NCs) are coupled to the “core” states.

    The magnitudes of the time scales computed using our current approach are worth discussing. Our primary references are the earlier studies of Reeves et al. (56) and Wong et al., (19) who explored the nonadiabatic dynamics of hot electron cooling in some H- and F-terminated NCs using the conventional decoherence-corrected FSSH approach formulated within the NBRA framework. In the initial study on 1.5 nm Si NCs (Si66H40 and Si66F40), Reeves et al. (56) found that the hot electron relaxation proceeded faster in the H-terminated Si NCs than in F-terminated NCs. They attributed this effect to the presence of UMO regions with very small NACs in the F-terminated NCs, which created bottlenecks in the decay dynamics. They demonstrated that such regions were not very pronounced in the H-terminated NCs. Considering an initial excitation of roughly 3 eV above the LUMO, they obtain decay times for the subsequent relaxation on the order of 400 and 700 fs for the H- and F-terminated NCs, respectively. In a follow-up study, Wong et al. (19) demonstrated that hot electron relaxation in F-terminated Si NCs was faster in the larger systems (Si220F120, 2.2 nm vs Si66F40, 1.5 nm). For instance, for the initial excitation placed 3 eV above the LUMO energy level, the relaxation time scales were 623 fs in the 2.2 nm NC and 1136 fs in the 1.5 nm NC. The authors also demonstrated that decoherence correction slowed down the relaxation dynamics, but not by more than a factor of 2.

    Our calculations agree with the trends reported both by Reeves et al. (56) and by Wong et al. (19) Similar to Reeves et al., (56) we observe slower relaxation in F-terminated NCs. We also observe the generally larger pairwise energy gaps in the F-terminated NCs, consistent with the suggestion of gap-induced bottlenecks caused by the surface fluorination. Similar to Wong et al., (19) we observe faster relaxation in larger NCs. The energy relaxation time scales obtained in our calculations are comparable in the order of magnitude to the numbers reported in the above-mentioned works. Most notably, the time scales we obtain for the smallest and largest NCs bracket the time scales reported by Wong et al. (Table 1). The differences can be attributed to many factors, such as the difference in the NCs’ composition, the NCs’ surface topology, and the electronic structure and nuclear dynamics methods used and somewhat to the different fitting functions used. First, our 1.5 nm NCs have the Si105X94 (X = H, F) stoichiometry as opposed to the Si66X40 of Reeves and Wong. Thus, our NCs contains nearly 80 more Si and F/H atoms combined. Second, the surface of our clusters contains Si atoms of various coordinations, including the silyl moieties on the surface of the smallest NC used. Such groups are likely to be present in realistic Si NCs. At the same time, it has been suggested that they, as well as other defects such as dangling bonds and surface oxidation, can facilitate and accelerate the nonadiabatic relaxation dynamics in comparison to more “idealized” NC surface-termination geometries. (57−59) Third, we utilize the SCC-DFTB (52) methodology for both producing nuclear trajectories and obtaining orbital energies, whereas both Reeves and Wong utilized the PBE (60) approach for these types of calculations. Although there is no immediate answer to which of the approaches is more accurate, it is expected that such differences may affect the computed dynamics. (14,55,61) Finally, unlike the works of Reeves et al. and Wong et al., our calculations suggest the presence of very slowly decaying states, which is the reason that a shift parameter was added to the stretched–compressed exponential fitting function. Accounting for such a slower relaxation component could increase the averaged relaxation times to be comparable to or even larger than the reported numbers. However, we believe that such averaging would be meaningless since the disparity in the decay dynamics may indicate a different physical nature of the involved states. For instance, these could be various defect states with much larger lifetimes, in particular, the LUMO + 1 state. Their prolonged lifetimes are likely explained by the LUMO – LUMO + 1 gap that is notably larger than the LUMO + n – LUMO + n + 1 gaps.

    In passing, we comment further on the applicability of the developed method. The prerequisite of the LZ approach is the linear dependence of the diabatic energy gap on time near the diabatic crossing region. Naturally, this condition is best satisfied when the adiabatic gaps are small. Indeed, if the adiabatic levels are well-separated, then their respective diabatic curves may cross in a nonlinear way, thus invalidating the prerequisite of the LZ approach. If the diabatic profiles can be considered to be linear, then their slopes determined from the corresponding adiabatic curves may have high dispersion, thus deteriorating the accuracy of the method. Our experience with some typical condensed-matter systems with adiabatic states separated by a noticeable gap (>0.5–1.0 eV) yields extremely small nonadiabatic transition rates, sometimes well below those reported by experiments. A much more detailed investigation of this question, potentially with the use of model systems, would be very useful. At this point, our recommendation is that the NBRA-BL-NA-MD approach is best suited for studying excited-state dynamics in dense manifolds of states, where energy gaps are typically below 0.1–0.2 eV.

    In summary, we report a new NBRA-BL-NA-MD methodology that leverages a recent reformulation of the Landau–Zener formula by Belyaev and Lebedev, the neglect of the back-reaction approximation, and a highly efficient SCC-DFTB approach to nuclear dynamics and electronic structure calculations on extended systems. The resulting scheme allowed us to study hot electron relaxation in H- and F-terminated Si NCs of various sizes, ranging in diameter from 0.8 to 2.2 nm and containing nearly 600 atoms. Our comprehensive study addresses both the role of the NC’s surface termination chemistry and the NC size and disentangles the role of the changed mass and electronegativity of the surface termination atoms. In agreement with the earlier works by Reeves et al. (56) and Wong et al., (19) our calculations suggest that hot electrons decay slower in the F-terminated NCs than in the H-terminated NCs and that the decay rates increase with the NC’s size. The computed time scales vary from 59 to 182 fs in H-terminated Si NCs and from 118 to 3025 fs in F-terminated Si NCs for all of the initial electronic excitations and NC sizes considered. The slower dynamics of the hot electrons in the F-terminated NCs is attributed to both the slower nuclear vibrations coupled to the corresponding electronic transitions and to the larger average energy gaps in the F-terminated NCs as opposed to the H-terminated ones. We find that both the ligand mass and chemical identity are equally important in determining the time scales for electronic energy relaxation, but only in the average sense (considering a range of initial excitation energies). We find that the mass of the surface termination atoms becomes more dominant in modulating hot electron relaxation rates for the higher-energy initial excitation energies. We also find a greater sensitivity of the computed energy relaxation time scale to the ligand’s mass change in the F-terminated NCs, which is attributed to the presence of additional higher-frequency modes coupled to electronic transitions, in contrast to H-terminated systems. We explain the two groups of vibrational modes found in the computed influence spectra by the presence of both core- and surface-localized states. In the F-terminated Si NCs, both types of states are present, whereas in the H-terminated NCs, the states are predominantly the surface-localized ones. Together with the ultrafast decay components in the studied Si NCs, we observe long-lived charge carrier energy decay components, which are the most pronounced in smaller NCs. Such slow decay channels are attributed to the increased UMO energy gaps (especially between LUMO + 1 and LUMO), which can act as the relaxation bottlenecks. The LUMO + 1 and LUMO energy gap is pronounced in the larger NCs, serving as the largest bottleneck of the decay for these systems.

        We examine the extent to which the exchange-correlation (XC) approxn. influences modeling interfacial charge transfer using fewest-switches surface hopping (FSSH) simulations within the single-particle description. A heterogeneous interface between a lithium ion and an extended boron-nitride sheet was considered here, being an extreme case in which wave function localization and energy level alignments are highly sensitive to the XC approxn. The PBE0 hybrid XC approxn. yields nonadiabatic couplings (NACs) that are significantly smaller than the values obtained from the PBE-GGA approxn. by an order of magnitude for localized electronic states.
