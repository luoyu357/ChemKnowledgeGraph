    Abstract

    Binodal curves for aqueous two-phase systems (ATPS) polyethylene glycol 1500 (PEG1500)–sodium sulfate–water with additions of sodium chloride and hydrochloric or sulfuric acids were determined by turbidimetric titration and analysis of coexisting phases. The results are compared with published data for the ATPS PEG1500–sodium sulfate–water without additions, and the influence of additions on the width of the two-phase field, tie-lines length, and slopes was traced. The influence of initial concentration of palladium, time of phase contact, pH, concentration of sodium chloride and sodium sulfate on the extraction of palladium(II) was studied. The coefficients of distribution of palladium(II) between the phases of ATPS were measured as a function of the concentrations of sodium chloride (0.1, 1.0, and 2.0 mol·L–1) and pH values. At lower concentrations of Cl– ions Pd(II) is extracted as a mixture of ions [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− and [PdCl4]2–, whereas the growth of C(Cl–) leads to prevalence of the latter, which has poorer extraction properties. Maximal values of the distribution coefficient DPd of about 9.5 ± 0.75 were achieved in the ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–0.1 M HCl and PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + 0.1 M NaCl).

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Palladium is widely used in various industries such as chemistry, electrical engineering, and hydrogen energetics and as a catalyst in internal combustion engines. Palladium compounds are also used in medicine as antitumor drugs. (1) Accounting for growing prices for this metal, both recovering palladium from industrial waste and obtaining pure chemical reagents based on palladium are particularly urgent. Various methods such as electrochemical deposition, chemical precipitation and ion exchange are used to recover palladium from scrap. (2−4) However, all these are multistage and complicated, and suffer from low efficiency and large energy consumption. One of the simplest and most effective methods for recovering palladium is solvent extraction. However, the organic solvents such as toluene and kerosene (5,6) used in the extraction are poisonous, flammable, and often carcinogenic and harmful to the environment. Aqueous two-phase systems (ATPS) are free from these disadvantages. These are successfully used for separating, purifying, and analyzing various substances: biological (7−9) and organic, (10,11) as well as metal ions. (12−15)
    Metals can be extracted into the polymer phase in ATPS only as anions such as MoO42– or VO3–. (16,17) If the metal does not form oxyanions, some inorganic or organic ligands forming anionic complexes should be added. The efficiency of such a process is determined by the stability of the extracted complex and degree of ion hydratation. (18) For this reason, metal extraction usually increases with increasing concentration of the extractant. (12,13)
    Besides the nature of the extracted compounds, the extraction ability of ATPS depends on such factors as molecular weight of the polymer, (18) the nature of phase-forming salt, (13,17,19) and the ratio of components in the polymer–salt–water system. (19,20) The interphase distribution of metals depends also on pH, since the acidity changes both composition of extracted metal compounds (16,17,20,21) and the width of heterogeneous region in ATPS. (18,22) The effects of the addition of various ions and of the acidity on phase equilibria in ATPS were studied in refs (23−27). Both increase of concentration of inorganic and organic salts (23,24) and increase of pH (25−27) expanded the heterogeneous region in the ATPS.
    Palladium is extracted routinely from hydrochloric acid solutions as complex anions formed by chloride. The complex [PdCl4]2– is stable at chloride ion concentrations above 0.5 mol·L–1. At lower concentrations of Cl–, complexes of various compositions [Pd(H2O)xCl4–x](2–x)– are formed. (28) To prevent hydrolysis of palladium(II), the pH should be less than 4. (29) High values of acidity and concentration of chloride ions needed for extraction will definitely affect the phase equilibria in ATPS.
    The present study is devoted to investigation of the influence of acidity and concentration of chloride ions on the phase formation and interphase distribution of palladium(II) in ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–water. The choice of ATPS is motivated by the relatively low content of both phase-forming salt and polymer in resulting ATPS. This reduces the consumption of reagents. Moreover, the resulting PEG enriched phase contains less salt and has lower viscosity due to low molecular weight of the PEG. This facilitates subsequent re-extraction of palladium from that phase. To separate the effects of acidity and of concentration of chloride ions we used two sources of Cl– (hydrochloric acid and sodium chloride) and two acids (sulfuric and hydrochloric). The effect of experimental details like the amount of added sodium chloride, the concentration of sodium sulfate, and pH on the efficiency of palladium extraction is also studied.

    2.2. Binodal Curves

    Binodal curves were plotted as a function of concentration of chloride ion and pH by turbidimetric titration (30) in a thermostatic bath at 298 K. Stock solutions of PEG1500 with concentrations 50.0 or 55 wt % and of sodium sulfate with concentrations 20 or 22.5 wt % were prepared using the solutions of sodium chloride (0.10, 1.00, and 2.00 mol·L–1), sulfuric acid (0.05, 0.50 mol·L–1), or hydrochloric acid (0.10 and 1.00 mol·L–1) as solvents. In both cases higher concentrations of salt and polymer solutions were used at higher acid concentrations. The weighing of salt and polymer was performed using analytical scales (E10640, OHAUS, Switzerland) with an accuracy of ±0.0001 g.

    2.3. Analysis of Equilibrated Phases

    The compositions of the equilibrium phases were determined for each binodal curve. To prepare a two-phase system, the required amounts of sodium sulfate and the polymer were dissolved in solutions of sulfuric or hydrochloric acid containing a certain amount of sodium chloride. Solutions weighing in total 40 to 50 g were mixed in a glass with a magnetic stirrer for an hour. Then, the emulsion was poured into a separatory funnel, where it was kept for at least 24 h. Achieving the equilibrium state was confirmed by the absence of turbidity in both the upper and lower phases, as well as by constancy of phase volumes. In addition, the polymer and salt phases were analyzed also after 24 and 36 h of contact. The concentrations of chloride, sulfate, and sodium ions proved to be very close. The temperature was fixed with an accuracy of ±0.5 K using a thermostat. After separation, the phases were analyzed for concentrations of Na+, SO42–, and Cl– ions.
    The sodium concentration was determined in both upper and lower phases using an ICP-AES 720 spectrometer (Agilent Technologies, USA). (33) An SPS3 autosampler was used for sample introduction. A Sc (20 mg/L) internal standard solution was added online to increase the accuracy of measurements. Results were collected and processed with ICP Expert II software 2.0.5 (Agilent Technologies). All samples were diluted 500 times.
    Concentrations of chloride and sulfate ions were determined only in the lower phase by ion chromatography with suppressed conductivity. (34) A Dionex ICS 1600 ion chromatography system (Dionex part of Thermo Scientific, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) equipped with an isocratic pump and a conductivity detector with a suppressor was used for ion chromatography. Data acquisition and processing were controlled by Chromeleon 7.0 (Dionex part of Thermo Scientific, Sunnyvale, CA, USA).     3.1. Effect of Sodium Chloride, Hydrochloric and Sulfuric Acids on Phase Equilibria in ATPS

In particular, at w(PEG1500) = 15 wt % and C(HCl) = 0.1 mol·L–1, the solubility of sodium sulfate is 7.4, 6.3, and 5.7 wt % correspondingly at 0.1, 1.0, and 2.0 mol·L–1 of chloride.     As can be seen from Figures 1 and 2, an increase in the concentration of acids leads to a shrinking of the two-phase region. However, sulfuric acid and hydrochloric acid have a different effect on phase equilibria. Thus, when increasing the concentration of hydrochloric acid from 0.1 to 1.0 mol·L–1, the solubility of sodium sulfate at a polymer content of 20 wt % grows from 5.8 to 11.3 wt %. On the other hand, an analogous growth of concentration of sulfuric acid from 0.05 to 0.50 mol·L–1 (at C(NaCl) = 0.1 mol·L–1), causes much less change of the solubility of Na2SO4 at the same polymer content, from 5.39 to 7.4 wt %.
    An increase of the solubility with the increasing concentration of acids may be related to the protonization of both the sulfate ion and the polyethylene glycol. Protonization of the C–O–C group of PEG increases the hydrophilic character (19) of the polymer and its solubility in the aqueous salt-rich phase, as well as the water solubility in the polymer phase. Formation of the less hydrophilic HSO4– ion leads to a decrease in the salting-out ability of the sulfate ion. Hydrochloric acid demonstrates both effects, so an increase of its concentration has a stronger effect on the phase equilibria in the PEG1500–Na2SO4–water system. On the other hand, the dissociation of sulfuric acid on the second stage is not so strong, and the presence of sulfate ions from Na2SO4 further suppresses it. For this reason, an increase in the concentration of sulfuric acid does not cause as strong a growth of the concentration of H+ ions as the addition of hydrochloric acid does. Moreover, addition of the latter may lead to a noticeable reduction of the concentration of sulfate ions and, as a result, the reduction of the salting-out effect of Na2SO4 in the formation of ATPS.

    3.2. The Palladium Extraction in Aqueous Two-Phase System PEG1500–Na2SO4–Water

    The effects of some experimental parameters, such as time of phase contact, initial palladium concentration, system component concentration, sodium chloride concentration, and pH on the palladium distribution coefficient (DPd2+) in an aqueous two-phase system PEG1500–Na2SO4–(HCl/H2SO4 + NaCl) were studied.
    First, the necessary time to achieve the extraction equilibrium was determined. To this end, the dependence of distribution coefficient of palladium in the ATPS PEG1500 (16 wt %)–Na2SO4 (8.6 wt %)–0.1 M HCl system from the time of phase contact was tested. The experiment time was 5 to 60 min, and the initial concentration of palladium was 0.001 mol·L–1. After 5 min of mixing, the palladium distribution coefficient reached 4.16 and remained essentially unchanged with a further increase in time of extraction. Thus, in all subsequent extraction studies, the time of extraction was fixed on 5 min.
    The effect of the initial concentration of palladium(II) in the concentration range of 0.0002–0.0010 mol·L–1 on its extraction from its solutions in hydrochloric and sulfuric acids with different contents of chloride ions was studied. Figure 6 shows typical isotherms of palladium(II) ions extraction in ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–(1 M HCl + 1 M NaCl) and PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + 0.1 M NaCl). These systems differ as much as possible in the compositions of equilibrium phases, acidity, and sodium chloride content, so they should have the most different extraction ability. The extraction isotherms are linear.

    Effect of Na2SO4 and PEG1500 Contents on the Partitioning of the Palladium(II) in ATPS of PEG1500–Na2SO4–(HCl/H2SO4 + NaCl)

    The extraction of palladium was studied with the gross compositions of the systems: (1) 15 wt % PEG–8 wt % Na2SO4, (2) 16.1 wt % PEG–8.6 wt % Na2SO4, (3) 17.3 wt % PEG–9.5 wt % Na2SO4, (4) 19 wt % PEG–11 wt % Na2SO4; for which the compositions of equilibrium phases, TTL and STL were determined. The choice of these compositions is motivated by the fact that in most subsystems of the PEG1500–Na2SO4–(HCl/H2SO4 + NaCl) system, the ratio of the volumes of the phases is close to 1:1. In the systems with 0.1, 1.0, and 2.0 mol·L–1 chloride ions and different acidity, an increase in the content of polymer and phase-forming salt, Na2SO4 leads to an increase in DPd2+.
    Figure 7 shows the dependence of the palladium distribution coefficient on the content of PEG and sodium sulfate in ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.1 M HCl + NaCl) systems with C(NaCl) = 0, 1.0, and 2.0 mol·L–1. At all the concentrations of chloride, the distribution coefficient of palladium increases with an increase in the content of the system components, while TTL also increases. Such an influence of the concentrations of the phase-forming salt and polymer on the distribution coefficient of the target components was observed in a number of papers (12,13,18,19) and explained by the increase of hydrophobicity of the polymer phase due to increased leaching action of salt anions.
    The distribution coefficient of palladium in the PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + 0.1 M NaCl) system with a low content of sodium sulfate and PEG is higher than in the PEG1500–Na2SO 0.1 M HCl system. This is due to the difference in the composition of the phases and the TLL value in these systems at a low content of ATPS components (Table 3). When the concentration of sodium sulfate and PEG increases further, the distribution coefficients remain practically unchanged, since, as was noted above, the compositions of the phases, TLL, and STL stop changing.

    Effect of NaCl Concentration on the Partitioning of the Palladium(II) in ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–(HCl/H2SO4 + NaCl)

    Figure 8 shows the dependence of palladium distribution coefficients on the concentration of chloride ions in hydrochloric and sulfuric acid solutions at gross content of polymer and sodium sulfate 19 and 11 wt %, respectively. These dependencies are almost identical for the PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.10 M HCl + NaCl) and PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + NaCl) systems. This occurs because the compositions of the phases of ATPS in these systems are very close (Table 3). An increase in the concentration of chloride ions in the extraction system from 0.1 to 1.0 mol·L–1 leads to a reduction of the distribution coefficient of palladium(II) from 9.6 to 5.8. With a further increase in the concentration of chloride ions, the distribution coefficient increases again (DPd2+ = 6.91 ± 0.59), but does not reach the values obtained during extraction from solutions with C(Cl–) = 0.1 mol·L–1.
    In the PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.5 M H2SO4 + NaCl) system, having the same concentrations of polymer and phase-forming salt (w(PEG1500) = 19 wt %, w(Na2SO4) = 11 wt %), an increase in the concentration of sodium chloride leads to increase in the palladium distribution coefficient. It takes the values of 5.2, 5.6, and 6.5 at concentrations of chloride ions 0.1, 1.0, and 2.0 mol·L–1, respectively (Figure 8).
    To explain the observed patterns, the composition of the extracted forms of palladium was determined. For this purpose, the electronic absorption spectra of the solutions before extraction, as well as of the polymer and salt enriched phases after extraction in all the systems studied were recorded. Figure 9 shows the spectra of the polymer-rich phase with extracted Pd(II) in the system PEG1500 (19 wt %)–Na2SO4 (11 wt %)–(0.1 M HCl + NaCl). At concentrations of chloride ions 1 and 2 mol·L–1, the bands at λ = 282 nm (C(Cl–) = 1 mol·L–1), 281 nm (C(Cl–) = 2 mol·L–1), and 474 nm (C(Cl–) = 1 and 2 mol·L–1) indicate that palladium is present as [PdCl4]2–. (35) At C(Cl–) = 0.1 mol·L–1, bands with λ = 282 nm and λ = 447 nm are observed. The [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− ion is characterized by a band with λ = 431 nm. (35) An observed shift of a wide band of the electronic absorption spectrum indicates that at a concentration of chloride ions 0.1 mol·L–1, palladium is present as a mixture of [PdCl4]2– and [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− (53% [PdCl4]2– and 47% [Pd(H2O)Cl3]−), which is consistent with the published data. (23)
    The electronic absorption spectra of solutions before extraction (without adding PEG1500 and sodium sulfate) and of the salt-rich phase showed that the presence of polymer and SO42– ions in the solutions practically do not affect the composition of palladium compounds. In all solutions, palladium exists either as the [PdCl4]2– ion (at a concentration of chloride ions 1 and 2 mol·L–1) or as a mixture of [PdCl4]2– and [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− (at C(Cl–) = 0.1 mol·L–1).
    The experimental data obtained by electronic absorption spectra were confirmed by calculation. Using the values of the stepwise formation constants (36) of [PdCl4]2– and the constants of palladium hydrolysis, (28) we calculated the distribution of various forms of palladium(II) in solution. At higher concentrations of chloride ions, acidity has no significant effect on palladium complexes. Figure 10 shows the fractions of various palladium complexes as a function of the chloride ion concentration for pH equal to 0 and 3. In the pH range from 0 to 3 and palladium concentrations of 0.5, 1, and 5 mmol·L–1, at a chloride ion content of 1 mol·L–1 more than 88% of all palladium ions exist in the form of [PdCl4]2–. At a chloride ion concentration of 0.1–0.15 mol·L–1 (depending on the initial concentration of palladium), [PdCl4]2– and [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− coexist in almost equal amounts, which is consistent with the literature data. (23) The concentration of palladium hydroxo-complexes is noticeable only at a C(Cl–) of less than 0.05 mol·L–1 at pH = 3; at pH less than 2, the content of hydroxo-complexes remains very low.
    Thus, in the PEG1500–Na2SO4–0.1 M HCl and PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + 0.10 M NaCl) systems, both the hydrophobic complex [PdCl4]2– and the more hydrophilic complex [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− are extracted into the polymer phase. According to our results (Table 3), the upper (polymer enriched) phase contains a large amount of water (>58 wt %) and sodium sulfate (∼2 wt %). High water content in the polymer phase leads to a preferred extraction of more hydrophilic [Pd(H2O)Cl3]− ions. This may explain the higher values of palladium distribution coefficients at a smaller concentration of Cl– ions. A decrease of the palladium distribution coefficient with increasing the concentration of sodium chloride to 1 mol·L–1 may be associated with a change in the extracted particles to more hydrophobic [PdCl4]2– anions. With further increase in the NaCl concentration to 2 mol·L–1, the polymer phase contains much less water (49 wt %) and sodium sulfate (0.4 wt %) and becomes more hydrophobic. This facilitates the extraction of the hydrophobic anion [PdCl4]2–.
    In the system PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.5 M H2SO4 + 0.1 M NaCl), the distribution coefficient is much lower, 5.6 ± 0.45. Unlike the 0.05 M H2SO4 system, the water content in this phase is noticeably lower (∼58.3 wt %), and the concentration of SO42– ions is higher, which complicates the extraction of palladium chloride complexes. In addition, a high concentration of sulfuric acid and sulfate ions with a very low Gibbs energy of hydration (37) leads to dehydration of the polyethylene glycol molecules in the upper phase, which complicates the extraction of hydrophilic ions [Pd(H2O)Cl3]−. With increasing concentration of sodium chloride, the content of water and sodium sulfate in the upper phase decreases, the hydrophobicity of the polymer phase increases, and the extraction of [PdCl4]2– becomes easier.

    Effect of pH on the Partitioning of Palladium(II) in ATPS of PEG1500–Na2SO4–(HCl/H2SO4 + NaCl)

    With increasing concentration of both hydrochloric acid and sulfuric acid, with the same content of polymer and phase-forming salt (19 wt % PEG1500 and 11 wt % Na2SO4), the palladium distribution coefficients decreased, as shown in Table 4. As the acid concentration increases, the salting-out ability of sulfate ion decreases due to the formation of HSO4– and the mutual solubility of the components increases. Therefore, the upper phase contains less polymer and more sodium sulfate and water, which reduces the extraction of [PdCl4]2–. At the same time, as expected, this trend is stronger in hydrochloric acid solutions, since HCl has a stronger effect on the phase formation in ATPS as it was found above.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    1.

    The influence of additions of acids (HCl and H2SO4) and of NaCl on phase equilibria in the aqueous two-phase system (ATPS) polyethylene glycol 1500 (PEG1500)–sodium sulfate–water was studied experimentally. An increase of NaCl concentration causes widening of the two-phase field and increases tie-line lengths, whereas an increase of acidity narrows the two-phase field and affects primarily the slopes of tie-lines.

    2.

    The distribution of Pd(II) between the phases of ATPS studied was investigated as a function of the initial concentration of Pd, of the time of contact of phases, of concentrations of sodium chloride and sulfate and of pH. Measured values of distribution coefficient DPd varied from 2.46 ± 0.19 to 9.6 ± 0.75.

    3.

    The distribution coefficients of Pd(II) between the phases of ATPS does not depend on initial concentration of Pd within the studied range of 0.0002 to 0.001 mol·L–1.

    4.

    Maximal values of the distribution coefficient DPd were achieved in the ATPS PEG1500–Na2SO4–0.1 M HCl and PEG1500–Na2SO4–(0.05 M H2SO4 + 0.1 M NaCl), in which the concentrations of Cl– are 0.1 mol·L–1. At that concentration Pd(II) exists as a mixture of complexes [PdCl4]2– and [Pd(H2O)Cl3]−, and both are extracted into the PEG-rich phase. The growth of the chloride content in ATPS leads to prevalence of the [PdCl4]2– ion, which has poorer extraction properties in the ATPS.
