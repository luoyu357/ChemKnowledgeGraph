    Abstract

    The separation of short-chained alkanes and alkenes is challenging because of their chemical similarity and thus being costly in energy. The implementation of a cryogenic adsorption process may overcome this problem, but systematic studies on light hydrocarbon adsorption at low temperatures are virtually lacking. Therefore, as a first step, in this paper, we present single-component adsorption isotherms of ethane, ethylene, propane, and propylene on activated carbon (AC) and zeolite 13X for temperatures of −80 to +20 °C and partial pressures of 5–1250 Pa. Based on these experimental data, the interactions of the adsorptives with the chemically different surfaces and their temperature dependence are discussed. Results show a strong increase in capacity with decreasing temperature for both AC and zeolite 13X. Cryogenic adsorption increases the overall (calculated) selectivity of alkane–alkene separation, especially for the zeolite 13X.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The separation and purification of short-chain alkanes and alkenes (C1–C4) are of great importance in the petrochemical industry and in technical environmental protection. Short-chain alkenes are the most important precursors in the petrochemical industry, but their production process is one of the most energy-intensive. (1−4) The mixture of various hydrocarbons obtained from steam cracking of naphtha is currently separated by low-temperature rectification. (1) An alternative to this can be an adsorption process. Adsorption is particularly suitable for the separation of traces of the target component from sidestreams or from exhaust gas of the process. (5) However, the capacity of commercial adsorbents for short-chain saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons is low under ambient conditions. Lowering the temperature below ambient temperature by integrating energy into the process can significantly increase the efficiency of an adsorber bed. This is especially beneficial for short-chain adsorptives, as shown in the authors’ preliminary work. (6−8)
    For the discussion of the cryogenic adsorption of alkanes and alkenes, their interactions with different adsorbent surfaces and temperature dependencies, systematic adsorption data are indispensable. Knowledge of these data is also necessary for the industrial design of such adsorbers. Furthermore, a prediction and evaluation of the separation of alkanes and alkenes at low temperatures can only be made on a solid database. However, systematic experimental data on the adsorption of alkanes and alkenes at temperatures below 20 °C are widely lacking.
    In contrast, the adsorption of short-chain alkanes and alkenes and their mixtures at ambient temperature has been discussed in some publications.
    Volumetric single-component isotherms of ethane and ethylene on zeolite 13X at temperatures of 25 and 50 °C were measured by Danner and Choi. (9) Mixtures of the components were analyzed chromatographically. Although the loads increase with decreasing temperature, the equilibrium between ethane and ethylene shifts only slightly. Triebe et al. also dealt with the adsorption of short-chain hydrocarbons (methane, ethane, and ethylene) on different zeolites. (10) Whereas the equilibrium loads of ethane on the zeolites 4A and 5A differ only slightly, the capacity of ethylene on the 5A zeolite containing a doubly charged calcium ion is greater than on the 4A zeolite with a singly charged sodium ion. The adsorption capacity of the zeolite is therefore determined by the interaction between the π-electrons of the alkenes and the lattice cations.
    Hosseinpour et al. investigated the selectivity of ethane and ethylene over methane on a CaX zeolite. (11) The analysis of the single-component isotherms measured by a volumetric apparatus at temperatures of 15, 25, and 35 °C shows that ethylene has the greatest and methane the least affinity to the CaX zeolite. The authors attribute this observation to the stronger interactions of the additional carbon atom of ethane compared to methane and the quadrupole moment of ethylene. Low pressures and high temperatures favor the separation of the compounds.
    Rege et al. carried out single-component and mixture measurements of ethane, ethylene, propane, and propylene in a volumetric apparatus at temperatures of 25 and 70 °C on a 4A zeolite. (12) The separation of ethane and ethylene is achieved by kinetic effects, with the best result being obtained for the lowest temperature. Propane and propylene are separated in zeolite 4A because of steric effects. Similar results of adsorptive separation on zeolites have been published in several other papers. (13−17)
    Jarvelin and Fair gravimetrically investigated the separation of propane and propylene on various adsorbents, including zeolite 13X and activated carbon (AC) at 25 °C. (18) Although AC has greater adsorption capacity for propane and propylene, it shows no significant selectivity. According to Jarvelin and Fair, the separation of propane and propylene is feasible with zeolite 13X owing to the different kinetics of propane and propylene.
    Volumetric measurements of various hydrocarbons at 25 °C on several ACs were carried out by Lewis et al. (19) The adsorption capacity of ACs increases for hydrocarbons with increasing chain length. In the range of low partial pressures, alkanes show a greater affinity to ACs than alkenes. Furthermore, Lewis et al. investigated binary and ternary mixtures of short-chain hydrocarbons at 25 °C on a silica gel and several ACs. (20) The adsorbed amount of a component from the mixture is lower than the adsorbed amount of the pure component in all measurements. The volatility of the saturated hydrocarbons is lower than the volatility of the unsaturated hydrocarbons. Adsorptives with a lower volatility show a higher adsorption capacity. An exception is the mixture of ethane and ethylene on the silica gel. Although ethylene is more volatile, it is preferentially adsorbed because of its molecular structure. The authors explain this with the influence of the double bond and the differing molecular weights of saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons. Both factors lose significance with increasing chain length, which is why the boiling point becomes more and more important.
    Reich et al. published isotherms of methane, ethane, and ethylene, as well as their binary and ternary mixtures at different temperatures (−60, 13, and 28 °C), pressures (up to 35 bar), and ratios on a microporous AC. (21) At 28 °C and low partial pressures, ethane shows greater adsorption capacity over ethylene. With increasing partial pressure, the adsorption isotherms of ethane and ethylene approach each other. At lower temperatures, the adsorption capacity of the adsorptives is generally higher. Adsorption from the mixture shows that the component with the higher ratio preferentially adsorbs.
    Zhu et al. compare the capacity of alkanes and alkenes on a microporous AC. (22) Adsorption isotherms were determined in volumetric measurements of ethane and ethylene at −80 to 65 °C and of propane and propylene at 0–85 °C at partial pressures of up to 10 bar. A comparison of the adsorption isotherms at the lowest measured temperatures of C2 and C3 alkanes and alkenes demonstrates that at low partial pressures, the capacity of the unsaturated hydrocarbons is smaller than the capacity of the saturated ones. As the partial pressure increases, the adsorption isotherms of the saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons converge until the capacity of the alkene exceeds the capacity of the alkane. The authors base this observation on the pore volume, which is available for adsorption. At low partial pressures, selectivity is dominated by the interaction between adsorptive and adsorbent and the alkanes are preferentially adsorbed. When approaching complete pore-filling at high partial pressures, the smaller molecules ethylene and propylene reach higher molar capacities. The adsorption of short-chain hydrocarbons on different ACs was investigated by other authors. (17,23−27)
    In summary, it can be said that there is some research on the adsorption of short-chain alkanes and alkenes at ambient temperatures and higher. Anyway, a systematic investigation of the temperature dependence of the adsorption of alkenes, in particular at low temperatures, has not yet been carried out. Also, to date, no assessment can be made of the influence of low temperatures on adsorptive separation.
    The aim of this work is therefore to systematically investigate the adsorption behavior of ethane, ethylene, propane, and propylene in a temperature range from −80 to 20 °C on different adsorbents. Isotherms of the compounds from mixtures with nitrogen at partial pressures from 5 to 1250 Pa and a total pressure of 1.1 bar were recorded on AC and zeolite 13X. Zeolite 13X was chosen as a candidate for polar adsorbents as its aperture is accessible for all adsorptives studied in this work. In case of 4A and 5A, steric hindrance is probable, especially for the larger C3 molecules, which makes kinetics very slow and thus experiments are lengthy and more error-prone. A comparison of the isotherms provides conclusions on the type and strength of the occurring interactions, which are discussed in detail. Selectivity numbers can be used to assess the influence of low temperatures on the separation of the compounds. Finally, the industrial applicability of the process is evaluated.

    A detailed structural description and a discussion of the interactions between the adsorbents’ surface and the adsorptives is given in Birkmann et al. (8) The AC has a multimodal pore size distribution. Most of the micropores have a pore size between 0.45–0.85 and 1.1–2.4 nm. Separation because of steric hindrance is essentially excluded as the critical molecular diameter of the adsorptives and carrier gases is between 0.44 and 0.50 nm. The surface of the AC mainly consists of disordered, nonpolar graphite-like areas that form slit pores. (30) An ultimate analysis revealed 86.9% carbon, 0.28% hydrogen, and 5.62% oxygen (according to DIN EN ISO/IEC 17025:2005 with EUROEA3000 Elemental Analyzer, EuroVector S.p.A.). Those low numbers of hydrogen and oxygen indicate few functional groups. Various types of interactions can lead to the adsorption of a molecule on a carbonaceous surface. Most important for nonpolar alkanes are dispersion interactions because of temporary polarization. (30,31) In contrast, alkene molecules have a permanent quadrupole, which can induce a charge shift in nonpolar surface groups, resulting in mutual attraction. Furthermore, π–π-interactions between graphite π-systems and the π-system of a double bond are possible. (31)
    Type X zeolites consist of a framework of aluminum and silicon atoms that are connected by oxygen atoms, forming a regular cage-like pore structure. The inner diameter of the cages is 1.37 nm with an aperture of 0.74–0.79 nm. (5,32−35) The negative charge introduced by the aluminum atoms is compensated by sodium cations, resulting in a high number of polar areas. When sodium cations are located on the surface of the pores, they can interact with adsorptive molecules. Therefore, in adsorption of nonpolar alkanes on type X zeolites, we can expect not only dispersion interactions with the less polar aluminum silicate structure but also induced interactions with sodium cations. (32−34) The sodium cation dislocates the charge within the alkane chain and induces a temporary dipole. Strong electrostatic interactions are expected between the sodium cations in the zeolite lattice structure and the quadrupole moment of alkenes. Generally, dispersion interactions are much weaker than induced and cation−π interactions. (36) Adsorbate molecules can also interact with neighboring molecules. Those lateral interactions become more prevalent with increasing loading, leading to S-shaped adsorption isotherms. (37)
    Interfering effects resulting from coadsorption of the carrier gas nitrogen can be excluded for the adsorptives studied in this work. It was shown in a previous work of the authors that the carrier gas has no effect on the adsorption of hydrocarbon molecules with a chain length of C2 and larger. (6) Thus, coadsorption of the carrier gas is not relevant here.

    2.2. Apparatus and Experimental Procedure

    Scheme 1 displays a schematic flow diagram of the experimental plant, which has already been described in detail in previous publications. (6−8) In a gas mixing chamber (1) a defined gas mixture of carrier gas and adsorptive is produced with mass flow controllers 2–4, which are located in a tempered box. The process temperature is adjusted with a cryogenic circulation thermostat (2). The steel adsorber measures 200 mm in height and 40 mm in diameter (3). A cooling medium is led through the adsorber’s double-jacket to minimize axial temperature gradients. The concentration of the adsorptive is analyzed with an FID (4).
    The AC is conditioned for 2 h at 175 °C and the zeolite 13X for 24 h at 300 °C. The hot material is filled immediately into the column and purged with dry carrier gas until the process temperature is reached. Before the start of an experiment, the concentration of the gas mixture is adjusted by passing the gas through a bypass to the FID. The experiment starts when the gas mixture is led through the adsorber. The concentration of the adsorptive is continuously analyzed at the outlet of the adsorber. We assume thermodynamic equilibrium when the outlet concentration reaches the concentration at the inlet of the adsorber. In a cumulative procedure, subsequent equilibrium points can be measured by increasing the concentration of the adsorptive step by step. (38)

Performing a mass balance around the adsorber, the adsorbed excess moles nads [mol] can be calculated from the BTC.     2.3. Experimental Uncertainties

    The experimental setup and resulting quantities are subject to systematic and random errors. Those errors lead to uncertainties in the concentration signal and in the equilibrium loading. The systematic uncertainty of the concentration signal is the sum of the uncertainties of the test gas (2%) and the FID (1%/24 h). The random uncertainty of the concentration signal was determined from the standard deviation of the concentration signal in equilibrium (∼0.2%). The systematic error of the equilibrium loading consists of the sum of the systematic deviations of the MFCs used (1%), the balance (0.025–0.15%), and the FID. As the random error of the equilibrium loading is not directly accessible, it was estimated by Gaussian error propagation. Overall, the uncertainties for the experiments shown here are in the range of 3–6%. Additional replicate measurements resulted in deviations of 2% for both, the concentration and the equilibrium loading.

    3.1. Adsorption on ACs

    Figure 1a displays isotherms of ethylene on AC at temperatures of −80 to 20 °C. The isotherms are described very well over a wide temperature range by the global temperature-dependent Toth isotherm model. The isotherms are right-curved and the loading is strongly temperature-dependent. As the temperature decreases, the isotherm curvature increases significantly. At low temperatures, the kinetic energy of the adsorptive is reduced. As a result, adsorption sites can be occupied at lower partial pressures than at high temperatures, which leads to a large isotherm slope in the initial partial pressure range. In addition, less energetically valuable adsorption sites can be occupied, thus increasing the capacity. At high partial pressures, the isotherms flatten out because fewer adsorption sites are available as the degree of coverage increases. However, no saturation plateau is reached in the partial pressure range covered here. A temperature reduction from 20 to −80 °C increases the loading from 0.29 to 4.70 mol kg–1 at 1200 Pa.
    Materials of natural origin like ACs may differ considerably in their adsorption properties, so any quantitative comparison between ACs is of limited significance. Anyway, we provide data of other working groups from literature for orientation purposes. Reich et al. find somewhat lower loads of 1.884 mol kg–1 ethylene on AC at −60 °C and 690 Pa (here 2.19 mol kg–1). (21) At −80 °C, already published loads are in very good agreement with the values shown here (Zhu et al.: 4.84 mol kg–1, here 4.70 mol kg–1 at 1250 Pa). (22) Monte Carlo simulations show an approximately 1.5-fold loading of ethylene on a graphitic surface. (39)
    Figure 1b presents adsorption isotherms of propylene on AC for temperatures from −80 to 20 °C. All isotherms are concave and the equilibrium loads are strongly temperature-dependent. The isotherm slope is very steep at the beginning and then flattens quickly. A temperature reduction from 20 to −80 °C increases the loading from 1.7 to 9 mol kg–1 at 1200 Pa. Zhu finds loads of 2.63 mol kg–1 at 0 °C and 1250 Pa (this work: 2.65 mol kg–1). (22) Comparable loads for propylene on AC have also been published for higher temperatures (1.49 mol kg–1 in Cheripally et al., this work: 1.78 mol kg–1, at 20 °C and 1250 Pa). (24)
    The error bars of the measurements at low temperatures are larger than at high temperatures, which can be attributed to the significantly longer measurement time until equilibrium is reached. For reasons of simplicity, no error bars are shown in the following. All experimental errors can be found in the Supporting Information in a tabular form.

    3.2. Adsorption on Zeolites

    Figure 2a displays adsorption isotherms of ethane on zeolite 13X at temperatures of −80 to 20 °C. Parts of the data have already been published in Schmittmann et al. and in Birkmann et al. (6,8) The isotherms assume different patterns as temperature changes: at high temperatures, they are linear and at low temperatures they are concave curved. At medium temperatures down to −40 °C, they assume an S-shape. They are linear at low partial pressures, convex-curved at higher pressures, and finally concave-curved when approaching saturation. This characteristic S-shape provides information on the mechanisms and interactions involved in the occupation of adsorption sites. The first molecules adsorb independently without any interactions with neighboring molecules, as the degree of coverage is very low. This leads to the linear initial slope of the isotherms. At higher partial pressures, some molecules are adsorbed on sites adjacent to already occupied sites, so that additional lateral interactions are formed between adsorbed molecules. This increase in interactions leads to the convex isotherm part. (37) As soon as most places of monomolecular coverage are occupied in the high partial pressure range, saturation begins and the slope of the isotherm flattens out. The transition between the gain of interaction by lateral interactions and the beginning of saturation is marked by the inflection point of the isotherm. For strongly adsorbing molecules and low temperatures (−80 °C in Figure 2a), the inflection point is below the measuring range at very low partial pressures. The inflection point then shifts toward higher partial pressures with increasing temperature. At temperatures greater than −40 °C, the inflection point is at high partial pressures which are now above the measuring range. This was discussed in detail by other authors. (17,36,37,40) The Toth isotherm is mathematically unsuitable for modeling an inflection point. Nevertheless, it describes the data in sufficient quality.
    Figure 2b illustrates adsorption isotherms of ethylene on zeolite 13X at temperatures from −80 to 20 °C. The isotherms are almost linear at high temperatures and strongly curved with rapid saturation at low temperatures. Lowering the temperature from 20 to −80 °C increases the loading from 0.5 to 2.5 mol kg–1 at 1250 Pa.
    Adsorption isotherms of propane on zeolite 13X are shown in Figure 2c. For better visualization, an extension of this figure showing the small partial pressure range to highlight the energetically most valuable adsorption sites can be found in the Supporting Information. S-shaped isotherms can also be observed here, especially at 0 and 20 °C. The S-shape at high temperatures leads to a less accurate approximation of the experimental data with the Toth-isotherm, as it cannot describe an inflexion point mathematically. At lower temperatures, the inflection point is at very low partial pressures and is no longer resolved by the measured data. The surface of zeolite 13X is already saturated at −40 °C and 750 Pa when adsorbing propane. A further reduction of the temperature hardly changes the isothermal form.
    Figure 2d depicts the adsorption isotherms of propylene on zeolite 13X. A particularly high initial slope at low partial pressures indicates strong interactions between the zeolite surface and the adsorptive. A saturation effect occurs at 0 °C already.
    Only adsorption data for high temperatures can be found in the literature; here, very good agreements can be found (ethylene: 0.43 mol kg–1 at 25 °C in Danner et al., this work: 0.40 mol kg–1 at 20 °C, 885 Pa.     3.3. Comparison of Alkane and Alkene Adsorption

    In the following, the adsorption properties of alkanes and alkenes at low temperatures will be compared and discussed for the two adsorbents. In particular, the influence of the double bond and the molecular chain length and size on the cryogenic adsorption has to be determined. To compare the equilibrium data, the isotherms of alkanes and alkenes of the same number of carbon atoms are displayed in one plot. The isotherm data of the adsorption of ethane and propane on the AC NORIT RX 1.5 Extra were experimentally determined and published by Birkmann et al. using the same setup. (7)
    Figure 3a illustrates adsorption isotherms of ethane and ethylene on the AC for temperatures from −80 to −40 °C. Ethane reaches significantly higher loads than ethylene over all temperatures. Both molecules interact with the mainly nonpolar surface of the AC by dispersion interactions and by lateral interactions with neighboring molecules. The strength of these interactions is mainly determined by the number of binding sites, the polarizability, and the sterical arrangement of the molecules. (37) Ethane and ethylene have the same number of binding sites. However, the molecules differ in their polarizability (see Table 1). Ethane is slightly larger and therefore better polarizable than ethylene, so that a temporary dipole moment can be more easily induced in ethane. Because of the higher evaporation enthalpy, ethane molecules interact more strongly with each other than ethylene molecules, which leads to stronger lateral interactions in the adsorption of ethane. Interactions between the π-electrons of the double bond and polar regions of the AC surface, such as strong induced interactions or π–π-interactions, do not seem to make a significant contribution to the sum of the interactions. Even the different sizes or molecular volumes do not (yet) have an effect here. If the partial pressure is further increased beyond the measuring range or if the temperature is lowered significantly below −80 °C, the ethylene loading could exceed the loading of ethane. A similar behavior was described by Zhu et al. and Lewis et al. (20,22)
    Figure 3b compares the isotherms of propane and propylene on the AC for temperatures from −80 to −40 °C. Both adsorptives adsorb equally strongly. Here, the chemical and structural differences between the molecules and their interaction characteristics with the surface are very small. Both, the polarizability and the evaporation enthalpy, as well as the molecule size are much more similar than in the case of ethane and ethylene. This means that interactions with the surface because of the double bond of propylene have no significant influence on the strength of adsorption on nonpolar ACs, as was already concluded by Zhu et al. (22)
    Figure 4a,c compares adsorption isotherms of ethane and ethylene on zeolite 13X. Ethylene adsorbs in significantly higher quantities than ethane. The ethylene molecule has a quadrupole moment that forms strong cation−π interactions with the sodium cations in the zeolite cage. Ethane cannot form these interactions because of the neutral charge distribution and therefore adsorbs less than ethylene on the zeolite. (10,11)
    A comparison of the isotherms of propane and propylene is given for temperatures from −80 to −40 °C in Figure 4b, and for temperatures of 0 and 20 °C in Figure 4d. At low temperatures and in saturation, propane and propylene adsorb to a similar extent. The molecules have a similar molecular volume and thus require the same amount of space on the surface. At high temperatures and low partial pressures, there are differences in loading. The molecules adsorb less at high temperatures. Thus, the small difference in the strength of the interactions between propane and propylene is more pronounced. Propylene interacts more strongly and saturation begins at lower partial pressures than with the adsorption of propane, resulting in greater loading differences.

    3.4. Temperature Dependence of Alkane and Alkene Adsorption

    Figure 5 displays the dependence of the loading on the process temperature of AC for the C2 and C3 hydrocarbons at 500 Pa. The slope of these curves is determined by two opposite effects. In the range of the highest temperatures, it is flat, because a slight temperature reduction has little influence on the equilibrium. If the temperature is lowered further, the curve steepens because the affinity of the adsorptive molecules is exponentially dependent on temperature (see eq 4). If the system approaches saturation at low temperatures, the curve flattens again. Thus, for each adsorptive, a temperature range with the greatest change in loading can be determined.
    Ethane and ethylene exhibit differences at very low temperatures. Here, the difference in the molecular properties appears to be more important for the capacity. The slope of the curve is much steeper for ethane than for ethylene. The measuring range displayed for ethane is in the upper range of the S-curve pattern, whereas ethylene has not yet reached the range with the greatest loading increases at the temperatures measured here. To observe a strong increase for ethylene, the temperature would have to be lowered significantly below −80 °C. The weaker a molecule adsorbs, the more profitable it is to reduce the temperature significantly with respect to the equilibrium load.
    The loads of propane and propylene are equally dependent on the temperature because of the almost identical physicochemical molecular properties.
    Figure 6 depicts the dependence of the loading on the process temperature for the C2 and C3 hydrocarbons on zeolite 13X at 500 Pa. Here, the temperature dependence of the equilibrium loading becomes particularly clear as different segments of the S-curve are displayed. The absolute load difference is highest for ethane and ethylene at moderate temperatures, because the curve of ethylene has already reached the turning point at about −40 °C and begins to approach saturation when the temperature drops further. Ethylene has a smaller molecular volume than C3 hydrocarbons. Thus, more molecules ethylene fit in a unit cell of the zeolite and the loading exceeds that of propane and propylene below −40 °C. This trend can also be seen with ethane, but the point would be outside the measuring range at a lower temperature.
    Figure 7a,b describes the selectivity as a function of temperature for adsorption on AC and zeolite 13X respectively. The selectivity is calculated from the ratio of the loading of the stronger-adsorbing to the less-adsorbing compound. Thus, the higher the ratio S, the greater the relative load difference between two compounds. Based on this number, a prediction of the thermodynamic separation of alkanes and alkenes is made. A prerequisite for the evaluation of the material separation on the basis of single-component measurements is the assumption that adsorptives behave in the same way in the mixtures as in the pure substance. Thus, the same interactions are formed between alkanes and alkenes as between molecules of the own chemical type. For a good separation, it is desirable that one component adsorbs particularly well, whereas the second component does not adsorb or adsorbs very little.
    The C2 hydrocarbons have a slightly higher selectivity on the AC than the C3 hydrocarbons (Figure 7a). These have a selectivity close to 1, which makes separation impossible. In both cases, the process temperature and the concentration have no significant influence on the quality of the separation.
    The separation of C2 hydrocarbons is much more promising on zeolite 13X (Figure 7b). The selectivity has its maximum at −20 °C and 250 Pa. Here, ethylene adsorbs nearly 10 times more than ethane. The formation of a distinct maximum can be attributed to a complex interplay of different effects. At 20 °C and 250 Pa, the capacity for ethylene is already eight times higher than the capacity of ethane. When the temperature drops down to −20 °C, the capacity of ethylene profits a lot (see Figure 6), whereas the capacity of ethane increases only slightly. This is the reason why the selectivity increases to its maximum from 20 to −20 °C. Toward very low temperatures, the more strongly adsorbing molecule ethylene begins to approach saturation. Ethane on the contrary now starts to profit much from the temperature decrease at temperatures lower than −40 °C (still being far away from saturation also at −80 °C). Thus, the capacities approach each other at temperatures below −20 °C, which is why now the selectivity decreases. The S-shape of the temperature dependence of the capacity and its slope are different for each component and the result is a maximum in the selectivity’s temperature dependence.
    The lower the partial pressure, the higher the selectivity. This behavior is constant over the entire temperature range. With low surface coverage, the interaction with the surface is based on dispersion and induction interactions. Here, ethane and ethylene differ in the strength of their interactions, which is reflected in a higher selectivity. If the partial pressure is increased and the degree of coverage of the surface increases, additional lateral interactions between the adsorptives are formed, which are similar for both molecules. The interactions with the surface contribute less to the sum of forces and the selectivity is low.
    The ratio of propylene to propane loads is very low at low temperatures and increases with temperature (Figure 7b). Here, the optimum is 20 °C. At low temperatures, both adsorptives are in saturation at almost the same load, so that no separation can take place here based on molecular differences. A lowering of the temperature is therefore not necessary. The influence of the concentration can be seen at high temperatures. Here, too, a low degree of coverage and few lateral interactions result in a high share of interactions with the surface, which are different because of the molecular chemistry. This results in higher selectivities at low concentrations.
    In order to realize an efficient adsorptive separation process for alkanes and alkenes, the capacity of the adsorbent has to be considered in addition to the optimal separation because of a high selectivity. This criterion is taken into account in the performance number. The selectivity is multiplied with the load difference of the components to be separated. A high performance stands for good separation and takes into account both the selectivity and the capacity of the system.
    Figure 7c,d depict the performance as a function of temperature for AC and zeolite 13X. Despite the high loads, AC is not suitable for alkane–alkene separation. Only at low temperatures can ethane–ethylene separation be achieved. Here, unlike all other systems, high concentrations have a higher performance than low concentrations. This can be attributed to the fact that the particularly high loads at low temperatures overcompensate for the higher selectivity at low concentrations. The optimum operating point of an ethane–ethylene separation on zeolite 13X is clearly −40 °C for 250 Pa. With increasing partial pressure, the maximum shifts to higher temperatures. At 1000 Pa, the optimum operating temperature is −20 °C. This shift is the result of the opposing influences of decreasing capacity versus increasing selectivity with increasing temperature on the performance number.
    A process temperature of 20 °C (or more) is suitable for the separation of propane and propylene. Here, too, maxima are indicated that are shifted to higher temperatures with increasing partial pressure.

    4. Summary and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This study investigated the adsorption behavior of short-chain alkane and alkene traces on an AC and zeolite 13X at low temperatures down to −80 °C. The interactions of the adsorptives with chemically different surfaces and their temperature dependence were discussed.
    ACs have a high capacity for the hydrocarbons investigated, which is significantly increased by temperature reduction. Alkanes and alkenes form similarly strong dispersion interactions with the nonpolar surface of AC. The double bond of the alkenes does not seem to have any influence on the capacity. As a result, selectivity is low and largely independent of temperature. Despite very high capacities, the performance of material separation is also poor. The temperature only influences the performance of the C2 separation, which is best at −80 °C.
    Zeolite 13X has significantly lower capacities for alkanes and alkenes. The influence of temperature on the equilibrium is strong. The interactions between the quadrupole moment of the alkenes and the cations of the zeolite are decisive for the selectivity, which is greatest for C2 hydrocarbons at −20 °C and for C3 hydrocarbons at 20 °C. Selectivity is higher at low concentrations. If, in addition to selectivity, capacities are also taken into account, it can be seen that the process performance for separating ethane and ethylene is highest at −40 °C. Ambient temperatures are sufficient to separate propane and propylene.
    In future work, we will extend the experiments to higher concentrations of hydrocarbons and to multicomponent adsorption.
