    Abstract

    Butyrylcholinesterase (BChE) is an essential human cholinesterase relevant to liver conditions and neurodegenerative diseases, which makes it a pivotal biomarker of health. It therefore remains challenging and highly desired to elaborate efficient chemical tools for BChE with simple operations and satisfactory working performance. In this work, a background-free detection strategy was built by virtue of the judicious coupling of a specific BChE-enzymatic reaction and in situ cyclization. High sensitivity with a low limit of detection (LOD) of 0.075 μg/mL could be readily achieved from the blank background and the as-produced emissive indicators, and the specific reaction site contributed to the high selectivity over other bio-species even acetylcholinesterase (AChE). In addition to the multifaceted spectral experiments to verify the sensing mechanism, this work assumed comprehensive studies on the application. The bio-investigation ranged from cells to an organism, declaring a noteworthy prospect in disease diagnosis, especially for Alzheimer’s disease (AD), a common neurodegenerative disease with over-expressed BChE. Moreover, its excellent work for inhibition efficacy elucidation was also proved with the accuracy IC50 of tacrine for BChE (8.6 nM), giving rise to an expanded application for trace pesticide determination.

    Butyrylcholinesterase (BChE) is a vital member of the cholinesterase class for cholinergic neurotransmission, which exists in various tissues, including the liver, pancreas, and brain. (1,2) It is produced by the liver typically and spreads into the blood circulation. Hence, it is reasonable that the BChE activity could reflect the condition of the liver and its capability to produce protein to a large extent. (3) Recent years have witnessed the growing emphasis on the clinic diagnosis for liver diseases by measuring the BChE activity conceivably in that a certain damage to the liver could lead to a reduction in BChE generation. (4) Notably, BChE is also reported to be relevant with Alzheimer’s disease (AD), a common type of neurodegenerative disease with dementia. (5−8) During the development of AD, an appreciable rise in BChE activity from 1% to 65% could be recorded. Compared with people without dementia, the BChE level in cerebral cortex of AD patients is significantly higher, demonstrating the great significance for monitoring AD by tracking BChE activity. (9,10) In addition, a set of pesticides possess a significant inhibition nature against BChE, indicating that the decrease in BChE activity could also be treated as an important parameter for clinical diagnosis of acute organophosphorus pesticide poisoning (AOPP) and assessment for pesticide residue detection. (11−15) It is reported that BChE also can be used for cocaine or organophosphorus detoxification. (16−18)
    Many diagnostic tools have been developed for BChE determination, such as enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), (19,20) Raman spectroscopy, (15,21) pH potentiometry, (22) and Ellman’s colorimetry. (23) It is evident that these tools may have been well developed, but suffer high costs in equipment, complex operations, low sensitivity, or poor stability, which impedes their further application. Therefore, there is an urgent need to establish a reliable sensing assay for BChE with excellent sensitivity and selectivity.
    For the development of research in detective receptors, multifunctional schemes are expected to tackle the above issues aptly. Although enormous progress for bio-imaging and analysis has been made nowadays, (24−26) strategies for BChE sensing are still under developed. Nanomaterials, for instance, quantum dots, (27,28) carbon dots, (29−31) nanorods, (32) and nanoclusters, (33) serve as efficient candidates for indicating BChE activity yet are challenged by the complex preparation, biocompatibility, and photostability, blocking further application in bio-environments at cellular or organism levels. (34) Capitalizing on the specific enzymatic reaction of BChE, organic receptors were also designed and synthesized with spectral profiles, mostly fluorescence, to report their expression or activity. (35−37) For example, Yang’s group reported an organic receptor for BChE activity based on the enzymatic hydrolysis of cyclopropylformate just recently. Unfortunately, most strategies could barely avoid the inherent background, which leads to unsatisfying sensitivity. (38) Precise control on the receptor by employing a non-emissive framework is expected to settle this matter, and the in situ reaction pattern is able to produce fluorescent chromophores as indicators. (39,40) By using the chromophore generated during the sensing assay as an output signal, the interference of background fluorescence could be eliminated effectively. (41,42)
    Keeping the above considerations in mind, a sensing platform for BChE was established on the basis of a background-free detection event (Scheme 1). The specific reaction site, cyclopropyl, was introduced onto the non-emissive skeleton to produce BChE-target receptor P1, ruling out the intrinsic fluorescence in the detective system. Upon the introduction of BChE, cyclopropyl could be removed followed by a spontaneous substitution. (43) The reaction in situ generated the fluorescent product PF as an indicator to reflect BChE activity. The fundamental understanding of this envisaged sensing procedure was studied by a variety of experiments, consisting of a spectral comparison between the reaction solution and the synthesized PF, 1H NMR tracking, high-resolution mass spectra (HR-MS) investigation, and Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) spectra. In addition to an outstanding LOD of 0.075 μg/mL, which could be contributed by the in situ reaction manner, this scheme was also good for selectivity with excellent discrimination over AChE. Therefore, probe P1 was expected to be helpful for the early diagnosis of AD. It was noteworthy that this assay was satisfactory in a series application, ranging from the imaging at cellular and organism levels to the inhibition investigation for IC50 determination and detection of trace carbamate pesticide.

    Materials and Instrumentation

    Materials

    Reagents for organic synthesis, including 4-(diethylamino)salicylaldehyde, triethylamine, cyclopropanecarboxylic acid chloride, 2-aminobenzenethiol, malononitrile, piperidine, and the BChE inhibitor (tacrine and iso-OMPA), were purchased from Energy Chemical (Shanghai, China). Solvents (dichloromethane, methanol, ethanol, and ethyl acetate) and inorganic salts (sodium sulfate, sodium bicarbonate, and sodium chloride) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagents Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All the reagents and solvents used for organic synthesis were used as received without further purification. Enzymes (alkaline phosphatase, lysozyme, inorganic pyrophosphatase, telomerase, thrombin, and AChE) and biological reagents (protein kinase A; ATP, GSH) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Shanghai, China).

    Instrumentation

    1H and 13C NMR spectra were taken on a 500 MHz NMR spectrometer (Bruker, Karlsruhe, Germany) using DMSO-d6 as solvent, and chemical shifts were reported in parts per million downfield relative to tetramethylsilane (TMS, δ scale with solvent resonances as internal standards). ESI mass spectra (ESI-MS) were obtained on an Esquire 3000 Plus mass spectrometer. Optical spectroscopy of absorption spectra and fluorescence spectra was carried out on a UH5300 UV–vis and Hitachi F-4600 spectrophotometer, respectively, in a 1 cm quartz cell. Fluorescent intracellular imaging was done on a Leica SP5 laser scanning confocal microscope. Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay was carried out to evaluate the cytotoxic effect of the probe on a Bio-Tek ELX800 microplate reader.

Cyclopropanecarboxylic acid chloride (0.63 g, 6.0 mmol) in 10 mL of dichloromethane was added. The mixture was stirred overnight at room temperature. After quenching the reaction, the solution was washed 3 times with brine and the organic phase was dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate.     General Procedures for Spectra Measurements

    The stock solutions of P1 and PF (5.0 mM) were prepared in DMSO and stored in a refrigerator at 4 °C. Spectral experiments were carried out using the stock solution of P1, which was diluted by a PBS buffer (10 mM, pH 7.4) to which BChE or other guest species were added.

    The detection limit (3.3σ/k) was measured based on the linear relationship between fluorescence intensity (at 528 nm) and the concentration of BChE where σ is the standard deviation of the blank measurements, k is the slope of the linear dependence of I528nm versus BChE concentration.

    Kinetic Studies and Inhibitor Characterization of Probe P1

    Probe P1 with different concentrations (2, 4, 8, 10, and 20 μM) was hydrolyzed by BChE (7 μg/mL). The reaction was monitored by measuring the fluorescence intensity at 528 nm for P1 after being incubated in PBS buffer (pH 7.4) for 30 min at 37 °C.     Cell Cytotoxicity Study

    To study the cytotoxicity, HEK 293 cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5 × 104 cells/well and incubated for 24 h before treatment followed by exposure to different concentrations (2–50 μM) of probe P1 for 4 h. After washing with DPBS, the cells were further incubated for 1–6 h. The cytotoxic effects of P1 were determined by a CCK-8 assay.

    Cell Culture and Fluorescent Imaging

    HEK 293 cells were seeded in high-glucose DMEM with 10% fetal bovine serum, 1% streptomycin, and 1% penicillin. For detection of intracellular BChE activity, the cells were cultured in PBS (pH 7.4) and then treated with P1 (5 μM) for 30 min before imaging.     Spectral Sensing for BChE

    The intrinsic spectroscopy characteristics of P1 and the feasibility of the proposed sensing proposal for BChE activity were first determined by absorption and fluorescence spectra, respectively, as displayed in Figure 1a and Figure S7. Probe P1 shows a maximum absorption at 458 nm (ε = 7.5 × 102 L/(mol·cm)). A 22 nm red shift of the maximum absorption after sensing analysis indicated that the basic skeleton of P1 was changed in the BChE catalytic reaction (Figure 1a). At the same time, a background-free detection with an eye-catching emission response for BChE activity could be obtained, according to the well-defined fluorescence spectra exhibited in Figure S7b. Negligible emission was observed for the individual receptor P1, indicative of a clear background and relatively high sensitivity for the following assay. The fluorescence intensity at 528 nm was enhanced more than 90 times. An intense fluorescence response with a fluorescence quantum yield of 0.53 was observed upon the enzyme-propelled hydrolysis of P1 by BChE, which was affirmed to be promising to determine the BChE activity with high sensitivity.
    For an overall understanding of this envisaged sensing scheme, experimental conditions were first taken into account with regard to the pH, temperature, and reaction time. Receptor P1 was clarified to remain silent in aqueous solutions of varying pH values, referring to the negligible fluorescence in Figure S8 during the investigation. An obvious enhancement in fluorescence could be obtained when receptor P1 was treated with BChE, indicating that the hydrolysis of P1 by enzyme BChE was workable in a wide pH range. As depicted by the increase of fluorescence intensity at 528 nm upon the pH change from 4.5 to 9.5, the reaction conversion showed a constant rise, tending to a saturation point as the pH reached alkalinity (≥7.4). The experiment suggested that probe P1 could be used for BChE determination in a neutral or basic environment. Considering further application in biological areas, 10 mM PBS buffer with a pH value of 7.4 was final chosen for the following experiments.
    It is common sense that temperature has a great impact on enzyme activity, which affects the relevant catalysis process as a result. A temperature-dependent assessment was therefore carried out, and the reaction efficacy was evaluated by the fluorescence intensity of individual receptors P1 and the reaction system, as revealed in Figure S9. Probe P1 exhibited little emission within the whole temperature range, whereas the responsive emission upon the presence of BChE was proved to be highly related to temperature. The weak response at 0–10 °C implied the low reaction conversion arising from the limited activity of enzyme at low temperature, whereas the decline in responsive emission from 37 to 70 °C could be explained by the damaged reactivity of enzyme BChE at high temperature. The plateau point located at 37 °C affirmed that the optimum working temperature accorded with the normal body temperature, which was retained for spectral exploration in vitro.
    An optimal reaction time was determined by the following time-course assay, as exhibited in Figure S10. Upon the addition of 7 μg/mL BChE, fluorescence spectra were recorded every 5 min for 140 min, and the hydrolysis efficiency of P1 by BChE was expressed by the fluorescence intensity at 528 nm. A gradual increase in responsive emission during the experiment was observed, which leveled off within 90 min and remained constant without an observable loss afterward. The stable performance indicated that the hydrolysis result could be sustained for at least 50 min, long enough for recording and analysis. On the contrary, probe P1 was quite stable during the experimental time.
    After the aforementioned optimization process, probe P1 was applied for quantitative determination for BChE activity under the optimal conditions by tracing the fluorescence response upon the concentration-dependent enzyme-driven hydrolysis. Referring to Figure 1b, an evident fluorescence response of P1 to BChE even at a concentration of 0.5 μg/mL could be readily captured, suggestive of a high sensing sensitivity. The evaluation for an enzymatic reaction was further expressed as the formation of intensity at 528 nm versus BChE concentrations in the Figure 1b inset with an abrupt enhancement in fluorescent emissions upon the increase of the BChE concentration ranging from 0 to 25 μg/mL, which tended to a saturated state when the BChE level was raised up to 40 μg/mL. It was noteworthy that a satisfactory linearity appeared between the intensity plot and BChE concentration within 0–7.0 μg/mL, giving rise to a high correlation coefficient and an LOD of 0.075 μg/mL, which was relatively superior to previous reports (Table S1). (48,49) This outstanding performance on detection sensitivity could be explained by the negligible background provided by probe P1 and the in situ reaction pattern for the response upon analysis. This high sensitivity for BChE activity might be helpful for the early diagnosis of AD due to it being a biomarker for AD. Furthermore, the kinetics of the enzymatic reaction was also studied by the Michaelis–Menten equation described as V = Vmax[P1]/(Km + [P1]), according to the Lineweaver–Burk plot built by varying P1 concentrations (Figure S11), which gave rise to a maximum initial reaction rate of 0.042 μM/min and a Michaelis constant of 6.49 μM.
    As a pivotal parameter, the selectivity of this scenario was studied afterward, and potential bio-interfering species including cations (Na+, K+, Ca2+, Fe3+, Cu2+, Mg2+, Zn2+, Ni2+, and Co2+), anions (Cl–, I–, SO42–, HCO3–, HSO4–, S2–, Ac–, NO3–, and NO2–), amino acids (Ala, Arg, Asn, Asp, Cys, Gln, Glu, Gly, His, Ile, Leu, Lys, Met, Phe, Pro, Ser, Thr, Try, and Val), and other biomolecules (such as ATP, GSH, AChE, ALP, lysozyme, inorganic pyrophosphatase, telomerase, thrombin, PKA, carboxylesterase, chymotrypsin, and trypsin) were involved (Figures S12 and S13 and Figure 1d). Learning from the results depicted above, only BChE could trigger the fluorescence response, and the elaborate probe P1 possessed little response toward the other species. Moreover, the co-existence of the employed biospecies caused negligible influence to the sensing event for BChE activity, referring to the competitive measurement. These profiles demonstrated that probe P1 preferred to detect BChE activity with preferable selectivity and was rather stable for the proposed assay, ruling out any potential interference from other bio-species.
    A clear illustration could help with the construction and application of this envisaged in situ sensing platform for more demands. Hence, an overall study was carried out to figure out the detection mechanism by spectral investigation and aided validation methods. Compound PF, which was supposed to be the sensing product, was synthesized as described in Scheme 2 to strengthen the proposed proposal displayed in Scheme 1. The comparison between the detection solution and purified PF was conducted by absorption and fluorescence, and a similar signal source presented in the spectral window in Figure S14 suggested that PF should be responsible for the fluorescence response of the in situ sensing pattern. FT-IR bands located within 1713 and 2204 cm–1 of probe P1 could be ascribed to the ester carbonyl and cyano moiety specifically, as shown in Figure S15, which vanished upon detection with BChE, and the resultant FT-IR spectra shared similar peaks to those of PF. Furthermore, the HR-MS spectral data offered proof to support the envisaged sensing mechanism in that the molecular ion peak of PF ([M + H]+: 350.1329, calcd: 350.1322) could be monitored in the mixture solution of P1 and BChE (Figure S16). These findings confirmed that PF, which was produced during the in situ detection procedure, served as the indicator fluorescently for BChE activity determination.

    Inhibition of BChE Activity

    Tacrine, a typical inhibitor for BChE, (29) was also employed to evaluate the working performance of probe P1, as exhibited in Figure 2. After the treatment of BChE with tacrine of increasing concentration, probe P1 was applied for tracking the inhibition efficacy by responsive fluorescence. The obvious inhibition efficiency was supported by an intense decrease in fluorescence upon the introduction of tacrine of even 0.5 nM, which indicated a rather high sensitivity of probe P1 capable of reporting a tiny decline in enzyme activity. The expression in Figure 2b claimed that the IC50 of tacrine for BChE was 8.6 nM, which matched with the previous experimental profile. (7,8)
    BChE was claimed to have potential in reporting disease development, as mentioned above, so the application investigation was first carried out in the biological analysis region. Serum tests were comprehensive examinations providing essential characterization for disease, and serum samples from healthy people and patients suffering from osteoarthritis or cancer were therefore collected for BChE activity determination, as exhibited in Figure S17. The strongest responsive fluorescence suggested the highest BChE level, which was within the standard parameter for a healthy organism, whereas there was a drop in the BChE activity in samples of patients because of the illness. The great loss of BChE in cancer samples further indicated that the expression of BChE was highly inhibited when the organism suffered from a serious illness, and BChE activity could be treated as a vital parameter to evaluate physical conditions by the elaborate probe P1.

    Living Cell Imaging

    Before exploration in cellular levels, the cytotoxicity of probe P1 was determined by HEK 293 cell lines, which was used for BChE activity determination. (50,51) Referring to Figure S18, a CCK-8 assay was utilized, and the BChE concentration ranged from 2 to 50 μM with the incubation period lasting for even 6 h. Cell livability under this condition remained above 80%, confirming the satisfactory biocompatibility qualifying for the ensuing imaging operation.
    Accordingly, the time-dependent imaging of BChE activity in HEK 293 cells was first evaluated (Figure S19); a green emission emerged within 5 min then reached maximum in 30 min and remained stable for at least 30 min. The cellular investigation acquired by a confocal microscope was assumed in three cell lines, including HEK 293, HeLa, and Hep G2 cells (Figure 3). After treatment with 5 μM probe P1 for 30 min, these cells were washed with DPBS then the images were taken (λex: 458 nm). Bright green fluorescence was readily captured in HEK 293 cells, which could be interpreted by a normal expression of the BChE enzyme and adequate detection inside cells. The weak responsive emission in green windows of HeLa and Hep G2 cells further affirmed the conclusion from serum testing that the illness could cause a decrease in BChE activity, especially for cancer. (52) Extensive studies with respect to the attribution of emissive responses in the fluorescence window were also conducted by the pivotal control experiment with the pre-treatment with tacrine to inhibit BChE activity. Learning from the weakened green emission, which was as weak as that in tumor cells (Figure 3b), the sensing event for BChE by probe P1 was proved to account for the fluorescent response inside cells and the decrease in BChE activity in tumor cells was also confirmed. The results were also affirmed by western blot analysis of BChE levels in HEK 293, HEK 293 pretreated with tacrine, HeLa, and Hep G2 cells (Figure 3c). Another BChE specific inhibitor, iso-OMPA, was also investigated (Figure S20), which further confirmed that the enhancement of intracellular fluorescence was caused by hydrolysis of P1 catalyzed by BChE.
    Notably, the nucleus region was scarcely stained fluorescently, as shown in Figure 3a, implying that this probe has potential in localizing cytoplasm specifically. Therefore, in-depth analysis on the distribution was performed by the co-localization protocol with the incorporation of a nuclear reagent, 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI). After the co-incubation with P1 and DAPI, fluorescence in HEK 293 cells was taken in two channels for BChE sensing and nuclear staining, respectively, as illustrated in Figure 4. The green emission was mainly distributed within the cytoplasm area, whereas the blue fluorescence for DAPI-staining concentrated inside the nucleus. According to the merged image, these two parts were distinguished clearly, as suggested by the low Pearson’s coefficient of only 0.321 with an overlap parameter of 0.232.

    In Vivo Imaging

    The intriguing sensing performance on account of sensitivity and selectivity further encouraged us to work on the imaging of mice brain slices to expand the usage of this elaborate probe. After the incubation with P1, a bright green emission was observed in Figure 5a, especially for the area around the vascular cross section, suggesting that the envisaged sensing platform was also workable for the tissue slice. The fluorescence intensity could be recorded to evaluate the BChE content, as supported by the control sample in Figure 5b. After the pretreatment with tacrine, the control sample was then cultured with probe P1. In addition to the fading fluorescence of the slice, the comparison between these two experiments was also quantified in Figure 5c, giving rise to a sharp contrast that the slice with pretreatment with inhibitor showed an intense drop in responsive intensity. These findings implied that probe P1 could also be applied for slice tests and the BChE activity could be learned visually.
    The aforementioned sensing results allow further tests to be undertaken for the animal models, as displayed in Figure 6. The BChE level of the experimental sample was at the normal range, which was enough to trigger the sensing procedure. Upon the treatment with probe P1, the emissive response could be readily captured in 0.5 h within the brain region, as illustrated in Figure 6a, suggestive of a quite fast detection manner for the endogenous BChE in living organisms. During the following investigation in 2 h, the fluorescence reaches saturation within 1.5 h with a concomitant growth in intensity. As for the control experiment assumed in Figure 6b, the mouse was pretreated with in situ injection of tacrine to inhibit the BChE activity, and the probe was applied afterward. The weak response, as expected, lasted for over 2 h without an obvious loss in intensity. This sensing scheme worked stably during the observation, capable of visualizing the expression level of endogenous BChE with high selectivity, which otherwise showed its potential application for pathological research.
    Interestingly, this sensing pattern showed great potential in reporting the inhibition efficacy, so its application as an indicator for BChE inhibitors was conducted. (46,47) As members of carbamate or organophosphorus pesticides, methomyl, carbofuran, and DDVP (Figure 7a) are well known to possess health hazards to the human body, which were utilized to figure out the feasibility of P1 for their trace analysis. As depicted in Figure 7b, it turned out that this sensing strategy made a tiny difference on the result from the standard method. The inhibition efficiency of BChE by carbofuran was recorded throughout its concentration range of 0–1.0 mM, which showed a prodigious increase leveling off at 0.2 mM (Figure 7c). A linearity appeared within 10–100 μM with a high correlation (Figure 7d), suggesting a workable model for carbofuran detection. The application for carbofuran determination in fruits is revealed in Figure 7e with the HPLC data to help assess the accuracy. The highly closed results confirmed the usage of probe P1 for trace detection of pesticides. Based on the reliable optical analysis for pesticide residues, P1 was also utilized to monitor pesticides in living cells. HEK 293 cells were pretreated with methomyl, carbofuran, or DDVP for 30 min then incubated with 5 μM P1 for another 30 min. As shown in Figure S21, the green fluorescence emission of the cells pretreated with carbofuran and DDVP was relatively weak, which was due to the obvious inhibition of carbofuran/DDVP on BChE activity. These results confirmed that probe P1 could be used for the analysis of pesticide residues in solutions and living cells.

    Conclusions

    In summary, the fancy receptor P1 for BChE determination was developed by incorporation of a specific enzymatic reaction triggered by BChE and a spontaneous reaction. The in situ sensing procedure could produce green-emissive fluorophores as indicators to report the BChE activity, which provided background-free detection by this non-emissive probe with high sensitivity, giving rise to an LOD as low as 0.075 μg/mL. Its preferable selectivity toward BChE over other biomolecules, even AChE, was verified in vitro and in vivo by spectral evaluation and inhibition exploration. To our gratification, this proposed detection scheme was workable in a variety of conditions including buffer solutions and imaging at cellular, slice, and organism levels. We reported the BChE activity and addressed the inhibition efficiency of the inhibitor in which we further affirmed its outstanding selectivity and expected it to contribute to the early diagnosis of related mental diseases (such as AD). Moreover, its application for trace levels of carbamate pesticide from fruits was also achievable with high accuracy, contributing to its additional usage in the issue of food safety.
